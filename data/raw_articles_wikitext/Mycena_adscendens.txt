'''''Mycena adscendens''''', commonly known as the '''frosty bonnet''', is a species of [[fungus]] in the family [[Mycenaceae]]. The fungus produces small white [[basidiocarp|fruit bodies]] ([[mushroom]]s) with [[pileus (mycology)|caps]] up to {{convert|7.5|mm|in|1|abbr=on}} in diameter that appear to be dusted with sugar-like granules. Caps are supported by thin, hollow [[stipe (mycology)|stems]] up to {{convert|20|mm|in|abbr=on|1}} long, which are set on a disc-like base. Its distribution includes Europe, Turkey and the Pacific coast of the United States. The fruit bodies grow on fallen twigs and other woody debris on the forest floor, including fallen [[hazel nut]]s. The [[variety (botany)|variety]] ''carpophila'' is known from Japan. There are several small white ''[[Mycena]]'' species that are similar in appearance to ''M.&nbsp;adscendens'', some of which can be reliably distinguished only by examining microscopic characteristics.

==Taxonomy==
The species, originally named ''Agaricus adscendens'' by [[Wilhelm Gottfried Lasch]] in 1829, was first collected in the [[Province of Brandenburg]], in what was then the [[Kingdom of Prussia]] (now Germany).<ref name="Lasch 1829"/> It was Dutch mycologist [[Rudolph Arnold Maas Geesteranus|Maas Geesteranus]] who assigned the species its current name in a 1981 publication.<ref name="Maas Geesteranus 1981"/> According to Maas Geesteranus,<ref name="Maas Geesteranus 1981"/><ref name="Maas Geesteranus 1982"/> [[Miles Berkeley]]'s  1836 ''Agaricus tenerrimus''<ref name="Berkeley 1836"/> is the same species as ''Mycena adscendens'', as well as all later [[synonym (biology)|synonyms]] based on this [[basionym]]: ''Mycena tenerrima'', published by [[Lucien Quélet]] in 1872;<ref name="Quélet 1872"/> ''Prunulus tenerrimus'' by [[William Alphonso Murrill]] in 1916;<ref name="Murrill 1916"/> and [[Karel Cejp]]'s 1930 ''Pseudomycena tenerrima''.<ref name="Cejp 1930"/> Although [[Index Fungorum]] agrees with Maas Geesteranus's synonymy,<ref name="urlFungorum synonymy: Mycena adscendens"/> other authorities treat the species as independent.<ref name="urlMycoBank: Mycena tenerrima"/><ref name="Bougher 2009"/> An additional synonym is ''Agaricus'' (''Mycena'') ''farinellus'', described by [[Johann Feltgen]] from Luxembourg in 1906.<ref name="Maas Geesteranus 1981"/>

The [[variety (botany)|variety]] ''M.&nbsp;adscendens'' var. ''carpophila'', published by Dennis Desjardin in 1995,<ref name="Desjardin 1995"/> was originally proposed as ''M.&nbsp;tenerrima'' var. ''carpophila'' by [[Jakob Emanuel Lange]] in 1914.<ref name="Lange 1914"/>

''Mycena adscendens'' is the [[type species]] of [[section (biology)|section]] ''Sacchariferae'' of the genus ''Mycena'', which contains white species with [[wikt:floccose|floccose]] caps (covered with tufts of soft woolly hairs). Other members of this section include ''[[Mycena floccifera|M.&nbsp;floccifera]]'', ''[[Mycena discopus|M.&nbsp;discopus]]'', and ''[[Mycena nucicola|M.&nbsp;nucicola]]''.
The mushroom is [[common name|commonly]] known as the "frosty bonnet".<ref name="BMS common names"/> The [[botanical name|specific epithet]] ''adscendens'', derived from the [[Latin]], means "ascending" or "curving up from a prostrate base".<ref name="Gledhill 2008"/> ''Tenerrima'' derives from the Latin ''tener'', meaning "tender" or "delicate".<ref name="Arora 1986"/>

==Description==

The [[pileus (mycology)|cap]] is white and small, with a diameter typically ranging from {{convert|2.5|to|7.5|mm|in|1|abbr=on}}. Initially convex to [[Glossary of leaf morphology#cucullate|cucullate]] (hood-shaped), it flattens during maturity, developing visible surface grooves that correspond to the gills underneath the cap; the surface may also be covered with glistening particles, remnants of the [[partial veil]].<ref name="Jordan 2004"/> The cap is pallid gray with a whitish margin when young, but soon becomes white overall. The [[trama (mycology)|flesh]] is membranous, fragile, and thin (less than 0.5&nbsp;mm). The [[lamella (mycology)|gills]] are free from attachment or narrowly attached (adnexed) to the [[stipe (mycology)|stem]].<ref name="Smith p.45-47"/> They are up to 0.5&nbsp;mm broad, distantly-spaced (usually numbering between 7 and 12), and sometimes adhering to each other to form a slight collar (a ''pseudocollarium'') around the stem. They are translucent-white throughout their development, with a fringed, white edge.<ref name="Maas Geesteranus 1983"/> The hollow stem is {{convert|0.5|to|2|cm|in|abbr=on|1}} long, and usually curved and threadlike. The bottom of the stem is enlarged into a slight bulb, which is initially nearly spherical.<ref name="Smith p.45-47">Smith (1947), [http://quod.lib.umich.edu/f/fung1tc/AGK0806.0001.001/63?rgn=full+text;view=image pp. 45–7.]</ref> At the very base of the stem is a small, white, and hairy disk-like base that attaches to the [[substrate (biology)|substrate]].<ref name="urlAronsen"/> The [[edible mushroom|edibility]] of the mushroom is unknown, but like many small Mycenas, they are insubstantial and  not likely to be considered for the table.<ref name="urlCalifornia Fungi: Mycena adscendens"/>

The [[variety (botany)|variety]] ''carpophila'' is characterized by its tiny white cap up to 1&nbsp;mm in diameter, and narrowly conical caulocystidia ([[cystidia]] found on the stem).<ref name="Tanaka 2003"/>

===Microscopic characteristics===
''Mycena adscendens'' produces a white [[spore print]]. The [[spore]]s are broadly [[wikt:ellipsoid|ellipsoid]], [[amyloid (mycology)|amyloid]], and have dimensions of 8–10 by 5–6.5&nbsp;[[micrometre|μm]].<ref name="Smith p.45-47"/> [[Basidia]] (spore-bearing cells) are two-spored, club-shaped, and measure 14–17 by 7–9&nbsp;μm.<ref name="Maas Geesteranus 1983"/> Pleurocystidia (cystidia on the gill faces) may be present or absent. If present, they are similar to the cheilocystidia (cystidia on the gill edges). The cheilocystidia are abundant, measuring 28–44 by 8–12&nbsp;μm. They are variable in shape, often fusoid-ventricose (fuse-shaped with a swollen center) or with 2–3 needle-like projections arising from the apex; the projections are sometimes forked. The swollen parts of the cheilocystidia are covered with short rodlike protuberances or warts. The flesh of the gills is [[wine (color)|vinaceous]]-brown when [[staining|stained]] in [[iodine]]. The flesh of the cap is made up of greatly enlarged cells, with the surface covered with club-shaped to almost globular cells measuring 25–40 by about 20&nbsp;μm. Their walls are finely verrucose (covered with small warts), and all but the verrucose cells are [[wikt:vinaceous|vinaceous]]-brown in iodine.<ref name="Smith p.45-47"/> [[Clamp connection]]s are abundant in the hyphae.<ref name="urlAronsen"/>

===Similar species===

Other Mycenas that resemble ''M.&nbsp;adscendens'' include ''[[Mycena alphitophora|M.&nbsp;alphitophora]]'' and ''[[Mycena stylobates|M.&nbsp;stylobates]]''. The former is distinguished from ''M.&nbsp;adscendens'' by a stem base that is not swollen or disc-like, the latter by its larger and sturdier fruit body and lack of granules on the cap.<ref name="urlCalifornia Fungi: Mycena adscendens"/> A poorly known Japanese species, ''[[Mycena cryptomeriicola|M.&nbsp;cryptomeriicola]]'', is similar to ''M.&nbsp;adscendens'', but has non-amyloid spores and lacks clamps.<ref name="Maas Geesteranus 1991"/> ''[[Mycena nucicola|M.&nbsp;nucicola]]'' is most reliably distinguished from ''M.&nbsp;adscendens'' by microscopic characteristics: ''M.&nbsp;nucicola'' has four-spored basidia, clamp connections are rare in the hyphae of the gill tissue, and the spores are less broad (typically 4.2–5&nbsp;μm).<ref name="Desjardin 1995"/> The Finnish species ''[[Mycena occulta|M.&nbsp;occulta]]'' grows on the decaying needles of [[Norway spruce]] and [[Scots pine]]. It differs from ''M.&nbsp;adscendens'' in that its gills do not form a pseudocollarium, it lacks clamps in the hyphae and cells of the hymenium, and the terminal cells in its cap cuticle are densely covered with protuberances.<ref name="Maas Geesteranus 1991b"/>

==Habitat and distribution==
Fruit bodies of ''Mycena adscendens'' are found scattered to grouped together in twos or threes on fallen twigs, bark, and woody debris of [[hardwood]]s during the spring and autumn;<ref name="urlAronsen"/> it fruits less frequently on the wood of [[conifer]]s.<ref name="Maas Geesteranus 1983"/> Fruitings are most common after periods of wet weather.<ref name="urlCalifornia Fungi: Mycena adscendens"/> They are also found growing on hazel nuts that have fallen to the ground; two other Mycenas known to grow on this [[substrate (biology)|substrate]] include ''M.&nbsp;discopus'' and ''M.&nbsp;nucicola''.<ref name="urlAronsen"/> In the United States, it is known from [[Washington (U.S. state)|Washington]] to [[California]].<ref name="Smith p.45-47"/> It is also found in Europe,<ref name="urlAronsen"/> and has been collected in [[Amasya Province]], Turkey.<ref name="Aktas 2009"/> The variety ''carpophila'', originally described from Denmark, was reported from Japan in 2003.<ref name="Tanaka 2003"/>
