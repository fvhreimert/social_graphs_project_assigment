'''''Gymnopilus junonius''''' is a type of [[mushroom]]-forming [[fungus]] in the family [[Hymenogastraceae]]. Commonly known as the '''spectacular rustgill''', this large orange mushroom is typically found growing on tree stumps, logs, or tree bases. It is found in Europe, [[Australasia]] and South America. It is inedible and contains some [[neurotoxin]]s.

==Description==
The [[pileus (mycology)|cap]] ranges from {{Cvt|5-30|cm|round=0.5}} across, bun-shaped at first,<ref name="audu">{{Cite book |last=[[Audubon]] |title=Mushrooms of North America |publisher=[[Knopf]] |year=2023 |isbn=978-0-593-31998-7 |pages=651}}</ref> then is convex to flat;<ref name=":0">{{Cite book|last1=Davis|first1=R. Michael|title=Field Guide to Mushrooms of Western North America|last2=Sommer|first2=Robert|last3=Menge|first3=John A.|publisher=[[University of California Press]]|year=2012|isbn=978-0-520-95360-4|location=Berkeley|pages=245–246|oclc=797915861}}</ref> it is bright yellow-orange in younger specimens and orange-brown or reddish brown in older ones, with a dry scaly surface. The [[trama (mycology)|flesh]] is yellow, the odor mild and taste bitter.<ref name=":0" /> The [[stipe (mycology)|stem]] is {{Cvt|5-25|cm|round=0.5}} long, 1–5 cm thick, and often narrows near the base.<ref name=":0" /> The frail [[annulus (mycology)|ring]] is dusted with rusty orange [[Basidiospore|spores]], and the gill attachment to the stem is [[Adnate gills|adnate]] to [[Decurrent gills|sub-decurrent]].<ref name=":0" /> It stains red with [[KOH test|KOH]] and turns green when cooked. The [[spore print]] is rusty brown.<ref name="audu" />

Unlike psychoactive relatives in the ''[[Psilocybe]]'' genus, ''G.&nbsp;junonius'' lacks psilocybin and does not stain blue, but smaller specimens occasionally exhibit bruising.<ref name="Thorn Malloch Saar Lamoureux pp. 293–315">{{cite journal | last1=Thorn | first1=R. Greg | last2=Malloch | first2=David W. | last3=Saar | first3=Irja | last4=Lamoureux | first4=Yves | last5=Nagasawa | first5=Eiji | last6=Redhead | first6=Scott A. | last7=Margaritescu | first7=Simona | last8=Moncalvo | first8=Jean-Marc | title=New species in the Gymnopilus junonius group (Basidiomycota: Agaricales) | journal=Botany | publisher=Canadian Science Publishing | date=2020-04-24 | volume=98 | issue=6 | issn=1916-2790 | doi=10.1139/cjb-2020-0006 | pages=293–315| doi-access= }}</ref> This mushroom usually grows in clusters from several to several dozen individuals, but sometimes grows solitary. It is inedible due to its bitter taste.<ref name="Arora1986">{{Cite book |last=Arora |first=David |author-link=David Arora |url=https://archive.org/details/arora-david-mushrooms-demystified-a-comprehensive-guide-to-the-fleshy-fungi-ten-speed-press-1986/page/410/mode/2up |title=Mushrooms Demystified: A Comprehensive Guide to the Fleshy Fungi |publisher=[[Ten Speed Press]] |year=1986 |isbn=978-0-89815-170-1 |edition=2nd |location=Berkeley, CA |pages=410–411 |orig-date=1979}}</ref>

===Similar species===
This mushroom is often mistaken for ''[[Gymnopilus ventricosus]]'',<ref name="Arora1986" /> which also contains no [[psilocybin]], and [[Gymnopilus luteus|''G.&nbsp;luteus'']] and [[Gymnopilus subspectabilis|''G.&nbsp;subspectabilis'']], which do.<ref name=":1">{{Cite web |date=2020 |title=New species in the ''Gymnopilus junonius'' group (Basidiomycota: Agaricales) |url=https://tspace.library.utoronto.ca/bitstream/1807/100286/1/cjb-2020-0006.pdf |access-date=2022-03-28}}</ref>{{Page needed|date=June 2025}} It also resembles ''[[Armillaria mellea]]'' and ''[[Omphalotus olivascens]]''.<ref name=":0" />

==Distribution and habitat==
''Gymnopilus junonius'' is found in Europe, [[Australasia]] and South America.<ref>{{Cite web |url=https://nature.mdc.mo.gov/discover-nature/field-guide/big-laughing-gym-big-laughing-jim |title=Big Laughing Jim/Scientific Name: Gymnopilus junonius (formerly G. spectabilis) |work=Missouri Department of Conservation (nature.mdc.mo.gov) |access-date=2020-09-27 |archive-date=2021-01-04 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210104064059/https://nature.mdc.mo.gov/discover-nature/field-guide/big-laughing-gym-big-laughing-jim |url-status=live }}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |url=https://www.knau.org/post/earth-notes-laughing-jim-mushroom |title=Earth Notes: The Laughing Jim Mushroom |work=www.knau.org |date=31 January 2018 |access-date=2020-09-27 |archive-date=2021-01-04 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210104064102/https://www.knau.org/post/earth-notes-laughing-jim-mushroom |url-status=live }}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |url=https://www.first-nature.com/fungi/gymnopilus-junonius.php |title=Gymnopilus junonius (Fr.) P. D. Orton - Spectacular Rustgill |work=www.first-nature.com |access-date=2020-09-27 |archive-date=2021-01-04 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210104064100/https://www.first-nature.com/fungi/gymnopilus-junonius.php |url-status=live }}</ref>  It grows in dense clusters on stumps and logs of hardwoods and conifers.  This mushroom is most common in moist, lowland wooded areas near rivers.

This species does not occur in North America, with similar mushrooms often being misidentified.<ref name=":1" />

==Biochemistry==
This mushroom contains bis-noryangonin and [[hispidin]], which are structurally related to [[pyrone|alpha-pyrones]] found in [[kava]].<ref name="Hatfield1969"/> It also contains [[neurotoxin]]s known as oligoisoprenoids,<ref name="Shirahama1993"/> such as gymnopilin.
