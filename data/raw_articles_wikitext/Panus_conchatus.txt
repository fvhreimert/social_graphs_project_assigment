'''''Panus conchatus''''', commonly known as the '''lilac oysterling''', '''smooth panus''', or '''conch panus''',<ref name="Arora1986">{{Cite book |last=Arora |first=David |author-link=David Arora |url=https://archive.org/details/arora-david-mushrooms-demystified-a-comprehensive-guide-to-the-fleshy-fungi-ten-speed-press-1986/page/138/mode/2up |title=Mushrooms Demystified: A Comprehensive Guide to the Fleshy Fungi |date=1986 |publisher=[[Ten Speed Press]] |isbn=978-0-89815-170-1 |edition=2nd |location=Berkeley, CA |pages=138-139 |orig-date=1979}}</ref> is a species of fungus. Despite being a [[lamella (mycology)|gilled]] species, [[phylogenetic]] analysis has shown it is closely related to the pored species found in the family [[Polyporaceae]].

The [[basidiocarp|fruit bodies]] are characterized by a smooth, [[lilac (color)|lilac]]- or [[tan (color)|tan]]-colored [[pileus (mycology)|cap]], and [[decurrent]] gills. The fungus is [[saprophytic]] and fruits on the decomposing wood of a wide variety of [[deciduous]] and [[conifer]]ous trees throughout the Northern Hemisphere. 

==Taxonomy==
The species was originally [[species description|described]] under the name ''Agaricus conchatus'' by French mycologist [[Jean Baptiste François Pierre Bulliard]] in volume 7 of his 1787 ''Herbier de la France''.<ref name="Bulliard 1787"/> [[Elias Magnus Fries]] transferred it to the genus ''[[Panus]]'' in 1838.<ref name="Fries 1838"/>

''Panus conchatus'' mushrooms have an extremely variable [[morphology (biology)|morphology]] that changes with the age of the fruitbodies. Young specimens are pliable and fleshy, colored lilac to purple, and have a monomitic [[hypha]]l system (containing only generative hyphae). Old fruitbodies lose the coloring and develop a tough texture. They have a dimitic hyphal system, containing both generative and skeletal hyphae. Because of this variability in fruitbody morphology, the fungus has been described several times under different names by different mycologists.<ref name="Pegler 1983"/> The following are heterotypic [[synonym (biology)|synonyms]] of ''Panus conchatus'' (based on a different [[type (biology)|type]]):<ref name="urlMycoBank: Panus conchatus"/>
*''Agaricus flabelliformis'' [[Jacob Christian Schäffer|Schaeff.]] (1774)<ref name="Schaffer 1774"/>
*''Agaricus carneotomentosus'' [[August Batsch|Batsch]] (1783)<ref name="Batsch 1783"/>
*''Panus monticola'' Berk (1851)<ref name="Berkeley 1851"/>
*''Panus vaporarius'' [[Bagl.]] (1865)<ref name="Baglietto 1865"/>
*''Lentinus percomis'' [[Miles Joseph Berkeley|Berk.]] & [[Christopher Edmund Broome|Broome]] (1875)<ref name="Berkeley 1874"/>
*''Lentinus divisus'' [[Stephan Schulzer von Müggenburg|Schulzer]] (1879)<ref name="Schulzer 1878"/>
*''Lentinus bresadolae'' Schulzer (1885)<ref name="Schulzer 1885"/>
*''Lentinus carneotomentosus''  [[Joseph Schröter|J.Schröt.]] (1889)<ref name="Schröter 1885"/>
*''Lentinus obconicus'' [[Charles Horton Peck|Peck]] (1906)<ref name="Peck 1906"/>

=== Phylogeny ===
The general growth form, or [[habit (biology)|habit]], of ''P.&nbsp;conchatus'' is ''[[Pleurotoid fungi|pleurotoid]]'', referring to its growth on wood, the presence of gills, and roughly semicircular-shaped caps and off-center stem attachment to the cap. A study of this and various other pleurotoid species using [[phylogenetic]] analysis determined that despite the presence of gills, ''P.&nbsp;conchatus'' is closely related to mushrooms with pores, hence their placement in the [[Polyporaceae]] family.<ref name="Thorn 2000" />

=== Etymology ===
The [[specific name (botany)|specific epithet]] ''conchatus'' is derived from the [[Latin language|Latin]] word meaning "shell-like".<ref name="urlPLEUROTOID species in the Pacific Northwest">{{cite web |author=Brown DE |year=2003 |title=PLEUROTOID species in the Pacific Northwest |url=http://www.svims.ca/council/Pleuro.htm |access-date=2009-04-05 |publisher=Pacific Northwest Key Council}}</ref> It is [[common name|commonly]] known as the lilac oysterling.<ref name="BMS" />

== Description ==
The [[pileus (mycology)|cap]] is {{convert|3 to 17|cm|frac=2}} wide<ref name="audu">{{Cite book |last=[[Audubon]] |title=Mushrooms of North America |publisher=[[Knopf]] |year=2023 |isbn=978-0-593-31998-7 |pages=236}}</ref><ref name="Arora1986" /> and initially convex before flattening and sometimes becoming centrally depressed in age.<ref name="Arora1986" /> The cap is [[tan (color)|tan]], [[lilac (color)|lilac]] or reddish-brown, and smooth ([[glabrousness (botany)|glabrous]]); in age the surface may crack into small flattened scales.<ref name="urlCalifornia Fungi: Panus conchatus" /> The cap margin is [[wikt:inrolled|inrolled]] and often has a wavy or lobed outline. The [[trama (mycology)|flesh]] is tough and whitish. The [[lamella (mycology)|gills]] are attached decurrently (running down the length of the stem) and are narrow and often forked. The gills initially have a violet tinge, but later become light yellow<ref name="audu" /> or reddish-violet. The [[stipe (mycology)|stem]] is {{convert|1.5 to 5|cm|abbr=on|frac=4}} long<ref name="audu" /> and {{convert|0.5|to|3|cm|abbr=on|frac=8}} thick,<ref name="Arora1986" /> roughly the same color as the cap, but covered with violet hairs; it is attached to the cap laterally, or off-center.<ref name="Orr 1979" /> The [[spore print]] is white.<ref name="audu" />

Viewed microscopically, [[spore]]s are elliptical, smooth, and non-[[amyloid]], with dimensions of 5–7 by 2.5–3.5&nbsp;[[μm]]. The [[pleurocystidia]] are either enlarged in the middle (''ventricose'') or enlarged and spherical at the tip (''capitate''); these cells have dimensions of 35–45 by 8–11&nbsp;μm.<ref name="urlMushroomExpert" />

=== Similar species ===
It can resemble ''[[Panus lecomtei]]'' and ''[[Pseudoarmillariella ectypoides]]''.<ref name="audu" />

==Habitat and distribution==
''Panus conchatus'' is a [[saprobic]] species—deriving nutrition from rotting or decaying organic matter. Fruit bodies can be found on [[hardwood]] stumps, logs and sticks, usually crowded together in clusters.<ref name="Orr 1979"/> Typical [[host (biology)|hosts]] include wood of deciduous trees—especially [[beech]], [[Populus|poplar]], [[birch]], and [[oak]], and less frequently on [[Fraxinus|ash]] and [[elm]]. Coniferous hosts include [[fir]], [[spruce]], [[pine]], and [[Taxus|yew]].<ref name="Pegler 1983"/>

Found throughout the Northern Hemisphere,<ref name="Johnson 1994"/> this species has been collected in North America<ref name="Orr 1979"/> and Europe.<ref name="urlRogers"/> It can be found in eastern North America from April to August and on the West Coast from December to June.<ref name="audu" />

==Potential uses==
Although believed to be non-toxic<ref name="urlCalifornia Fungi: Panus conchatus"/> and sometimes eaten when young, ''P.&nbsp;conchatus'' is not recommended for consumption due to its tough and leathery texture.<ref name="urlMushroomExpert"/><ref name="Miller 2006">{{cite book|last1=Miller Jr.|first1=Orson K.|title=North American Mushrooms: A Field Guide to Edible and Inedible Fungi|last2=Miller|first2=Hope H.|publisher=[[FalconGuide]]|year=2006|isbn=978-0-7627-3109-1|location=Guilford, CN|pages=161|author-link=Orson K. Miller Jr.}}</ref>

''Panus conchatus'' contains a [[laccase]], a [[polyphenol oxidase]] enzyme. These enzymes have potential in industrial applications for [[Bleaching of wood pulp|pulp bleaching]], [[wastewater treatment]] in mills, and removal of [[phenol]]ic compounds in the food industry. Most laccases have an [[active site]] containing four [[copper]] molecules, and are known as [[multicopper oxidase|blue copper phenol oxidases]]. ''P.&nbsp;conchatus'', however, contains a white laccase that lacks the typical blue copper color.<ref name="Zhou 2014"/> The crudely purified enzyme has been used for pulp bleaching<ref name="Mo 2006"/> and wastewater decoloration in experimental studies.<ref name="Ding 2012"/>
