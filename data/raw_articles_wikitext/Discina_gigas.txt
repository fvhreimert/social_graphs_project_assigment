'''''Discina gigas''''', commonly known as the '''snow mushroom''', '''snowbank false morel''', '''walnut''',<ref name="Arora1986">{{Cite book |last=Arora |first=David |author-link=David Arora |url=https://archive.org/details/arora-david-mushrooms-demystified-a-comprehensive-guide-to-the-fleshy-fungi-ten-speed-press-1986/page/800/mode/2up |title=Mushrooms Demystified: A Comprehensive Guide to the Fleshy Fungi |publisher=[[Ten Speed Press]] |year=1986 |isbn=978-0-89815-170-1 |edition=2nd |location=Berkeley, California |pages=800-801 |orig-date=1979}}</ref> '''giants false morel''',<ref>{{cite web |url= https://www.britmycolsoc.org.uk/library/english-names |title= Gyromitra gigas · giants false morel |access-date= 2021-05-21 |work= The British Mycological Society |archive-date= 2022-02-05 |archive-url= https://web.archive.org/web/20220205063207/https://www.britmycolsoc.org.uk/library/english-names }}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url= https://species.nbnatlas.org/species/NBNSYS0000019570 |title= Gyromitra gigas · giants false morel |access-date=2021-05-21 |work= NBN Atlas }}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url= https://inaturalist.nz/taxa/131537-Gyromitra-gigas |title= Giant's False Morel (Gyromitra gigas) |access-date=2021-05-21 |work= Ninaturalist.nz }}</ref> '''snow morel''', '''snow false morel''', '''calf brain''', or '''bull nose''', is a species of [[fungus]] and a member of the [[Ascomycota]] found in Europe.

It is referred to as one of the [[false morel]]s, due to its similar appearance and occurrence in the spring and early summer in similar habitats to true morels (''[[Morchella]]'' ssp.).

==Taxonomy==
The species was first described scientifically by [[Julius Vincenz von Krombholz]] as ''Helvella gigas''.<ref name=Krombholz1834/>

==Description==
The tannish, wrinkled cap grows up to {{Convert|10|cm|frac=4}} broad and 6&nbsp;cm tall.<ref name="Arora1986" /> The whitish stalk is typically almost as thick as the cap, up to 10&nbsp;cm long and broad.<ref name="Arora1986" />

=== Similar species ===
Many of the common names of ''Discina gigas'' refer to both its resemblance to true morels, ''[[Morchella]]'' ssp., and to the related genus ''[[Gyromitra]]''.<ref name="volk" />

A very similar and directly related species, ''D.&nbsp;montana'', occurs in North America; it apparently has larger spores than ''D.&nbsp;gigas''.<ref name="Miller 2006">{{cite book|last1=Miller Jr.|first1=Orson K.|title=North American Mushrooms: A Field Guide to Edible and Inedible Fungi|last2=Miller|first2=Hope H.|publisher=[[FalconGuides]]|year=2006|isbn=978-0-7627-3109-1|location=Guilford, CN|page=509|author-link=Orson K. Miller Jr.}}</ref> While both species are reportedly [[Edible mushroom|edible]] in small amounts if thoroughly cooked, eating is not recommended due to their similarity to more toxic species of mushrooms.<ref name="volk" /><ref>{{cite book |last1=Trudell |first1=Steve |last2=Ammirati |first2=Joe |chapter=Gyromitra montana |chapter-url={{GBurl|WevHvt6Tr8kC|p=281}} |title=Mushrooms of the Pacific Northwest |date=2009 |publisher=Timber Press |isbn=978-1-60469-141-2 |page=281 }}</ref>

==Toxicity==
It contains small quantities of [[hydrazines]];<ref>{{cite book |last=Phillips |first=Roger |title=Mushrooms and Other Fungi of North America |publisher=Firefly Books |year=2010 |isbn=978-1-55407-651-2 |location=Buffalo, NY |page=363}}</ref> its content in [[gyromitrin]] was scientifically assessed by Viernstein et al. (1980) and resulted of about 1mg per kg of fresh mushroom (roughly 1,500-fold less compared to that of ''[[Gyromitra esculenta]]''). Though no casualties have been ascribed to its consumption, parboiling is still highly recommended. Some guides have listed it as being edible if properly prepared.<ref name="Arora1986" /> However, consumption is not recommended due to variability and similarity to other more toxic species of ''Gyromitra''.<ref name="volk" />

This fungus has been banned for sale in France since 1991 due to potential toxicity, the effects of which would cause a fairly rare fatal neurodegenerative disease, [[amyotrophic lateral sclerosis]] (ALS).<ref name="sciencesetavenir-maladie-de-charcot">{{cite news |last1=Kaldy |first1=Pierre |title=Un champignon lié à des cas de maladie de Charcot: la fin d'une énigme médicale vieille de plus de dix ans |trans-title=Fungus Linked to Lou Gehrig's Disease: End to a Ten-Year-Old Medical Enigma |url=https://www.sciencesetavenir.fr/sante/os-et-muscles/un-champignon-lie-a-des-cas-de-maladie-de-charcot_157084 |work=Sciences et Avenir |date=4 September 2021 |language=fr }}</ref><ref>{{cite journal |last1=Lagrange |first1=E. |last2=Vernoux |first2=J.P. |last3=Reis |first3=J. |last4=Palmer |first4=V. |last5=Camu |first5=W. |last6=Spencer |first6=P.S. |title=An amyotrophic lateral sclerosis hot spot in the French Alps associated with genotoxic fungi |journal=Journal of the Neurological Sciences |date=August 2021 |volume=427 |article-number=117558 |doi=10.1016/j.jns.2021.117558 |pmid=34216974 }}</ref>
