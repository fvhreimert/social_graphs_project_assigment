'''''Omphalotus olivascens''''', commonly known as the '''western jack-o'-lantern mushroom''',<ref name=Arora1986/> is an orange to brown-colored gilled [[poisonous mushroom]] endemic to the North American west coast. It has several lookalikes including ''[[Cantharellus cibarius]]''.

==Taxonomy==
The fungus was [[species description|described]] as new to science in 1976 by American mycologists [[Howard E. Bigelow]], [[Orson K. Miller Jr.]], and [[Harry D. Thiers]].<ref name="Bigelow 1976"/> A subspecies with blue [[trama (mycology)|flesh]], ''O.&nbsp;olivascens'' var. ''indigo'', was described growing on [[Quercus agrifolia|live oak]] in [[Baja California]], Mexico.<ref name="Moreno 1993"/>

==Description==
The cap is {{Cvt|4-18|cm}} wide. The stalks are {{Cvt|4-15|cm}} long and {{Cvt|1-4|cm}} wide. The spores are white to pale yellow.<ref name=":0">{{Cite book |last1=Davis |first1=R. Michael |title=Field Guide to Mushrooms of Western North America |last2=Sommer |first2=Robert |last3=Menge |first3=John A. |publisher=[[University of California Press]] |year=2012 |isbn=978-0-520-95360-4 |location=Berkeley |pages=143–144 |oclc=797915861}}</ref>

=== Similar species ===
To an untrained eye, ''O.&nbsp;olivascens'' appears similar to ''[[Cantharellus cibarius]]'',<ref name=Arora1986>{{Cite book |last=Arora |first=David |author-link=David Arora |title=Mushrooms Demystified: A Comprehensive Guide to the Fleshy Fungi |url=https://archive.org/details/arora-david-mushrooms-demystified-a-comprehensive-guide-to-the-fleshy-fungi-ten-speed-press-1986/page/147/mode/2up |date=1986 |publisher=[[Ten Speed Press]] |isbn=978-0-89815-170-1 |location=Berkeley, CA |orig-date=1979 |edition=2nd |pages=147}}</ref> but the jack-o'-lantern mushroom has true, blade-like<ref>{{cite book |last1=Meuninck |first1=Jim |title=Foraging Mushrooms Oregon: Finding, Identifying, and Preparing Edible Wild Mushrooms |date=2017 |publisher=[[Falcon Guides]] |isbn=978-1-4930-2669-2 |page=4}}</ref> [[Lamella (mycology)|gills]] (rather than ridges) and it can have olive coloration that chanterelles lack; also, ''Omphalotus'' species are [[saprotrophic]], grow directly on wood, and are [[bioluminescent]].<ref name="myko" />

Several ''Omphalotus'' species with similar bioluminescent properties occur worldwide, all of which are presumed [[Mushroom poisoning|poisonous]]. The best known are the North American jack o'lantern mushroom (''[[Omphalotus olearius|O.&nbsp;olearius]]'') and the ''tsukiyotake'' (''[[Omphalotus japonicus|O.&nbsp;japonicus]]'' (Kawam.) Kirchm. & O.K. Mill. (formerly known as ''Lampteromyces japonicus'' (Kawam.) Sing.), found in Japan and eastern Asia. [[molecular phylogenetics|Molecular]] analysis shows the jack-o'-lantern to be most closely related to the ghost fungus [[Omphalotus nidiformis|''O.&nbsp;nidiformis'']],<ref name="Kirchmair 2004" /> the colours and shades of which most closely resemble ''O.&nbsp;olivascens''.<ref name="Miller 1994" /> Additionally, ''[[Omphalotus illudens|O.&nbsp;illudens]]'' is similar.<ref name="audu">{{Cite book |last=[[Audubon]] |title=Mushrooms of North America |publisher=[[Knopf]] |year=2023 |isbn=978-0-593-31998-7 |pages=476–477}}</ref>

''[[Gymnopilus junonius]]'' is another similar-looking species.<ref name=":0" />

==Distribution and habitat==
It is endemic to the  North American west coast,<ref name=Arora1986/> namely the [[California Floristic Province]].{{citation needed|date=May 2025}}

==Ecology==
A saprobe or [[parasite]], ''O.&nbsp;olivascens'' is usually found on [[oak]] or ''[[Eucalyptus]]'',<ref name="audu" /> rarely on other [[host (biology)|hosts]].

''Omphalotus'' species cause a [[Wood-decay fungus#White rot|white rot]] by breaking down [[lignin]] in their tree hosts.<ref name="Kirchmair 2004"/>

==Toxicity==
The jack o'lantern mushroom is poisonous; while not lethal, consuming this mushroom leads to very severe [[cramp]]s, [[vomiting]], and [[diarrhea]].<ref name=myko/>

The toxic ingredient of many species of ''Omphalotus'' is a [[sesquiterpene]] compound known as [[illudin]]&nbsp;S.<ref name="Benjamin 1995"/> This, along with illudin&nbsp;M, have been identified in ''O.&nbsp;nidiformis''. The two illudins are common to the genus ''Omphalotus'' and not found in any other [[basidiomycete]] mushroom.<ref name="Kirchmair 1999"/>
