'''''Phaeolepiota''''' is a [[genus]] of fungi in the family [[Squamanitaceae]]. The genus is [[monotypic]], containing the single species '''''Phaeolepiota aurea'''''. Commonly known as '''Alaskan gold''',<ref name=":1">Arora, David (February 1, 1991). ''All that the Rain Promises and More: A Hip Pocket Guide to Western Mushrooms''. p. 139. {{ISBN|9780898153880}}.</ref> '''Alaska gold''',<ref name=":2">Siegel, Noah; Schwarz, Christian (September 1, 2024). ''Mushrooms of Cascadia: A Comprehensive Guide to Fungi of the Pacific Northwest''. Humboldt County, CA: Backcountry Press. p. 62. {{ISBN|9781941624197}}.</ref> '''golden bootleg''' or '''golden cap''', ''P.&nbsp;aurea'' is an [[agaric]] (gilled mushroom) found throughout North America and Eurasia – often in groups and next to [[Urtica|nettles]]. 

==Taxonomy==
Recent [[molecular phylogenetics]] research shows that ''Phaeolepiota'' is closely related to and may need to be merged into the genus ''[[Cystoderma]]''.<ref name="Liu2021" /><ref name="Saar2022" />

==Description==
The mushroom is large and golden, and its stem has a skirt-like [[Annulus (mycology)|ring]].<ref name=":0">{{cite book |last1=Trudell |first1=Steve |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=WevHvt6Tr8kC |title=Mushrooms of the Pacific Northwest |last2=Ammirati |first2=Joe |publisher=Timber Press |year=2009 |isbn=978-0-88192-935-5 |series=Timber Press Field Guides |location=Portland, OR |pages=185}}</ref> The cap is up to {{Convert|20|cm|frac=2}} wide and the stem up to 20&nbsp;cm long. It has a mild smell.<ref name="audu">{{Cite book |last=[[Audubon]] |title=Mushrooms of North America |publisher=[[Knopf]] |year=2023 |isbn=978-0-593-31998-7 |pages=584}}</ref> The spores are brown,<ref name=":0" /> producing a tannish [[spore print]].<ref name="audu" />

=== Similar species ===
It can resemble ''[[Cystoderma fallax]]'', ''[[Cystoderma granosum|C.&nbsp;granosum]]'', ''[[Gymnopilus spectabilis]]'', and ''[[Agrocybe praecox]]''.<ref name="audu" />

==Distribution and habitat==
It is found throughout North America and Eurasia – often in groups<ref name=":0" /> and next to [[Urtica|nettles]].<ref>{{cite book |author=Phillips R |title=Mushrooms and Other Fungi of North America |publisher=Firefly Books |year=2005 |isbn=978-1-55407-115-9 |page=320}}</ref> 

==Edibility==
The fruit bodies have been considered [[Edible mushroom|edible]] and are eaten in Russia and China.<ref name="Boa2004" /> It is not, however, recommended for consumption as it is known to cause [[gastrointestinal upset]].<ref name="Miller 2006">{{cite book|last1=Miller Jr.|first1=Orson K.|title=North American Mushrooms: A Field Guide to Edible and Inedible Fungi|last2=Miller|first2=Hope H.|publisher=[[FalconGuide]]|year=2006|isbn=978-0-7627-3109-1|location=Guilford, CN|pages=260|author-link=Orson K. Miller Jr.}}</ref><ref name=":2" /><ref name=":1" /> Studies have shown that this fungus contains unacceptable amounts of both [[cadmium]] and [[cyanide]] compounds. Cooking reduces the concentration of the cyanide compounds present, which may be the reason why it has been consumed in some countries.<ref>[http://www.mushroomthejournal.com/bestof/Paureatext.html Phaeolepiota aurea] ''mushroomthejournal.com'' {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080828005706/http://www.mushroomthejournal.com/bestof/Paureatext.html |date=2008-08-28 }}</ref>
