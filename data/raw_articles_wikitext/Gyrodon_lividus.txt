'''''Gyrodon lividus''''', commonly known as the '''alder bolete''', is a pored [[mushroom]] bearing close affinity to the genus ''[[Paxillus]]''. Although found predominantly in Europe, where it grows in a [[mycorrhiza]]l association with [[alder]], it has also recorded from China, Japan and [[California]]. [[Basidiocarp|Fruit bodies]] are distinguished from other [[bolete]]s by [[decurrent]] bright yellow pores that turn blue-grey on bruising. ''G.&nbsp;lividus'' mushrooms are [[edible mushroom|edible]].

==Taxonomy==
The alder bolete was initially [[species description|described]] by French mycologist [[Jean Baptiste François Pierre Bulliard|Pierre Bulliard]] in 1791 as ''Boletus lividus'',<ref name="Bulliard 1791"/> before being given its current binomial name in 1888 by [[Pier Andrea Saccardo]] when he transferred it to ''[[Gyrodon]]''.<ref name="Saccardo 1888"/> When Saccardo [[circumscription (taxonomy)|circumscribed]] ''Gyroporus'', he included ''Boletus sistotremoides'' (published by [[Elias Fries]] in 1815) as the [[type species]]. [[Rolf Singer]] later determined that Fries's taxon was the same species as ''Gyroporus lividus''.<ref name="Singer 1951"/> Before this, in 1886 [[Lucien Quélet]] erected the genus ''Uliporus'' with ''Boletus lividus'' as the type.<ref name="Quelet 1886"/> As a result of Singer's discovery, the genus ''Uliporus'' was rendered obsolete, and ''Boletus sistrotremoides'' became [[synonym (biology)|synonymous]] with ''Gyropus lividus''.<ref name="Singer 1951"/> The generic term ''Gyrodon'' is derived from the [[Ancient Greek]] ''gyros'' "whorl" and ''odon'' "tooth",<ref name="Nilper 1977"/><ref name="Liddell 1980"/> while the [[botanical name|specific epithet]] ''lividus'' is [[Latin]] for "lead-coloured".<ref name="Simpson 1979"/> The fungus is [[common name|commonly]] known as the alder bolete.<ref name="BMS"/>

[[Molecular phylogenetics|Molecular]] research confirms the relations of the genus ''[[Gyrodon]]'' and the gilled genus ''[[Paxillus]]'' as [[sister group|sister taxa]], and one of the earliest diverging [[lineage (evolution)|lineages]] of the suborder [[Boletineae]].<ref name="Kretzer 1999"/>

Two [[subspecies]] of ''G.&nbsp;lividus'' have been described: subsp. ''alneti'', published by [[Sven Johan Lindgren]] in 1874, and subsp. ''labyrinthicus'', published by Saccardo in 1888. Neither are considered to have independent [[taxonomy (biology)|taxonomic]] significance.<ref name="urlFungorum synonymy: Gyrodon lividus"/>

==Description==
''Gyrodon lividus'' has a pale brown, buff or ochre [[Pileus (mycology)|cap]] {{convert|4|–|10|cm|in|round=0.5|abbr=on}} in diameter which is convex and later flat in shape, and can be sticky when wet. Like other boletes, it has pores instead of [[lamella (mycology)|gills]] that make up the [[hymenophore]] on the underside of the cap. These large pores are [[decurrent]] in their attachment to the stipe. Bright yellow, they turn blue-grey when cut or bruised. The thin [[trama (mycology)|flesh]] is pale yellow. The ringless [[Stipe (mycology)|stipe]] is initially the same colour as the cap but later darkens to a red-brown; it is {{convert|3|–|7|cm|in|round=0.5|abbr=on}} high by {{convert|1|–|2|cm|in|round=0.5|abbr=on}} wide. The [[spore print]] is olive-brown and the oval spores are 4.5–6 x 3–4&nbsp;[[micrometre|μm]]. The mushroom has a non-distinctive smell and taste.<ref name="Phillips 2006"/>

In the United States, ''Gyrodon lividus'' could be mistaken for ''[[Boletinellus merulioides]]'', which is generally a larger mushroom overall with larger spores and grows under ash (''[[Fraxinus]]''), or ''[[Boletinellus proximus|B.&nbsp;proximus]]'', a dark brown or purple-brown capped species that does not change colour when bruised and is found only in [[Florida]].<ref name="Bessette 2000"/>

==Distribution and habitat==
''Gyrodon lividus'' is found across Europe, including [[Ķemeri National Park]] in [[Latvia]],<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.kemeri.gov.lv/_Administracija/KNP_MP.pdf |title=The Management Plan of Kemeri National Park |access-date=2008-08-02 |author=Kemeri NP Administration |year=2002 |publisher=Ministry of Environment of Denmark, DANCEE and Government of Latvia. |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20081123034723/http://www.kemeri.gov.lv/_Administracija/KNP_MP.pdf |archive-date=November 23, 2008 }}</ref> In Asia, it has been recorded in China<ref name="Zhuang 2001"/> and Japan.<ref name="Hongo 1989"/> It is also found in several locales in Turkey, including [[Trabzon]], [[Maçka]] and [[Sevinç, Manavgat|Sevinç]].<ref name="SESLİ 2015"/> In North America, it has been reported to occur in [[California]], under alder (''[[Alnus rhombifolia]]'').<ref name="Hayward 1984"/>

As its common name suggests, ''Gyrodon lividus'' is found under [[alder]] (''Alnus rhombifolia''), with which it forms a [[mycorrhiza]]l relationship. Fruit bodies may be found alone or in clumps and appear in autumn. The [[ectomycorrhiza]] of the fungus is characterized by its yellow colour in young specimens, [[rhizomorph]]s that are highly differentiated, and the presence of [[sclerotia]].<ref name="Agerer 1987"/><!--cites previous sentence-->

==Edibility==
It is reported as [[edible mushroom|edible]] by some authors,<ref name="Phillips 2006"/> and inedible by others.<ref name="Nilper 1977"/>

==Bioactive compounds==
Fruit bodies of ''Gyrodon lividus'' contain the [[cyclopentanedione]] compounds chamonixin and involution.<ref name="Besl 1980"/>
