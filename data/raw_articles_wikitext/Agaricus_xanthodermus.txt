'''''Agaricus xanthodermus''''', commonly known as the '''yellow-staining agaricus''',<ref name=Arora1986>{{Cite book |last=Arora |first=David |author-link=David Arora |title=Mushrooms Demystified: A Comprehensive Guide to the Fleshy Fungi |url=https://archive.org/details/arora-david-mushrooms-demystified-a-comprehensive-guide-to-the-fleshy-fungi-ten-speed-press-1986/page/329/mode/2up |date=1986 |publisher=[[Ten Speed Press]] |isbn=978-0-89815-170-1 |location=Berkeley, CA |orig-date=1979 |edition=2nd |pages=329–331}}</ref> '''yellow-staining mushroom''' or '''yellow-stainer''',<ref name=Phillips/> is a species of fungus in the genus ''[[Agaricus]]''. It displays a strong yellow colouration at the base of the stem when cut. It has a phenolic smell. It is [[mushroom poisoning|poisonous]] for most people, causing gastric upset, but can be eaten by some without apparent negative effect.

==Taxonomy==
This species was first officially defined under the name ''Agaricus xanthodermus'' in 1876 by Léon Gaston Genevier, in a letter published in the bulletin of the [[Société botanique de France|French Botanical Society]].<ref name=SBF/><ref name=IndexFungorum/>  Genevier described the ''[[Agaricus]]'' mushrooms commonly eaten (perhaps sometimes inadvisedly) in the region of [[Nantes]], and attempted to clarify the distinctions between them. He proposed a detailed reclassification into 5 species, including this new one. Apparently up until that time, these yellow-staining mushrooms were considered to be just varieties of other edible species: ''A.&nbsp;edulis'', ''[[Agaricus arvensis|A.&nbsp;arvensis]]'', and ''[[Agaricus silvicola|A.&nbsp;silvicola]]''.<ref name=Genevier/>

The epithet ''xanthodermus'' is derived from the [[Ancient Greek]] words for "yellow" and "skin", which were then given a Latin adjective ending. This is the official name, but the form ''Agaricus xanthoderma'' is also often seen.<ref name="Bon"/><ref name="Courtecuisse"/><ref name=Moser/>  In the latter name, ''-derma'' "skin" is a neuter noun which does not have to agree in gender with ''Agaricus'', and so this form is legal according to the rules of botanical nomenclature.<ref name="Nomenclature"/>

==Description==

The [[pileus (mycology)|cap]] ranges from {{convert|5–15|cm|round=0.5|abbr=on}} in diameter.<ref name=":0">{{Cite book|last1=Davis|first1=R. Michael|title=Field Guide to Mushrooms of Western North America|last2=Sommer|first2=Robert|last3=Menge|first3=John A.|publisher=[[University of California Press]]|year=2012|isbn=978-0-520-95360-4|location=Berkeley|pages=230|oclc=797915861}}</ref> It is initially convex, with some young specimens having a squarish shape, though flattening with age. It is whitish, with light brown tints towards the centre. The cap is dry and smooth, but can be scaly when old. The [[lamella (mycology)|gills]] of this mushroom progress from pale-pink to a chocolate color. Its white [[stipe (mycology)|stipe]] measures {{Cvt|5-12|cm}} tall and 1–3 wide,<ref name=":0" /> and is bulbous with a skirt-like ring. Microscopically, the [[cheilocystidia]] are club-shaped. The [[Basidiospore|spores]] are brown, elliptical, and smooth,<ref name=":0" /> measuring 6–7 × 3–4&nbsp;[[μm]].

The species has an unpleasant characteristic smell, which is phenolic, reminiscent of ink or [[carbolic soap]]. The smell is especially strong at the base of the stem. On cooking, the smell becomes very noticeable, and this may deter people from eating it inadvertently.<ref name=Jordan/>

===Similar species===
The main identifying feature is an immediate bright yellow colouration on cutting through the base of the stem, or scraping the flesh; later, the affected area fades to a dull brown. Numerous edible ''Agaricus'' species, such as ''[[Agaricus augustus|A.&nbsp;augustus]]'',<ref name=Arora1986/> ''[[Agaricus arvensis|A.&nbsp;arvensis]]'' and ''[[Agaricus silvicola|A.&nbsp;silvicola]]'', turn yellow to a greater or lesser extent, but they do not display such an intense reaction.

''Agaricus xanthodermus'' belongs to a group of related species  (the "Xanthodermati") which likewise discolour bright yellow and have a [[phenol]]ic smell. They include ''A.&nbsp;praeclaresquamosus'' (formerly ''[[Agaricus placomyces|A.&nbsp;placomyces]]'') which has dark grey scales, ''[[Agaricus moelleri|A.&nbsp;moelleri]]'', and ''[[Agaricus pilatianus|A.&nbsp;pilatianus]]'', which does not have a bulbous stem.<ref name="Courtecuisse"/>

''[[Agaricus californicus]]'' is also similar in appearance.<ref name=":0" />


==Distribution and habitat==

This mushroom is very common and widely distributed in North America, Europe, West Asia including eastern [[Anatolia]]<ref name=Dem04/> and Iran,<ref name=Asef2010/> North Africa,<ref name="Courtecuisse"/> and [[southern Africa]].<ref name=Westhuizen/>  It has been introduced into [[Australia]]. It occurs in woods, lawns, gardens and hedgerows in autumn. This is a [[saprobic]] species.


==Toxicity==
Although it appears that some people can eat this mushroom without ill effects,<ref name=Phillips/><ref name=Jordan/><ref name=Genevier/> it contains toxins which can cause serious [[gastrointestinal upset]].<ref name="Miller 2006">{{cite book|last1=Miller Jr.|first1=Orson K.|title=North American Mushrooms: A Field Guide to Edible and Inedible Fungi|last2=Miller|first2=Hope H.|publisher=[[FalconGuide]]|year=2006|isbn=978-0-7627-3109-1|location=Guilford, CN|pages=276|author-link=Orson K. Miller Jr.}}</ref> It is indigestible and gives rise to symptoms of sweating, flushing, and severe stomach cramps.<ref name=Phillips/><ref name=Bon/><ref name=Jordan/> It is considered poisonous and specifically prohibited for sale in [[Italy]].<ref>{{Cite web |title=Wayback Machine |url=https://www.regione.piemonte.it/web/sites/default/files/media/documenti/2021-09/guida_commestibilita_funghi_isbn_979-12-200-9297-5.pdf |archive-url=http://web.archive.org/web/20250522162221/https://www.regione.piemonte.it/web/sites/default/files/media/documenti/2021-09/guida_commestibilita_funghi_isbn_979-12-200-9297-5.pdf |archive-date=2025-05-22 |access-date=2025-10-19 |website=www.regione.piemonte.it}}</ref>

Of those who gather ''Agaricus''-style mushrooms, about 50% of the cases of poisoning are from this species.{{citation needed|date=March 2025}}
