'''''Omphalotus japonicus''''', commonly known as the '''tsukiyotake''' {{nihongo||月夜茸|}}, is an orange to brown-colored gilled [[mushroom]] native to Japan and Eastern Asia. It is a member of the [[wikt:cosmopolitan|cosmopolitan]] genus ''[[Omphalotus]]'', the members of which have [[bioluminescence|bioluminescent]] fruit bodies which glow in darkness. A 2004 [[molecular phylogenetics|molecular]] study shows it to be most closely related to a [[clade]] composed of ''[[Omphalotus nidiformis]]'' of [[Australia]], ''[[Omphalotus olivascens]]'' of Western [[North America]] and ''[[Omphalotus olearius]]'' of [[Europe]].<ref name="Kirchmair 2004"/>

''Omphalotus japonicus'' is poisonous. Its consumption results in acute nausea and vomiting for several hours. It is often confused with edible fungi and mistakenly consumed in Japan.

==Taxonomy==
Inoko first described this fungus as ''Pleurotus noctilucens'' in 1889, however the name proved invalid as the binomial had already been used for another species.<ref name="Neda 2004"/> Given the name ''Pleurotus japonicus'' by [[Seiichi Kawamura]] in 1915, it was given the name ''Lampteromyces japonicus'' by [[Rolf Singer]] in 1947,<ref name="urlMycoBank: Omphalotus japonicus"/> until the genus ''Lampteromyces'' was sunk into ''[[Omphalotus]]'' in 2004.<ref name="Kirchmair 2004"/>  Hitoshi Neda has proposed this fungus is the same as one described by [[Miles Joseph Berkeley]] as ''Agaricus guepiniformis'' in 1878, as the [[type specimen]] fits the description of ''O. japonicus'' and hence, based on the [[Correct name (botany)|principle of priority]], the name should be ''Omphalotus guepiniformis'' (Berk.) Neda.<ref name="Neda 2004"/> A proposal was submitted in 2006 to [[conserved name|conserve]] the epithet ''japonicus'' against ''guepiniformis'' and another [[synonym (biology)|synonym]], ''Pleurotus harmandii''.<ref name="Redhead 2006"/> The proposal was accepted by the [[Nomenclature Committee for Fungi]] in 2008.<ref name="Norvell 2008"/>

The little-known ''Lampteromyces luminescens'', described in 1979 in China by M. Zang, is similar genetically and may be a synonym, however the taxon is insufficiently known to confirm this.<ref name="Yang  2013"/>

The species is mentioned in ''[[Konjaku Monogatarishū]]'', an anthology of Japanese folk tales dating from the 12th century.<ref name="Redhead 2006"/> The Japanese name ''tsukiyotake'' translates as "moon-night mushroom".<ref name="Kawamura 1915"/>

==Description==
The fleshy [[basidiocarp|fruit bodies]] have an eccentric [[stipe (mycology)|stem]] rendering the cap kidney- or half-moon-shaped and only round when young. The [[pileus (mycology)|cap]] is light brown when young and darkens with age, with yellow or pinkish tinges. The [[trama (mycology)|flesh]] is white and up to 2&nbsp;cm (0.8&nbsp;in) thick in the cap. The thick white [[lamella (mycology)|gills]] are [[decurrent]] and can yellow with age. The stem is thick and fleshy and can be up to 2&nbsp;cm (0.8&nbsp;in) thick and 5&nbsp;cm (2&nbsp;in) long. The gills glow with a whitish light. Kawamura reported that the fungi can be seen from 30&nbsp;m (100&nbsp;ft) away at night, and that 100 square centimetres of glowing mushrooms allowed him to read Roman characters of 8&nbsp;mm size by their light.<ref name="Kawamura 1915"/><!-- cites para -->

This fungus is mistaken for the commonly eaten oyster mushroom (''[[Pleurotus ostreatus]]''), Shiitake (''[[Lentinula edodes]]''), and Mukitake (''[[Sarcomixa serotina]]''), but is poisonous. ''O.&nbsp;japonicus'' was responsible for 31.6% of mushroom poisoning cases in Japan between 1996 and 2005—more than any other mushroom.<ref name="Tsurida 2012"/> Symptoms of ''O.&nbsp;japonicus'' poisoning are nausea, vomiting and diarrhea.<ref name="Kawamura 1915"/> The most common treatment is [[intravenous therapy|fluid therapy]].<ref name="Hayashida 2011"/>

==Habitat and distribution==
The fungus grows on dead [[beech]] trees, and is found in mountainous regions of Japan, the fruit bodies appearing in September and October.<ref name="Kawamura 1915"/> One of the most common mushrooms growing on decaying beech wood in that country, it is classified as "[[Vulnerable species|Vulnerable]]" because beech forests are being depleted.<ref name="Fukasawa 2010"/> It is also found in Korea,<ref name="Ka 2010"/> China, and far eastern Russia.<ref name="Redhead 2006"/>

==Bioactive compounds==
Fruit bodies contain the [[sesquiterpene]] compounds [[illudin]] S and illudin M.<ref name="Tanaka 1990"/><ref name="Tanaka 1992"/><ref name="Tanaka 1996a"/><ref name="Tanaka 1996b"/>
