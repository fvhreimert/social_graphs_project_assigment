'''''Britzelmayria multipedata''''' is a species of [[mushroom]] producing [[fungus]] in the family [[Psathyrellaceae]].<ref>{{Cite web |title=Species Fungorum - Britzelmayria multipedata (Peck) D. Wächt. & A. Melzer, Mycol. Progr. 19(11): 1213 (2020) |url=http://www.speciesfungorum.org/Names/GSDSpecies.asp?RecordID=831797 |access-date=2022-07-16 |website=www.speciesfungorum.org}}</ref> It is commonly known as the '''clustered brittlestem.'''

== Taxonomy ==
It was first [[Species description|described]] in 1905 by the American mycologist [[Charles Horton Peck]] who classified it as ''Psathyra multipedata.''<ref>{{Cite web |title=Species Fungorum - Psathyra multipedata Peck, Bull. Torrey bot. Club 32(2): 80 (1905) |url=http://www.speciesfungorum.org/Names/NamesRecord.asp?RecordID=182150 |access-date=2022-07-16 |website=www.speciesfungorum.org}}</ref> It was reclassified as ''Psathyrella multipedata'' in 1941 by the American mycologist[[Alexander H. Smith]]<ref>{{Cite web |title=Species Fungorum - Psathyrella multipedata (Peck) A.H. Sm., Contr. Univ. Mich. Herb. 5: 33 (1941) |url=http://www.speciesfungorum.org/Names/NamesRecord.asp?RecordID=290048 |access-date=2022-07-16 |website=www.speciesfungorum.org}}</ref> and remained known as such until recently. In 2020 the German mycologists [[Dieter Wächter]] & [[Andreas Melzer (Mycologist)|Andreas Melzer]] reclassified many species in the [[Psathyrellaceae]] family based on [[Phylogenetics|phylogenetic]] analysis and placed this species in the newly created genus ''[[Britzelmayria]]''.<ref name=":0">{{Cite journal |last1=Wächter |first1=Dieter |last2=Melzer |first2=Andreas |date=2020-11-01 |title=Proposal for a subdivision of the family Psathyrellaceae based on a taxon-rich phylogenetic analysis with iterative multigene guide tree |url=https://doi.org/10.1007/s11557-020-01606-3 |journal=Mycological Progress |language=en |volume=19 |issue=11 |pages=1151–1265 |doi=10.1007/s11557-020-01606-3 |bibcode=2020MycPr..19.1151W |issn=1861-8952|url-access=subscription }}</ref>

Many mushroom field guides and websites still refer to this species as ''Psathyrella multipedata.''

== Description ==
''Britzelmayria multipedata'' is a small brittlestem mushroom with white flesh and a brown cap which is known for growing in dense clusters.

'''Cap:''' 1-3cm. Starts conical before flattening into a convex cap which may become [[campanulate]] or bell shaped with age. The smooth, brown cap becomes paler when dry. '''Gills:''' Adnate or adnexed. Crowded. Light grey or brown with white fringes maturing to dark brown. '''Stem:''' 7-12cm in height with a thickness of 3-6mm tapering slightly towards the cap. It often grows in a wavy fashion with the base fused together with other members of the cluster. '''Spore print:''' Dark purplish brown. '''Spores:''' Ellipsoid and smooth with a germ pore. 6.5-10 x 3.5-4 μm. '''Taste:''' Indistinct and mild. '''Smell:''' Faint and mushroomy.<ref name=":1" /><ref name=":2" />



== Habitat and distribution ==
''Britzelmayria multipedata'' is found on soil amongst grass and in open grassy spaces amongst woodland. It is [[Saprotrophic nutrition|saprotrophic]] and grows on buried fallen trees through the late Summer to Autumn. This species is widespread and found occasionally.<ref name=":1">{{Cite book |last=Buczacki |first=Stefan |title=Collins fungi guide |date=2012 |publisher=Collins |isbn=978-0-00-724290-0 |location=London |oclc=793683235}}</ref><ref name=":2">{{Cite web |title=Psathyrella multipedata, Clustered Brittlestem mushroom |url=https://www.first-nature.com/fungi/psathyrella-multipedata.php |access-date=2022-07-16 |website=www.first-nature.com}}</ref>

Observations of this species appear most common in the UK, West Europe and the East Coast of the United States.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Clustered Brittlestem (Britzelmayria multipedata) |url=https://www.inaturalist.org/taxa/1179764-Britzelmayria-multipedata |access-date=2022-07-16 |website=iNaturalist |language=en}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web |title=Mushroom Observer |url=https://mushroomobserver.org/observer/map_observations?q=1nHbp |access-date=2022-07-16 |website=mushroomobserver.org}}</ref>
