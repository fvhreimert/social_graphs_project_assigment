'''''Pseudocolus fusiformis''''' is a stinkhorn mushroom in the [[Phallaceae]], a family well known for a remarkable range of fruit body types.<ref name=Cannon2007/> It is [[common name|commonly]] known as the '''stinky squid''',<ref name="urlKuo: Pseudocolus fusiformis"/><ref>{{Cite book |last=Sisson |first=Liv |url=https://www.worldcat.org/title/on1372569849 |title=Fungi of Aotearoa: a curious forager's field guide |last2=Vigus |first2=Paula |date=2023 |publisher=Penguin Books |isbn=978-1-76104-787-9 |location=Auckland, New Zealand |pages=262 |oclc=on1372569849}}</ref> because of its fetid odor, and its three or four upright "arms" which are connected at the top. The malodorous smell comes from the dark greenish slimy [[gleba]] covering the inside faces of the arms, and attracts insects that help to disperse the [[spore]]s.

It is the most widely distributed member of the genus ''[[Pseudocolus]]'' and has been found in the United States, Australia, and Asia.

==Taxonomy==

The first appearance of this species in the literature was in 1890, under the name ''Colus fusiformis'', when [[Eduard Fischer (mycologist)|Eduard Fischer]] wrote a description based on a painting he found in the [[Paris Museum of Natural History]].<ref name=Fischer1890/> In his 1944 [[monograph]] on the [[Gasteromycetes]] of Australia and New Zealand, [[Gordon Herriot Cunningham]] considered this naming to be a ''[[nomen nudum]]''—not published with an adequate description.<ref name=Cunningham1944/> However, it was [[valid name (botany)|valid]] under the rules of the [[ICBN|International Code of Botanical Nomenclature]]. In 1899 Penzig described the species ''Colus javanicus'' based on a single specimen found on Java,<ref name=Penzig1899/> and a year later Fischer amended the name of his original ''Colus fusiformis'' to ''Colus javanicus'', as he was not satisfied with the quality of his original description.<ref name=Fischer1900/> Despite his doubts on the validity of his description, his original naming is both legitimate and has [[Principle of Priority|priority]] over ''C.&nbsp;javanicus''.<ref name=Blanton1976/>

In 1907, [[Curtis Gates Lloyd]] described the new genus ''[[Pseudocolus]]'', and reduced several species to synonyms of ''Pseudocolus fusiformis''.<ref name=Lloyd1907/> The first North American description of this species (as ''Colus schellenbergiae'') was in 1916 by [[David Ross Sumstine]];<ref name=Sumstine1916/> Johnson later (1929) transferred this to ''Pseudocollus schellenbergiae''.<ref name=Johnson1929/> Although Cunningham (1931) revised the genus ''[[Anthurus]]'' to include members of ''Pseudocolus'',<ref name=Cunningham1931/> Dring in 1973 considered the genera to be distinct.<ref name=Ainsworth1973/> Up until the appearance of an extensive study published in 1980, 13 different [[binomial name|binomials]] had been used in the literature to name the species.<ref name=Blanton1980/> [[Index Fungorum]] lists the following synonyms of ''P.&nbsp;fusiformis'':<ref name="urlFungorum synonymy: Pseudocolus fusiformis"/>

*''Colus fusiformis'' E.Fisch. (1891)
*''Colus elegans'' Welw. (1842)
*''Anthurus rothae'' (Berk. ex E.Fisch.) E.Fisch. (1893)
*''Colus rothae'' Berk. ex E.Fisch. (1893)
*''Pseudocolus rothae'' (E.Fisch.) Yasuda (1916)
*''Colus javanicus'' Penz. (1899)
*''Pseudocolus javanicus'' (Penz.) Lloyd (1907)
*''Anthurus javanicus'' (Penz.) G.Cunn. (1931)
*''Pseudocolus rothae'' Lloyd (1907)
*''Colus rothae'' (Lloyd) Sacc. & Traverso (1910)
*''Colus schellenbergiae'' Sumst. (1916)
*''Pseudocolus schellenbergiae'' (Sumst.) M.M.Johnson (1929)

==Description==

Immature [[fruiting bodies]] resemble egg- or pear-shaped [[puffballs]],  grayish-brown to pale gray in color, with dimensions of {{convert|0.5|to|2.5|cm|abbr=on|frac=8}} diameter; the top surface is broken into small regions by cracks or crevices (''[[wikt:areolate|areolate]]'').<ref name=Blanton1976/> As the fungus matures, the fruiting body cracks open and forms a stalk with tapering arms, a [[volva (mycology)|volva]], and a spore mass known as a [[gleba]]. The mature fruiting body is typically {{convert|3|to|6|cm|abbr=on|frac=4}} in height, with arms that are 2–5 times the length of the [[stipe (mycology)|stipe]]. The stipe itself does not extend past the volva, and is hollow, thin-walled, chambered, wrinkled, and flares towards the upper end. The color of the stipe is white or grayish-white; it is {{convert|1|to|3.5|cm|abbr=on|frac=4}} in height, and {{convert|0.5|to|2.5|cm|abbr=on|frac=4}} thick at the widest diameter.<ref name=Blanton1976/> The three or four arms extending from the stipe average {{convert|3.6|cm|abbr=on|frac=4}} in length (ranging from {{convert|1|to|13|cm|abbr=on|frac=2}},<ref name=Blanton1980/> and wrinkled on the side bearing the gleba. The arms, which are joined at the top, are shaped like a [[lance]] (''[[wikt:lanceolate|lanceolate]]''), pointed towards the apex; they are orange-colored. The internal structure of the arms is made of chambers; one large chamber towards the outside, and typically three smaller chambers on the inside of the arm. The gleba is commonly found on the upper two-thirds of the inside surface of the arms, and is dark green and slimy. The fetid odor of the gleba, described by one author as comparable to "fresh pig manure",<ref name=Hemmes2002/> attracts insects that help to disperse the spores.

The [[spore]]s are elliptical or [[wikt:ovoid|ovoid]], smooth, translucent ([[hyaline]]), with dimensions of 4.5–5.5 by 2–2.5&nbsp;[[μm]].<ref name=Bessette1995/> The [[basidia]], the spore-bearing cells, are attached to 6–8 [[Sessility (botany)|sessile]] spores.<ref name=Blanton1976/>

===Similar species===

The stinkhorn species ''[[Clathrus columnatus]]'' somewhat resembles ''P.&nbsp;fusiformis'' in that it has 3 or 4 arms that extend upwards and join at the top. However, unlike ''C.&nbsp;columnatus'', the arms of ''P.&nbsp;fusiformis'' share a common stem, and the immature egg-form is gray or grayish brown, rather than white.<ref name="urlKuo: Clathrus columnatus"/>

''[[Clathrus archeri]]'' has more tentacle-like arms.<ref name=":02">{{Cite book |last=[[Audubon]] |title=Mushrooms of North America |publisher=[[Knopf]] |year=2023 |isbn=978-0-593-31998-7 |pages=117}}</ref>

==Habitat and distribution==

This species grows scattered or in groups on disturbed soil in [[conifer]]ous or [[mixed forest]]s. It is also found growing on [[wood chips]] used as [[mulch]] in gardens or for landscaping;<ref name=Bessette1995/> Blanton has also reported it growing in a garden "solitary and very large".<ref name=Blanton1980/>

''Pseudocolus fusiformis'' has been collected from a variety of locations worldwide, including Europe,<ref name=Cahiers_Mycologiques_Nantais/> [[Australia]], [[Japan]], [[Java]], the [[Philippines]], [[Reunion Island]], the United States,<ref name=Blanton1976/> and [[Turkey]].<ref name=Akata2011/>

It is thought to have been introduced from southeast Asia to North America,<ref name="Kibby1994" /> where it can be found from May to September.<ref name=":02" /> In the [[continental United States]], it was first collected in [[Pittsburgh]] in 1915;<ref name="urlKuo: Pseudocolus fusiformis" /> since then it has been found in [[Alabama]], [[Connecticut]], [[Georgia (U.S. state)|Georgia]], [[Louisiana]], [[Florida]], [[Massachusetts]], [[Mississippi]], [[New Jersey]], [[New York (state)|New York]], [[North Carolina]], [[Rhode Island]], [[Tennessee]], and [[Virginia]]; it has also been found in [[Hawaii]].<ref name="Burk1976" />

==Inedibility==

Although not considered poisonous, ''P.&nbsp;fusiformis'' is not recommended for consumption.<ref name=McKnight1987/> Some related species such as ''[[Phallus impudicus]]'' or ''[[Mutinus caninus]]'' are considered to be edible (or even delicacies) in the immature egg stage;<ref name=Lucas1982/> however, the foul smell of stinkhorns at maturity would likely deter most individuals from eating them.
