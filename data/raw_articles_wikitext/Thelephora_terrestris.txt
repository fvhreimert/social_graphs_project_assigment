'''''Thelephora terrestris''''', commonly known as the '''common fiber vase'''<ref name="Audubon1981" /> or '''earthfan fungus'''<ref name="Petrzik2015" /> is an inedible species of [[fungus]] in the [[Basidiomycota]] phylum.<ref name =Radulovic2005 />

==Taxonomy==
This fungus was first described by [[Jakob Friedrich Ehrhart]] in 1787.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Mycobank: ''Thelephora terrestris''|url=https://www.mycobank.org/page/Name%20details%20page/field/Mycobank%20%23/193195|access-date=2021-02-19|website=Mycobank}}</ref><ref name = Burt1914 />

== Description ==
''Thelephora terrestris'' is present year round, though is mostly seen July to December.<ref name="Audubon1981" /> As the [[basidiome]] forms, it starts off lighter in colour then turns to a darker shade of brown as it ages.<ref name="Lopez-Gutierrez2018" /> A stipe may not be present, if there is one, it is usually very short.<ref name="Audubon1981" /> Sometimes the fungus is grown in large colonies.<ref name="Ellis1990" /> The shape is described as a fan and can grow up to {{Convert|6|cm|frac=4}} wide.<ref name="Ellis1990" /> It has been described to have a moldy earth-like smell.<ref name="Audubon1981" />

The [[hyphae]] of mycorrhizal forms walls  that becomes thicker as it ages, while in earlier stages may be spiney.<ref name="Agerer2018" /> When mating, the hyphae forms [[clamp connection]]s<ref name="Lopez-Gutierrez2018" /> The [[spores]] are purple-brown colour,<ref name="Audubon1981" /> ellipsoid or angular shape.<ref name="Ellis1990" />

The edibility of this fungus is unknown, but it is considered too tough to be worthwhile.<ref name=":0">{{Cite book |last1=Davis |first1=R. Michael |title=Field Guide to Mushrooms of Western North America |last2=Sommer |first2=Robert |last3=Menge |first3=John A. |publisher=[[University of California Press]] |year=2012 |isbn=978-0-520-95360-4 |location=Berkeley |pages=310–311 |oclc=797915861}}</ref>

=== Similar species ===
''[[Thelephora palmata]]'' is comparatively stinky and less widely distributed.<ref name=":0" /><ref>{{cite book |last1=Trudell |first1=Steve |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=WevHvt6Tr8kC |title=Mushrooms of the Pacific Northwest |last2=Ammirati |first2=Joe |publisher=Timber Press |year=2009 |isbn=978-0-88192-935-5 |series=Timber Press Field Guides |location=Portland, OR |pages=253 |language=en}}</ref> ''[[Thelephora vialis|T.&nbsp;vialis]]'' is more robust; ''[[Cotylidia diaphana]]'' and ''[[Podoscypha petalodes]]'' are similar; ''[[Hydnellum]]'' can appear similar but has teeth below.<ref name="audu">{{Cite book |last=[[Audubon]] |title=Mushrooms of North America |publisher=[[Knopf]] |year=2023 |isbn=978-0-593-31998-7 |pages=210}}</ref>

== Habitat and ecology ==
Throughout North America<ref name= Audubon1981 /> and Europe<ref name = Burt1914 /> ''Thelephora terrestris'' can be found in soil. It is commonly found in sandy soils under pine trees, on roots<ref name= Audubon1981 /> and twigs.<ref name = Ellis1990 />

This [[ectomycorrhiza]]l fungus forms a [[symbiotic]] relationship known as [[mycorrhizae]], especially with ''[[Pinus]]'' species.<ref name= Lopez-Gutierrez2018 /> It is commonly found in pine forests<ref name = "Moeller2016" /> as well as [[plant nursery]] soils world wide.<ref name= Smith2002 /> This fungus is known to get water and nutrients from far away<ref name = Hilszczafiska2008 /> and being capable of growing in both low [[fertile|fertility]] and high fertility soils.<ref name = Petrzik2015 />
 
It is a dominant mycorrhizal fungus,<ref name= Smith2002 /> re-establishes quickly after disturbances such as forest fire, and is considered stress tolerant.<ref name = Vesela2019 />

Outside of the ''Pinus'' genus, it is also capable of forming mycorrizha with other trees such as [[alder]], [[birch]], [[oak]], [[beech]], and [[Populus|poplar]].<ref name = Petrzik2015 />

''Thelephora terrestris'' virus 1 (TtV1), which is a [[mycovirus]], can infect this fungus.<ref name = Petrzik2015 />

==Physiology==

The full life cycle can be reproduced and studied in a laboratory, both ectomycorrhizal form and mushroom form.<ref name = Birraux1981 />

Due to the [[mycotoxin]]s that the fungi produces, it protects pinus trees from root [[pathogen]] ''[[Phytophthora cinnamomi]]''.<ref name= Smith2002 />
