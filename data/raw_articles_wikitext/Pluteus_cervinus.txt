'''''Pluteus cervinus''''', commonly known as the '''deer shield''',<ref name="BMS" /> '''deer mushroom''', or '''fawn mushroom''',<ref>National Audubon Society Field Guide to North American Mushrooms, 2006</ref> is a species of [[fungus]] in the order [[Agaricales]]. Fruit bodies are [[agaric]]oid (mushroom-shaped). ''Pluteus cervinus'' is [[saprotrophic]] and fruit bodies are found on rotten logs, roots, tree stumps, sawdust, and other wood waste. It is common in Europe and eastern North America.     

==Etymology==
The species epithet, ''cervinus'', means "deer-like"" and refers to the colour of the cap (described as "rehfarbig" in [[Jacob Christian Schäffer]]'s original 1774 description).<ref name="Schaeffer"/>

==Description==
The [[Pileus (mycology)|cap]] typically grows up to {{Convert|12|cm|frac=4}} in diameter.<ref name="tfb">{{Cite book |last=Francis-Baker |first=Tiffany |title=Concise Foraging Guide |date=2021 |publisher=[[Bloomsbury Publishing|Bloomsbury]] |isbn=978-1-4729-8474-6 |series=[[The Wildlife Trusts]] |location=London |pages=149}}</ref> Initially it is bell-shaped and often wrinkled when young. Later it expands to a convex shape.<ref name="Arora1986">{{Cite book |last=Arora |first=David |author-link=David Arora |url=https://archive.org/details/arora-david-mushrooms-demystified-a-comprehensive-guide-to-the-fleshy-fungi-ten-speed-press-1986/page/255/mode/2up |title=Mushrooms Demystified: A Comprehensive Guide to the Fleshy Fungi |date=1986 |publisher=[[Ten Speed Press]] |isbn=978-0-89815-170-1 |edition=2nd |location=Berkeley, CA |pages=255-54 |orig-date=1979}}</ref> The cap can be deer-brown, but varies from light ochre-brown to dark brown, with a variable admixture of grey or black. The centre of the cap may be darker.<ref name=":1">{{cite book |last1=Trudell |first1=Steve |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=WevHvt6Tr8kC&pg=PA144 |title=Mushrooms of the Pacific Northwest |last2=Ammirati |first2=Joe |publisher=Timber Press |year=2009 |isbn=978-0-88192-935-5 |series=Timber Press Field Guides |location=Portland, OR |pages=144}}</ref> The cap surface is smooth and matt to silky-reflective. The cap skin shows dark radial fibres when seen through a lens, indicating that the microscopic cuticle structure is filamentous. The [[Lamella (mycology)|gills]] are initially white, but soon show a distinctive pinkish sheen,<ref name=":1" /> caused by the ripening spores.

The stipe is 5–12&nbsp;cm long and 0.5–2&nbsp;cm in diameter, usually thicker at the base. It is white and covered with brown vertical fibrils. The flesh is soft and white.<ref name=":1" /> The fruit body has a mild to earthy radish smell<ref name=":1" /> and a mild taste at first, which may become slightly bitter.<ref name="Kuo">
{{cite web |last=Kuo |first=Michael |date=December 2004 |title=Pluteus cervinus: The Deer Mushroom |url=http://www.mushroomexpert.com/pluteus_cervinus.html |access-date=August 8, 2013}}</ref>

The spores are elliptical, smooth and measure approximately 7.0–8.0 × 5.0–5.5&nbsp;[[μm]]. The [[hypha]]e lack [[clamp connection]]s. The [[cystidia]] are thick-walled with apical projections. The [[spore print]] is pinkish brown.<ref name="Justo2014" />

=== Similar species ===
Similar species include ''[[Pluteus atromarginatus]]'', which has a dark brown edge to the gills; ''[[Pluteus rangifer|P.&nbsp;rangifer]]'', a subarctic species; ''[[Pluteus elaphinus|P.&nbsp;elaphinus]]'' in eastern North America; ''[[Pluteus hongoi|P.&nbsp;hongoi]]'' which typically has a paler cap and occurs in Eurasia and eastern North America; and ''[[Pluteus exilis|P.&nbsp;exilis]]'' which typically produces fruit bodies in spring and occurs in western North America.<ref name="Justo2014"/> Similarly coloured species of ''[[Entoloma]]'' have gills that are attached to the stipe (not free) and distinctive, angular spores.

==Distribution and habitat==
''Pluteus cervinus'' grows on stumps and wood debris.<ref name="tfb" />

It is common in Europe (April to December)<ref name="tfb" /> and eastern North America, but rare and possibly introduced in western North America.<ref name="Justo2014" />

==Uses==
Although a poor-quality [[edible mushroom]]<ref>{{cite book |last=Phillips |first=Roger |title=Mushrooms and Other Fungi of North America |publisher=Firefly Books |year=2010 |isbn=978-1-55407-651-2 |location=Buffalo, NY |page=159}}</ref><ref name="Kuo" /> with an unremarkable flavour (and not preserving well), young specimens can be added to other cooked dishes.<ref name="tfb" /><ref name="Miller 2006">{{cite book |last1=Miller Jr. |first1=Orson K. |author-link=Orson K. Miller Jr. |title=North American Mushrooms: A Field Guide to Edible and Inedible Fungi |last2=Miller |first2=Hope H. |publisher=[[FalconGuide]] |year=2006 |isbn=978-0-7627-3109-1 |location=Guilford, CN |pages=203}}</ref>
