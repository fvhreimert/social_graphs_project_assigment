'''''Mycena rosea''''', commonly known as the '''rosy bonnet''',<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.fungi4schools.org/Reprints/ENGLISH_NAMES.pdf |title=Recommended English Names for Fungi in the UK |publisher=[[British Mycological Society]] |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110716083053/http://www.fungi4schools.org/Reprints/ENGLISH_NAMES.pdf |archive-date=2011-07-16 }}</ref> is a species of bioluminescent [[mushroom]] in the family [[Mycenaceae]]. First named ''Agaricus roseus'' in 1803 by Danish botanist  [[Heinrich Christian Friedrich Schumacher]], it was given its present name in 1912 by Gramberg.

==Description==
The [[pileus (mycology)|cap]] initially has a convex shape before flattening; its diameter may reach up to {{convert|6|cm|in|1|abbr=on}}.<ref name=Jordan2004>{{cite book |author=Jordan M. |title=The Encyclopedia of Fungi of Britain and Europe |publisher=Frances Lincoln |location=London |year=2004 |page=171 |isbn=0-7112-2378-5|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=bFMfytLn3bEC&q=Mycena+rosea&pg=PA171 |access-date=2009-09-26}}</ref>

===Similar species===
''Mycena sororius'' is a closely related species that has been reliably distinguished from ''M.&nbsp;rosea'' by the [[electrophoresis|electrophoretic migration]] of [[isozyme]]s, as well as having larger spores&mdash;7.5&ndash;8.5 to 10 by 4.8&ndash;5.5&nbsp;[[micrometre|μm]], compared to 6.5&ndash;9 by 4.5&ndash;5&nbsp;μm for ''M.&nbsp;rosea''.<ref>{{cite journal |vauthors=Perreau Bertrand J, BoisselierDubayle MC, Lambourdiere J |year=1996 |title=''Mycena sororia'' sp nov, close to ''M.&nbsp;rosea'' Gramberg (Basidiomycotina)|journal=Mycotaxon |volume=60 |pages=263–73}}</ref>

==Bioactive compounds==
The fruit bodies of ''Mycena rosea'' contain two red [[alkaloid]] [[pigment]]s that are unique to this species. Named mycenarubin A, and mycenarubin B, these chemicals are related to the so-called [[damirone]]s that are found in [[marine sponge]]s.<ref>{{cite journal |vauthors=Peters S, Spiteller P |year=2007|title=Mycenarubins A and B, red pyrroloquinoline alkaloids from the mushroom ''Mycena rosea'' |journal=Journal of Organic Chemistry |volume= 2007|issue=10 |pages=1571–76 |doi=10.1002/ejoc.200600826}}</ref>
