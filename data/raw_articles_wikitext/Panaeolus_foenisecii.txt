'''''Panaeolus foenisecii''''', commonly called the '''haymaker's panaeolus''',<ref name="Arora1986">{{Cite book |last=Arora |first=David |author-link=David Arora |url=https://archive.org/details/arora-david-mushrooms-demystified-a-comprehensive-guide-to-the-fleshy-fungi-ten-speed-press-1986/page/360/mode/2up |title=Mushrooms Demystified: A Comprehensive Guide to the Fleshy Fungi |publisher=[[Ten Speed Press]] |year=1986 |isbn=978-0-89815-170-1 |edition=2nd |location=Berkeley, CA |pages=360 |orig-date=1979}}</ref> '''lawnmower's mushroom''',<ref>{{Cite book |last=Siegel |first=Noah |title=Mushrooms of Cascadia: A Comprehensive Guide to Fungi of the Pacific Northwest |last2=Schwarz |first2=Christian |publisher=Backcountry Press |isbn=9781941624197 |pages=103}}</ref> '''mower's mushroom''', '''haymaker''', or '''brown hay mushroom''', is a very common and widely distributed little brown [[mushroom]] often found on lawns. It is not edible.

== Description ==
The cap is 1 to 4&nbsp;cm across, conic to convex, [[hygrophanous]] with a brownish colour when moist and tannish when dry,<ref name="Arora1986" /> often with a dark band around the margin which fades as the mushroom dries.<ref name=":0">{{Cite book|last1=Davis|first1=R. Michael|title=Field Guide to Mushrooms of Western North America|last2=Sommer|first2=Robert|last3=Menge|first3=John A.|publisher=[[University of California Press]]|year=2012|isbn=978-0-520-95360-4|location=Berkeley|pages=211–212|oclc=797915861}}</ref>

The [[Lamella (mycology)|gills]] are broad, adnate, brown with lighter edges, becoming mottled as the spores mature.<ref name=":0" /> The [[spore print]] is deep brown, sometimes purplish.<ref name="Arora1986" />

The stipe is 3 to 8&nbsp;cm by 1 to 3&nbsp;mm, fragile, hollow, beige to light brown,<ref name=":0" /> fibrous,<ref name=":1">{{cite book|last1=Trudell|first1=Steve|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=WevHvt6Tr8kC|title=Mushrooms of the Pacific Northwest|last2=Ammirati|first2=Joe|publisher=Timber Press|year=2009|isbn=978-0-88192-935-5|series=Timber Press Field Guides|location=Portland, OR|pages=200–201}}</ref> [[wikt:pruinose|pruinose]], and slightly striate.

It has a slightly unpleasant nutty fungal taste. The odor is nutty and slightly unpleasant.

=== Microscopic features ===
Spores measure 12–17 x 7–11&nbsp;[[μm]], subfusoid to lemon shaped, rough, [[dextrinoid]], with an apical germ pore. [[Cheilocystidia]] subfusoid to cylindric or subcapitate, often wavy, up to 50&nbsp;[[micrometre|μm]] long. Pleurocystidia absent, but some authors report inconspicuous "pseudocystidia". The [[pileipellis]] a cellular cuticle with subglobose elements and has pileocystidia.<ref name="Mushroomexpert" />

=== Similar species ===
Similar species include ''[[Agaricus campestris]]'', ''[[Conocybe apala]]'', ''[[Marasmius oreades]]'', ''[[Psathyrella candolleana]]'', and ''[[Psathyrella gracilis]]''.<ref name=":0" />

It is sometimes mistaken for the psychedelic ''[[Panaeolus cinctulus]]'' or [[Panaeolus olivaceus|''P.&nbsp;olivaceus'']], both of which share the same habitat and can be differentiated by their jet black spores. This is probably why ''P.&nbsp;foenisecii'' is occasionally listed as a psychoactive species in older literature.{{Original research inline|date=October 2021}}

== Habitat and distribution ==
It can be found throughout North America.<ref name="audu">{{Cite book |last=[[Audubon]] |title=Mushrooms of North America |publisher=[[Knopf]] |year=2023 |isbn=978-0-593-31998-7 |pages=613}}</ref> In the [[Pacific Northwest]] of the United States, the species may be the most common to appear in lawns.<ref name=":1" /> It is also found on lawns along the East Coast.

==Biochemistry==
In 1963, Tyler and Smith found that this mushroom contains [[serotonin]], {{Nowrap|[[5-HTP]]}} and [[5-hydroxyindoleacetic acid]].<ref name="Tyler1963" /> In many field guides it is listed as [[Psychoactive mushroom|psychoactive]], but the mushroom does not produce any hallucinogenic effects as it only contains trace amounts of [[psilocybin]].<ref name="Arora1986" /><ref>{{cite web |author=John W. Allen |author2=Mark D. Merlin |name-list-style=amp |title=Observations Regarding the Suspected Psychoactive Properties of Panaeolus foenisecii Maire |url=http://www.erowid.org/plants/mushrooms/mushrooms_article4.shtml |accessdate=2012-06-10}}</ref>
