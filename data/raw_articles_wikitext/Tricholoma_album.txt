'''''Tricholoma album''''', commonly known as the '''white knight''', is an all-white [[mushroom]] of the large genus ''[[Tricholoma]]''. It is found in Europe, India, and possibly North America. The [[pileus (mycology)|cap]] and [[lamella (mycology)|gills]] are white. The whitish [[stipe (mycology)|stipe]] has no [[annulus (mycology)|ring]].

==Taxonomy, naming, and classification==
The species was originally described as ''Agaricus albus'' by [[Jacob Christian Schäffer]] in 1774,<ref name=Schaeffer1774/> and reclassified as ''Gyrophila alba'' by mycologist [[Lucien Quélet]] in 1886.<ref name=Quelet1886/> It was given its current [[binomial name]] by German [[Paul Kummer]] in 1871.<ref name=Kummer1871/> The [[British Mycological Society]] has listed "white knight" as its [[common name]].<ref name=BMS/> The generic name derives from the [[Ancient Greek|Greek]] ''trichos''/τριχος 'hair' and ''loma''/λωμα 'hem', 'fringe' or 'border', while the [[specific name (botany)|specific epithet]] is the [[Latin]] adjective ''albus'' "white".<ref name=Nilper1977/>

The fungus is classified in the [[section (biology)|section]] ''Lasciva'' of the genus ''Tricholoma'', characterised by species with a strong odor and acrid or bitter taste;<ref name=Nederlandica1999/> an older classification has it placed in section ''Inamoena''.<ref name=Riva1988/>

[[Marcel Bon]] named the [[variety (botany)|variety]] ''Tricholoma album'' var. ''thalliophilum'' to account for those mushrooms that differed by staining blue-green with [[thallium(I) hydroxide|thallium oxide]] and [[sulfoformol]];<ref name=Bon1970/> in the absence of additional differentiating characters, some later authors have questioned the [[Taxonomy (biology)|taxonomical]] value of this characteristic.<ref name=Christensen1999/>

==Description==

The [[pileus (mycology)|cap]] is {{convert|3|–|7.5|cm|in|abbr=on}} wide and white with a pale yellow tinge, and more yellow or ochre in the centre as the fruit body ages. Convex with a slight boss, the cap is broadly conical in shape with inrolled margins. The white to pale yellow or ochre-tinged [[Stipe (mycology)|stipe]] is {{convert|3|–|8.5|cm|in|abbr=on}} high and 0.8–1.5&nbsp;cm wide and has no ring. There is no ring or [[volva (mycology)|volva]]. The mushroom has a prominent unpleasant sweet smell reminiscent of honey and radishes, and has an acrid and disagreeable taste.<ref name="MycoBank: Tricholoma album"/> The thick [[lamella (mycology)|gills]] are widely spaced with finely serrated edges. The [[spore print]] is white, the oval or oblong [[spore]]s 5–7&nbsp;[[micrometre|μm]] long by 3.5–5&nbsp;[[micrometre|μm]] wide.<ref name=Nederlandica1999/>

==Ecology, habitat and distribution==
''Tricholoma album'' is found across Europe, the fruit bodies appearing between August and December, in association with oak (''[[Quercus]]'') trees,<ref name=Nederlandica1999/> with which they form a [[mycorrhiza]]l relationship.<ref name=Trappe1962/> Experiments have demonstrated that inoculating blue pine (''[[Pinus wallichiana]]'') and deodar (''[[Cedrus deodara]]'') seedlings with the fungus increases plant height and shoot and root biomass.<ref name=Dar2010/> The mushroom can be encountered growing in sizeable [[fairy ring]]s.<ref name=Nilper1977/> The presence of the mushroom in North America has not been confirmed.<ref name=Kuo/> It has been reported from India in 2010.<ref name=Hedawoo2010/>
