'''''Phallus rubicundus''''' is a species of [[fungus]] in the [[stinkhorn]] family. First described in 1811, it has a wide distribution in [[tropical]] regions. It has the typical stinkhorn structure consisting of a spongy [[stipe (mycology)|stalk]] up to {{convert|15|cm|in|abbr=on}} tall arising from a gelatinous "egg" up to {{convert|3|cm|in|abbr=on}} in diameter. Atop the stalk is a pitted, conical [[pileus (mycology)|cap]] that has a foul-smelling, gelatinous, green [[gleba|spore mass]] spread over it.

==Taxonomy==
The species was first [[species description|described]] under the name ''Satyrus rubicundus'' by French botanist [[Louis Augustin Guillaume Bosc]] in 1811,<ref name="Bosc 1811"/> from collections made in [[South Carolina]].<ref name="urlKuo"/> It was later transferred to the genus ''[[Phallus (fungus)|Phallus]]'' in 1823 by [[Elias Fries]].<ref name="Fries 1823"/> [[Synonym (biology)|Synonyms]] include binomials resulting from the transfer to ''Ithyphallus'' by [[Eduard Fischer (mycologist)|Eduard Fischer]] in 1888, and to ''Leiophallus'' by [[Émile-Victor Mussat]] in 1900.<ref name="urlMycoBank: Phallus rubicundus"/>

==Description==

Immature (unopened) specimens of ''Phallus rubicundus'' are spherical to egg-shaped, whitish, and measure {{convert|2|–|3|cm|in|1|abbr=on}} long by {{convert|1|–|3|cm|in|1|abbr=on}} wide. They occur singly or in groups of two to six eggs that are formed from a common [[mycelium]].<ref name="Long 1907"/> They are attached to the [[substrate (biology)|substrate]] by a cordlike [[rhizomorph]]. After expanding, the fruit bodies are up to {{convert|15|cm|in|abbr=on}} tall, and consist of a hollow cylindrical [[stipe (mycology)|stalk]] supporting a conical to bell-shaped [[pileus (mycology)|cap]]. The orange to scarlet stalk tapers towards to top, and has a pitted surface.<ref name="Dissing 1962"/> The wrinkled cap is scarlet red, and measures {{convert|2|–|3|cm|in|1|abbr=on}} high by {{convert|1|–|2|cm|in|1|abbr=on}} wide. It is initially covered with a foetid, slimy grayish-olive [[gleba]].<ref name="Dissing 1962"/> The egg case remains at the base of the stalk as a [[volva (mycology)|volva]].<ref name="Hemmes 2002"/> The [[spore]]s are smooth, elliptical, and measure 3.6–4.2 by 1.6–2.0&nbsp;[[micrometre|μm]].<ref name="Dissing 1962"/>

''Phallus rubicundus'' is often confused with the similar ''[[Mutinus elegans]]'', but the latter species does not have a clearly separated cap, and instead bears its gleba on the apex of its pointed stalk.<ref name="urlKuo"/>

==Uses==
In the Indian state of [[Madhya Pradesh]], where it is known locally as {{transliteration|hi|jhiri pihiri}}, it is used by two primitive forest tribes, the [[Bharia people|Bharia]] and the [[Baiga (tribe)|Baiga]], as a treatment against [[typhoid]], and also by the Baiga to treat [[childbirth|labour]] pain. The fungus is prepared by grinding and mixing with sugar-cake, and one teaspoon is administered three times daily.<ref name="Rai 1993"/> The fungus has been reported to have been used by [[Aboriginal Australians]] as an [[aphrodisiac]].<ref name="Cambie 1997"/>

One study noted that [[mosquito]]es, attracted to the smell of the gleba, perish after consuming it, and so the fungus may be suitable for further investigating as a [[biocontrol]] agent.<ref name="Gardezi 1992"/>

==Ecology and distribution==
The fungus is [[saprobic]], and grows in sandy soils, lawns, gardens, and yards, especially those that are well-manured or use wood [[mulch]]. It is widely distributed in southern and eastern United States (including Hawaii<ref name="Hemmes 2002"/>), having possibly been spread through the use of imported wood mulch in landscaping.<ref name="urlKuo"/> In Australia it grows mainly in the [[tropics]] and [[subtropic]]s, in areas where rotten wood and/or mulch are present.<ref name="Fuhrer 2005"/> In Asia, it has been recorded from China,<ref name="Huang 1985"/> Japan,<ref name="Kasuya 2007"/> Korea,<ref name="Wojewoda 1993"/> India,<ref name="Rai 1993"/> and Thailand.<ref name="Ellingsen 1982"/> African locales include Ghana, Nigeria,<ref name="Dring 1977"/> Congo, Kenya,<ref name="Williams 1991"/> and South Africa.<ref name="Dissing 1962"/> It is also known from South America (Argentina<ref name="De Toledo 1995"/> and Brazil<ref name="Bonini 1984"/>) and the Caribbean.<ref name="Dissing 1962"/> The fungus was featured on a [[Sierra Leone]]an postage stamp in 1993.<ref name="Moss 1998"/>
