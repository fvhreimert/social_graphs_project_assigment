'''''Pestalotiopsis microspora''''' is a species of [[endophyte|endophytic]] [[fungus]] capable of breaking down and [[Anaerobic digestion|digesting]] [[polyurethane]].<ref>

|bibcode=2011ApEnM..77.6076R }}</ref> Originally identified in 1880  in fallen foliage of [[Hedera helix|common ivy]] (''Hedera helix'') in [[Buenos Aires]],<ref>{{cite book |title=Sylloge fungorum omnium hucusque cognitorum |author=Saccardo, Pier Andrea |ol=7025165M |volume=3 |page=789 |language=Latin |url=https://archive.org/stream/syllogefungorumo03sacc#page/788/mode/2up/search/pestalozzia+microspora |publisher=Patavii, sumptibus auctoris |date=1882–1931 }}</ref> it also causes [[leaf spot]] in [[Hypericum patulum|''Hypericum'' 'Hidcote']] (''Hypericum patulum'') shrubs in [[Japan]].<ref>{{cite journal |title=First Report of Pestalotiopsis microspora Causing Leaf Spot of Hidcote (Hypericum patulum) in Japan |first1=M. |last1=Zhang |first2=H.Y. |last2=Wu |first3=T. |last3=Tsukiboshi |first4=I. |last4=Okabe |doi=10.1094/PDIS-94-8-1064B |journal=Plant Disease | date=August 2010 |volume=94 |issue=8 |page=1064|pmid=30743469 |doi-access= |bibcode=2010PlDis..94.1064Z}}</ref>

However, its polyurethane degradation activity was discovered only in the 2010s in two distinct ''P. microspora'' strains isolated from plant stems in the [[Yasuni National Park|Yasuni National Forest]] within the [[Ecuador]]ian [[Amazon rainforest]] by a group of student researchers led by molecular [[biochemistry]] professor [[Scott Strobel]] as part of [[Yale University]]'s annual Rainforest Expedition and Laboratory. It is the first fungus species found to be able to subsist on polyurethane in [[anaerobic organism|anaerobic]] conditions. This makes the fungus a potential candidate for [[bioremediation]] projects involving large quantities of [[plastic]].<ref>{{cite web |last1=Anderson |first1=Stacey |date=December 15, 2014 |title=The Plastic-Eating Fungi That Could Solve Our Garbage Problem |url=http://www.newsweek.com/2014/12/26/plastic-eating-fungi-could-solve-our-garbage-problem-291694.html |website=[[Newsweek]] |access-date=2015-10-26}}</ref>

''Pestalotiopsis microspora'' was originally [[species description|described]] from Buenos Aires, Argentina in 1880 by mycologist [[Carlo Luigi Spegazzini]], who named it ''Pestalotia microspora''.<ref>{{cite journal |last=Spegazzini |first=C.L. |year=1880 |title=Fungi argentini. Pugillus secundus (Continuacion). |journal=Anales de la Sociedad Científica Argentina |volume=10 |pages=5–33 |language=Latin |url=https://www.biodiversitylibrary.org/page/32326211}}</ref>

In 1996 [[Julie C. Lee]] first isolated [[torreyanic acid]], a dimeric [[quinone]], from ''P. microspora'', and noted that the species is likely the cause of the decline of [[Torreya taxifolia|Florida torreya]] (''Torreya taxifolia''), an endangered species of a tree that is related to the [[paclitaxel]]-producing yew tree ''[[Taxus brevifolia]]''.<ref name="one">{{cite journal | author=Lee, Julie C.|title=Torreyanic Acid: A Selectively Cytotoxic Quinone Dimer from the Endophytic Fungus ''Pestalotiopsis microspora''.|journal=The Journal of Organic Chemistry|volume=61|pages=3232–3233|year=1996 | doi=10.1021/jo960471x | issue=10|display-authors=etal}}</ref>
