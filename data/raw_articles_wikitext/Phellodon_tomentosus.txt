'''''Phellodon tomentosus''''', commonly known as the '''zoned phellodon'''<ref name="Arora1986">{{Cite book |last=Arora |first=David |author-link=David Arora |url=https://archive.org/details/arora-david-mushrooms-demystified-a-comprehensive-guide-to-the-fleshy-fungi-ten-speed-press-1986/page/628/mode/2up |title=Mushrooms Demystified: A Comprehensive Guide to the Fleshy Fungi |publisher=[[Ten Speed Press]] |year=1986 |isbn=978-0-89815-170-1 |edition=2nd |location=Berkeley, California |pages=628 |orig-date=1979}}</ref> or '''zoned cork hydnum''',<ref name="McKnight 1987"/> is a species of [[tooth fungus]] in the family [[Bankeraceae]]. First described as ''Hydnum tomentosum'' by [[Carl Linnaeus]] in 1753, it was transferred to the genus ''[[Phellodon]]'' by [[Howard James Banker]] in 1906.<ref name="Banker 1906"/>

The brown [[Pileus (mycology)|cap]] is 1.5–5 cm wide, broad, flat, or funnel-shaped,<ref name=":0" /> sometimes fused with others, zoned with a white margin,<ref name=":1">{{cite book|last1=Trudell|first1=Steve|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=WevHvt6Tr8kC|title=Mushrooms of the Pacific Northwest|last2=Ammirati|first2=Joe|publisher=Timber Press|year=2009|isbn=978-0-88192-935-5|series=Timber Press Field Guides|location=Portland, OR|pages=234|language=en}}</ref> dry, thin, and fragrant-smelling; the taste is mild to bitter.<ref name=":0">{{Cite book|last1=Davis|first1=R. Michael|title=Field Guide to Mushrooms of Western North America|last2=Sommer|first2=Robert|last3=Menge|first3=John A.|publisher=[[University of California Press]]|year=2012|isbn=978-0-520-95360-4|location=Berkeley|pages=284–285|oclc=797915861}}</ref> The spines on the undersurface are 1–3 mm long, grayish-brown with pale tips, some running down the upper portion of the [[Stipe (mycology)|stalk]].<ref name=":0" /> The flesh is brown.<ref name=":1" /> The stalk is 1–5 cm tall and 2–5 mm wide.<ref name=":0" /> The [[Basidiospore|spores]] are white and more or less globose and spiny.<ref name=":0" />

Similar species include ''[[Phellodon atratus]]'', ''Coltricia cinnamomea'', ''[[Sarcodon fuscoindicus]]''.<ref name=":0" />

The mushroom is found in Asia, Europe, and North America.<ref name="Zhishu 1993" /> It is inedible.<ref name=":0" />
