'''''Agaricus arvensis''''', commonly known as the '''horse mushroom''',<ref name="Kuo 2007"/> is a [[mushroom]]-forming fungus of the genus ''[[Agaricus]]''.

==Taxonomy==
It was [[species description|described]] as ''Agaricus arvensis'' by [[Jacob Christian Schaeffer]] in 1774,<ref name="Schaeffer 1774"/> and given numerous binomial descriptions since. Its specific name ''arvensis'' means "of the field".

==Description==


The [[pileus (mycology)|cap]] is {{Convert|7-20|cm|abbr=on|frac=2}} across, whitish, smooth, and dry; it stains yellow, particularly when young.<ref name=":0">{{Cite book|last1=Davis|first1=R. Michael|title=Field Guide to Mushrooms of Western North America|last2=Sommer|first2=Robert|last3=Menge|first3=John A.|publisher=[[University of California Press]]|year=2012|isbn=978-0-520-95360-4|location=Berkeley|page=220|oclc=797915861}}</ref> The [[Lamella (mycology)|gills]] are pale pink to white at first, later passing through grey and brown to become dull chocolate.<ref name=":0" /> A large, spreading [[Annulus (mycology)|ring]] has white above, but sometimes with yellowish scales underneath. Viewed from below, on a closed-cap specimen, the twin-layered ring has a well-developed "cogwheel" pattern around the stipe. This is the lower part of the double ring. The stalk is {{Convert|5-12|cm|abbr=on|frac=2}} long and 1–3&nbsp;cm wide.<ref name=":0" />

The [[basidiospore|spores]] are brown and smooth,<ref name=":0" /> producing a dark brown [[spore print]].<ref name="audu">{{Cite book |last=[[Audubon]] |title=Mushrooms of North America |publisher=[[Knopf]] |year=2023 |isbn=978-0-593-31998-7 |page=541}}</ref> Its odor is similar to that of almond extract or [[marzipan]], due to the presence of [[benzaldehyde]].<ref>Dogan, A., Dalar, A., Sadullahoglu, C., Battal, A., Uzun, Y., Celik, I., &amp; Demirel, K. (2018). Investigation of the protective effects of horse mushroom (''Agaricus arvensis'' Schaeff.) against carbon tetrachloride-induced oxidative stress in rats. Molecular Biology Reports, 45(5), 787–797. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11033-018-4218-4</ref> It tends to stain yellow on bruising.<ref name=Arora1986>{{Cite book |last=Arora |first=David |author-link=David Arora |title=Mushrooms Demystified: A Comprehensive Guide to the Fleshy Fungi |url=https://archive.org/details/arora-david-mushrooms-demystified-a-comprehensive-guide-to-the-fleshy-fungi-ten-speed-press-1986/page/332/mode/2up |date=1986 |publisher=[[Ten Speed Press]] |isbn=978-0-89815-170-1 |location=Berkeley, CA |orig-date=1979 |edition=2nd |pages=332–333}}</ref>

===Similar species===
In addition to its similarity to other related species,<ref name="audu" /> this fungus can be confused with deadly species of ''[[Amanita]]''.<ref name="McKnight 1987"/>

''[[Agaricus osecanus]]'' is rare, and lacks the almond smell.<ref name="Phillips06">{{cite book | author = Roger Phillips | year = 2006  | title = Mushrooms | publisher = Pan MacMillan | isbn = 0-330-44237-6}}</ref> ''[[Agaricus xanthodermus]]'', the yellow stainer, can cause stomach upsets.<ref name=":0" />

==Distribution and habitat==
It is one of the largest white ''Agaricus'' species in Britain (where it appears during July–November), West Asia (Iran),<ref name=Asef2010>{{cite book |title=قارچهای سمی ایران (Qarch-ha-ye Sammi-ye Iran) |trans-title=Poisonous mushrooms of Iran |year=2010 |author=Asef Shayan MR. |publisher=Iran shenasi |page=214 |isbn=978-964-2725-29-8 |language=Persian}}</ref> and North America.

Frequently found near stables, as well as in meadows, it may form [[fairy ring]]s. The mushroom is often found growing with nettles (a plant that also likes nutrient-rich soil).{{Citation needed|date=March 2025}} It is associated with [[cypress]] on the North American West Coast and [[spruce]] to the east.<ref name="audu" />

==Conservation==
This mushroom is considered common and widespread, thus is not a conservation concern.<ref name="kew.org">{{cite web |url=http://www.kew.org/plants-fungi/Agaricus-arvensis.htm |title=Plants & Fungi: Agaricus arvensis (horse mushroom) - Species profile from |publisher=Kew |access-date=2013-05-19 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130318061001/http://www.kew.org/plants-fungi/Agaricus-arvensis.htm |archive-date=2013-03-18 }}</ref>

==Edibility==
This is a choice [[Edible mushroom|edible]] species.<ref name="Miller 2006">{{cite book|last1=Miller Jr.|first1=Orson K.|title=North American Mushrooms: A Field Guide to Edible and Inedible Fungi|last2=Miller|first2=Hope H.|publisher=[[FalconGuide]]|year=2006|isbn=978-0-7627-3109-1|location=Guilford, CN|page=280|author-link1=Orson K. Miller Jr.}}</ref><ref name=Arora1986/> Despite this, the [[fruit bodies]] of this and other yellow-staining ''Agaricus'' species often have a build-up of [[Heavy metal (chemistry)|heavy metals]], such as [[cadmium]], and [[copper]].<ref name="kew.org"/> Additionally, this mushroom can resemble deadly ''Amanita'' mushrooms, such as ''[[Amanita verna|A.&nbsp;verna]]''.<ref name="McKnight 1987"/>
