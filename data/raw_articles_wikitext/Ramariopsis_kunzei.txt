'''''Ramariopsis kunzei''''' is an [[edible mushroom|edible]] species of [[coral fungi]] in the family [[Clavariaceae]], and the [[type species]] of the genus ''[[Ramariopsis]]''. It is [[common name|commonly]] known as '''white coral''' because of the branched structure of the fruit bodies that resemble marine coral.

The [[sporocarp (fungus)|fruit bodies]] are up to {{convert|5|cm|abbr=on|frac=4}} tall by {{convert|4|cm|abbr=on|frac=4}} wide, with numerous branches originating from a short rudimentary stem. The branches are one to two millimeters thick, smooth, and white, sometimes with yellowish tips in age.

''Ramariopsis kunzei'' has a widespread distribution, and is found in North America, Eurasia, and Australia.

==Taxonomy==
The species was first described as ''Clavaria kunzei'' by pioneer mycologist [[Elias Magnus Fries]] in 1821.<ref name=Fries1821/> [[E.&nbsp;J.&nbsp;H. Corner]] transferred the species to ''[[Ramariopsis]]'' in 1950, and made it the [[type species]].<ref name=Corner1950/> In general, coral fungi often have extensive [[Taxonomy (biology)|taxonomic]] histories, as mycologists have not agreed on the best way to [[classification (biology)|classify]] them. In addition to ''[[Clavaria]]'' and ''[[Ramariopsis]]'', the ''R.&nbsp;kunzei'' has been placed in the genera ''[[Ramaria]]'' by [[Lucien Quélet]] in 1888, and ''[[Clavulinopsis]]'' by [[Walter Jülich]] in 1985. According to the taxonomic database [[MycoBank]],<ref name="urlMycoBank:Ramariopsis kunzei"/> the species has acquired a sizable list of [[synonym (biology)|synonyms]], listed in the taxobox. It is [[common name|commonly]] known as '''white coral''' because of the branched structure of the fruit bodies that resemble marine coral.<ref name=TyluktiVI1979/>

A [[phylogenetic]] analysis of [[clavarioid fungi]] concluded that ''R.&nbsp;kunzei'' is in a phylogenetic lineage together with several ''[[Clavulinopsis]]'' species (including ''[[Clavulinopsis sulcata|C.&nbsp;sulcata]]'', ''[[Clavulinopsis helvola|C.&nbsp;helvola]]'' and ''[[Clavulinopsis fusiformis|C.&nbsp;fusiformis]]''), and that this [[cladistics|clade]] (the ''ramariopsis'' clade) is nested within a group of species representing the family [[Clavariaceae]].<ref name=Dentinger2006/>

==Description==

The [[sporocarp (fungi)|fruit bodies]] are white to whitish-yellow in color, and are highly branched structures resembling coral; the dimensions are typically up to {{convert|8|cm|1|abbr=on}} tall and {{convert|6|cm|1|abbr=on}} wide. Older specimens may have a pinkish tinge. The tips of the branches are blunt, not crested as in some other species of coral fungi, like ''[[Clavulina cristata]]'';<ref name=TyluktiVI1979/> branches are between 1 and 5&nbsp;millimeters thick.<ref name="MushroomExpert.Com"/> The branch tips of mature specimens may be yellow.<ref name=Hemmes2002/> A [[stipe (mycology)|stem]], if present, may be up to {{convert|1|cm|1|abbr=on}} long and scurfy—covered with small flakes or scales.<ref name="Rogers"/> The texture of the [[trama (mycology)|flesh]] may range from pliable to brittle.<ref name="MushroomExpert.Com"/> The odor and taste are not distinctive.<ref name="MushroomExpert.Com" /><ref name="Jordan2004" /> This mushroom does not change color upon bruising or injury,<ref name=Hemmes2002/> but a 10%&nbsp;solution of [[Iron(II) sulfate|FeSO<sub>4</sub>]] (a [[Chemical tests in mushroom identification|chemical test]] known as "iron salts") will turn it green.<ref name=Jordan2004/>

In [[spore print|deposit]], the spores are white. Viewed with a [[light microscope]], the [[basidiospore|spores]] are [[hyaline|translucent]] and have an [[wikt:ellipsoid|ellipsoid]] to roughly spherical shape with spines on the surface, and dimensions of 3–5.5 by 2.5–4.5&nbsp;[[μm]].<ref name="Rogers"/> Spores are [[amyloid|non-amyloid]], meaning that they do not absorb [[iodine]] when stained with [[Melzer's reagent]].<ref name=Jordan2004/>  The spore-bearing cells, the [[basidia]], are usually 25–45&nbsp;μm long by 6–7&nbsp;μm wide, and 4-spored.<ref name=Ellis1990/> [[Clamp connection]]s are present in the [[hypha]]e of this species.<ref name="MushroomExpert.Com"/>

===Similar species===
'' is a lookalike species.]]

The "crested coral" (''[[Clavulina cristata]]'', edible) is similar in appearance to ''R.&nbsp;kunzei'',<ref name=":0">{{Cite book|last1=Davis|first1=R. Michael|title=Field Guide to Mushrooms of Western North America|last2=Sommer|first2=Robert|last3=Menge|first3=John A.|publisher=[[University of California Press]]|year=2012|isbn=978-0-520-95360-4|location=Berkeley|pages=294|oclc=797915861}}</ref> but its branches have fringed, feathery tips. The "jellied false coral" (''[[Tremellodendron pallidum]]'', edible)<ref name=Bessette1997/> has whitish, tough, [[wikt:cartilaginous|cartilaginous]] branches with blunt tips.<ref name=Roody2003/> ''[[Ramariopsis lentofragilis]]'' (poisonous) is an eastern North American lookalike that causes severe abdominal pain, general weakness, and pain under the sternum.<ref name=Bessette1997/>

Also similar are ''[[Artomyces pyxidatus]]'', ''[[Clavulina cinerea]]'', ''[[Clavulina rugosa]]'', and ''Clavulinopsis corniculata''.<ref name=":0" />

==Habitat and distribution==

The species is thought to be [[saprobic]] and is found growing on the ground, in [[forest floor|duff]], or less frequently on well-decayed wood.<ref name="MushroomExpert.Com"/> Fruit bodies may grow singly, in groups, or clustered together.<ref name=Ellis1990/> [[David Arora]] has noted a preference for growing under [[conifer]]s, as well as a prevalence in [[North Coast (California)|redwood forest]]s of North America.<ref name=Arora1986/> In contrast, an earlier author claimed this species grows "rarely in coniferous woods."<ref name=Coker1974/>

In Europe, ''Ramariopsis kunzei'' has been collected in Scotland (specifically, on the islands of [[Isle of Arran|Arran]], [[Gigha]] and [[Kintyre]] peninsula),<ref name=Kirk1984/> the Netherlands,<ref name=Reijnders1979/> Norway,<ref name=Gulden1974/> former Czechoslovakia,<ref name=Pilat1959/> Germany,<ref name=Gerhardt1990/> Poland,<ref name=Kornas1981/> and Russia ([[Zhiguli Mountains]]).<ref name=Malysheva2006/> It has also been found in China,<ref name=Zhuang2001/> India,<ref name=Thind1986/> Iran,<ref name=Saber1989/> the [[Solomon Islands]],<ref name=Corner1967/> and Australia.<ref name=Petersen1978/> In North America, the distribution extends north to Canada,<ref name=Pomerleau1964/> and includes the United States (including Hawaii and Puerto Rico).<ref name=Hemmes2002/><ref name=Hughes2009/>

== Edibility ==
The species is [[edible mushroom|edible]],<ref name="TyluktiVI1979" /> but according to David Arora, "fleshless and flavorless".<ref name="Arora1986" />
