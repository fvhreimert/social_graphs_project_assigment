'''''Coprinellus disseminatus''''', formerly known as '''''Coprinus disseminatus''''' and [[common name|commonly]] known as the '''fairy inkcap''',<ref name="urlRecommended English Names for Fungi - Scottish Fungi" /><ref name=":0" /> '''fairy bonnet''',<ref name="Arora1986">{{Cite book |last=Arora |first=David |author-link=David Arora |url=https://archive.org/details/arora-david-mushrooms-demystified-a-comprehensive-guide-to-the-fleshy-fungi-ten-speed-press-1986/page/352/mode/2up |title=Mushrooms Demystified: A Comprehensive Guide to the Fleshy Fungi |publisher=[[Ten Speed Press]] |year=1986 |isbn=978-0-89815-170-1 |edition=2nd |location=Berkeley, CA |pages=352-53 |orig-date=1979}}</ref> or '''trooping crumble cap''',<ref name="Harris 2014" /> is a species of [[agaric]] fungus in the family [[Psathyrellaceae]]. It can be found around dead wood in Europe and North America.

== Taxonomy ==
The species was given its current name in 1939 by [[Jakob Emanuel Lange]].<ref name="Lange 1938" />

== Description ==
''Coprinellus disseminatus'' grows to {{Convert|4|cm|frac=8}} tall. The bell-shaped cap is tannish, becoming gray with age.<ref name="tfb">{{Cite book |last=Francis-Baker |first=Tiffany |title=Concise Foraging Guide |date=2021 |publisher=[[Bloomsbury Publishing|Bloomsbury]] |isbn=978-1-4729-8474-6 |series=[[The Wildlife Trusts]] |location=London |pages=164}}</ref> The [[spore print]] is blackish-brown.<ref name="audu">{{Cite book |last=[[Audubon]] |title=Mushrooms of North America |publisher=[[Knopf]] |year=2023 |isbn=978-0-593-31998-7 |pages=593}}</ref>

The species has about 143 sexes ([[mating type]]s).<ref name=":0" />

=== Similar species ===
It is difficult to distinguish from related species or lookalikes in ''[[Tulosesus]]''.<ref name="audu" />

''C.&nbsp;disseminatus'' does not dissolve into black ink (''deliquesce'') in maturity,<ref name="Arora1986" /> unlike most other coprinoid mushrooms.{{Citation needed|date=March 2025}}

== Distribution and habitat ==
The species grows on dead wood such as rotting stumps and is widespread across Europe.<ref name="tfb" /> It can be found in North America from June to November in the East and October to March near the West Coast.<ref name="audu" />

== Uses ==
The species is nonpoisonous.<ref name="Miller 2006">{{cite book|last=Miller Jr.|first=Orson K.|title=North American Mushrooms: A Field Guide to Edible and Inedible Fungi|last2=Miller|first2=Hope H.|publisher=[[FalconGuide]]|year=2006|isbn=978-0-7627-3109-1|location=Guilford, CN|pages=227|author-link=Orson K. Miller Jr.}}</ref> It can be eaten raw or cooked but does not preserve well.<ref name="tfb" /> It is considered too small to be of value.<ref name="Arora1986" />
