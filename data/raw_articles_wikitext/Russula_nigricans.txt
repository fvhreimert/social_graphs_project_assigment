'''''Russula adusta''''', commonly known as the '''blackening brittlegill''' or '''blackening russula''', is a [[species]] of [[lamella (mycology)|gilled]] [[mushroom]]. It is a member of the ''[[Russula]]'' subgenus ''Compactae''.<ref name=":0">{{cite book|last1=Trudell|first1=Steve|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=WevHvt6Tr8kC|title=Mushrooms of the Pacific Northwest|last2=Ammirati|first2=Joe|publisher=Timber Press|year=2009|isbn=978-0-88192-935-5|series=Timber Press Field Guides|location=Portland, OR|pages=49}}</ref> The [[Pileus (mycology)|cap]] is brown to gray and somewhat shiny, with a mild taste and, reportedly, an odor of empty wine barrels.<ref name=":0" /> It has a propensity to turn black from cutting or bruising and has white [[Basidiospore|spores]].<ref name=":0" /> Similar species include ''[[Russula albonigra]]'' and ''[[Russula densifolia|R.&nbsp;densifolia]]''.<ref name=":0" />

''Russula adusta'' is found in woodlands of Europe and North America, growing under [[conifer]]s.

==Taxonomy==
''Russula adusta'' was first described by the French mycologist [[Jean Baptiste François Pierre Bulliard|Pierre Bulliard]] in 1785 as ''Agaricus nigricans'', before gaining its current binomial name from the Swedish mycologist [[Elias Magnus Fries]].

==Description==
This is a large member of the genus ''[[Russula]]'', and it has a [[pileus (mycology)|cap]] that is dirty white when young, but swiftly turns brown, and then black on aging. It measures {{convert|5|–|20|cm|in|0|abbr=on}} in diameter.<ref name=":20">{{Cite book|last1=Davis|first1=R. Michael|title=Field Guide to Mushrooms of Western North America|last2=Sommer|first2=Robert|last3=Menge|first3=John A.|publisher=[[University of California Press]]|year=2012|isbn=978-0-520-95360-4|location=Berkeley|pages=111–112|oclc=797915861}}</ref> There is usually a large depression in the centre of mature caps, which are three quarter peeling. The [[stipe (mycology)|stem]] is white, firm, and straight, measuring {{convert|5|–|8|cm|in|0|abbr=on}} long and {{convert|2|–|5|cm|in|0|abbr=on}} wide; it too blackens with age.<ref name=":20" /> The [[gills]] are off-white initially, very widely spaced, and are adnate. These turn red; then grey, and finally black, when bruised. The flesh, which has a fruity smell, when cut turns pale [[Indian red]], and then grey, and black within 20 minutes.<ref name=Phillips06/> The [[spore print]] is white, and the warty oval spores measure 7–8 x 6–7&nbsp;[[μm]].<ref name=Phillips06>{{cite book| author = Roger Phillips | year = 2006  | title = Mushrooms | publisher = Pan MacMillan | isbn = 0-330-44237-6|page= 47}}</ref>

Old specimens are sometimes parasitised by fungi of the genus ''[[Asterophora]]'' or ''Nyctalis'', in particular the species ''N.&nbsp;parasitica'' and ''N.&nbsp;asterophora'' (the pick-a-back toadstool).

=== Similar species ===
Species that also bruise red then black include ''[[Russula acrifolia]]'' and ''[[Russula dissimulans|R. dissimulans]]''.<ref name=":20" />

''[[Russula albonigra]]'' has closer gills and is far less common. It bruises directly to black, lacking the red intermediary phase.<ref name="MarcelBon87">{{cite book |author=Marcel Bon |title=The Mushrooms and Toadstools of Britain and North Western Europe |publisher=Hodder and Stoughton |year=1987 |isbn=0-340-39935-X}}</ref><ref name="Phillips06" />

== Distribution and habitat ==
''Russula adusta'' appears in late summer and autumn in both deciduous and coniferous woodland, under conifer trees,<ref name=":0" /><ref name="audu">{{Cite book |last=[[Audubon]] |title=Mushrooms of North America |publisher=[[Knopf]] |year=2023 |isbn=978-0-593-31998-7 |pages=202}}</ref> across Britain, northern Europe, and North America.<ref name="Arora2nd">{{cite book |author=David Arora |year=1986 |title=Mushrooms Demystified |publisher=Ten Speed Press |isbn=0-89815-169-4 |url-access=registration |url=https://archive.org/details/mushroomsdemysti00aror_0 }}</ref> In North America, it appears in the [[Pacific Northwest]] and northern California from October to February.<ref name="audu" />

==Toxicity==
The species contains toxins which could cause gastrointestinal upset.<ref name="Miller2006">{{cite book|last1=Miller Jr.|first1=Orson K.|title=North American Mushrooms: A Field Guide to Edible and Inedible Fungi|last2=Miller|first2=Hope H.|publisher=[[FalconGuide]]|year=2006|isbn=978-0-7627-3109-1|location=Guilford, CN|pages=80}}</ref> It is used in pickling, but must be [[Parboiling|parboiled]] first.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Подгруздок черный (Russula adusta) фото и описание |url=https://wikigrib.ru/podgruzdok-chernyj/ |access-date=2025-08-25 |website=ВикиГриб |language=ru-RU}}</ref>
