'''''Tulosesus impatiens''''' (formerly ''Coprinellus impatiens)'' is a species of [[fungus]] in the family [[Psathyrellaceae]]. First described in 1821, it has been [[classification (biology)|classified]] variously in the genera ''[[Psathyrella]]'', ''Pseudocoprinus'', ''Coprinarius'', and ''[[Coprinus]]'', before [[molecular phylogenetics]] reaffirmed it as a ''[[Coprinellus]]'' species in 2001. In 2020, German mycologists Dieter Wächter and Andreas Melzer reclassified many species of the Psathyrellaceae family based on a phylogenetic analysis and the species was renamed ''Tulosesus impatiens''.

The fungus is found in North America and Europe, where the mushrooms grow on the ground in [[deciduous forest]]s. The [[basidiocarp|fruit bodies]] have [[buff (color)|buff]] [[pileus (mycology)|caps]] that are up to {{convert|4|cm|in|abbr=on}} in diameter, held by slender whitish [[stipe (mycology)|stems]] that can be up to {{convert|10|cm|in|abbr=on}} tall. Several other ''[[Coprinopsis]]'' species that resemble ''T.&nbsp;impatiens'' may be distinguished by differences in appearance, [[habit (biology)|habit]], or spore [[morphology (biology)|morphology]].

==Taxonomy and phylogeny==
The species was first described in 1821 as ''Agaricus impatiens'' by Swedish mycologist [[Elias Magnus Fries]] in his ''Systema Mycologicum''.<ref name=Fries1821/> In 1886, [[Lucien Quélet]] transferred the species to ''Coprinarius''<ref name=Quelet1886/> (a defunct genus now [[synonym (biology)|synonymous]] with ''[[Panaeolus]]''<ref name=Kirk2008/>) and then to ''[[Coprinus]]'' a couple of years later in his ''Flore Mycologique de la France''.<ref name=Quelet1888/> In 1936, [[Robert Kühner]] segregated the genus ''Pseudocoprinus'' from ''Coprinus'', including species that did not have [[wikt:deliquescent|deliquescent]] gills (that is, [[lamella (mycology)|gills]] that "melt" into liquid), and he included ''Coprinus impatiens'' in this generic transfer. He later changed his mind about the [[Taxonomy (biology)|taxonomic]] separation of ''Coprinus'' and ''Pseudocoprinus''.<ref name=Redhead2001/> [[Claude Casimir Gillet|Gillet]] transferred the species to ''[[Psathyrella]]'' in 1936.<ref name=Gillet1936/> In 1938 [[Jakob Emanuel Lange]] published the new combination ''Coprinellus impatiens''.<ref name=Lange1938/> Despite the taxonomic shuffling, the species was popularly known as ''Coprinus impatiens'' until 2001, when a large-scale [[phylogenetic]] analysis resulted in the splitting of the genus ''Coprinus'' into several smaller genera, and the authors confirmed the validity of the generic placement in ''[[Coprinellus]]''.<ref name=Redhead2001/> The [[specific name (botany)|specific epithet]] ''impatiens'' is derived from the [[Latin]] word for "impatient".<ref name=Davidson1996/>

A 2005 [[phylogenetics]] study proposed that C''.&nbsp;impatiens'' was sister (closely related  on the [[phylogenetic tree]]) to a large ''Psathyrella'' [[cladistics|clade]], and that consequently, the genus ''Coprinellus'' was [[polyphyletic]].<ref name=Walther2005/> A later (2008) study suggested, however, that these results were due to an artifact of [[Maximum parsimony (phylogenetics)#Taxon sampling|taxon sampling]]—not enough species were analyzed to adequately represent the genetic variation in the genera. The 2008 study demonstrated that ''Coprinellus'', at that time including ''T.&nbsp;impatiens'', was [[monophyletic]], descended from a common ancestor. In their analysis, ''T.&nbsp;impatiens'' was most closely related to ''[[Tulosesus congregatus|T.&nbsp;congregatus]]'', [[Tulosesus bisporus|''T.&nbsp;bisporus'']], [[Tulosesus callinus|''T.&nbsp;callinus'']], and [[Tulosesus heterosetulosus|''T.&nbsp;heterosetulosus'']].<ref name=Padamsee2008/>

The species was known as ''Coprinellus impatiens'' until 2020 when the German mycologists [[Dieter Wächter]] & [[Andreas Melzer (Mycologist)|Andreas Melzer]] reclassified many species in the [[Psathyrellaceae]] family based on [[Phylogenetics|phylogenetic]] analysis.<ref name=":0">{{Cite journal |last1=Wächter |first1=Dieter |last2=Melzer |first2=Andreas |date=2020-11-01 |title=Proposal for a subdivision of the family Psathyrellaceae based on a taxon-rich phylogenetic analysis with iterative multigene guide tree |url=https://doi.org/10.1007/s11557-020-01606-3 |journal=Mycological Progress |language=en |volume=19 |issue=11 |pages=1151–1265 |doi=10.1007/s11557-020-01606-3 |s2cid=228976812 |issn=1861-8952|url-access=subscription }}</ref>

==Description==

The [[pileus (mycology)|cap]] of the [[basidiocarp|fruit bodies]] is initially egg-shaped, then conical to convex before flattening out, reaching diameters between {{convert|1.8|to|4|cm|in|abbr=on}}. It has deep narrow grooves reaching almost as far as the center of the cap. The surface color is a pale [[buff (color)|buff]], [[tawny (color)|tawny]] or cinnamon towards the center, but the color loses intensity when the mushroom is dry. The [[trama (mycology)|flesh]] is whitish, thin, fragile and barely deliquescent (auto-digesting). The [[lamella (mycology)|gills]] are initially [[buff (color)|buff]], then turn grayish brown. They are either free from attachment to the [[stipe (mycology)|stem]], or ''adnexed'', meaning only a small portion of the gill is attached. The stem is whitish, very slender, and more or less equal in width throughout its length, or slightly thicker at the base; its dimensions are {{convert|7|to|10|cm|in|abbr=on}} by {{convert|0.2|to|0.4|cm|in|2|abbr=on}} thick. The stem surface of young specimens are ''pruinose''—appearing to be coated with a minute layer of fine white particles; this eventually is sloughed off, leaving a smooth or silky surface. The odor and taste of the fruit bodies are not distinctive. The gills of this species do not autodigest with age, or barely do so; the fruit bodies tend to become more fragile with age.<ref name=Orton1979/><ref name=Jordan2004/>

The [[spore print]] is dark brown. The spores are smooth, [[wikt:ellipsoid|ellipsoid]] or almond-shaped, with a [[germ pore]]; they measure 9–12 by 5–6&nbsp;[[micrometre|μm]]. The spore-bearing cells, the [[basidia]], are four-spored and tetramorphic (the spores lie on several different levels, and mature at different times). The [[wikt:cheilocystidium|cheilocystidia]] ([[cystidia]] found on the gill edge) are roughly spherical, 20–35&nbsp;μm broad, or [[wikt:lageniform|lageniform]] (flask-shaped), 36–64 by 10–15&nbsp;μm, with the apex often rather acute, about 3–5&nbsp;μm wide. [[Pleurocystidium|Pleurocystidia]] (cystidia found on the gill face) are absent in this species.<ref name=Orton1979/>



==Similar species==

''[[Coprinellus disseminatus]]'' resembles ''Tulosesus&nbsp;impatiens'', but may be distinguished by its slightly larger fruit body, somewhat deliquescent gills, and tendency to fruit in smaller groups on the ground, rather than on or around rotting wood.<ref name=Arora1986/> Also, ''C.&nbsp;disseminatus'' has smaller spores than ''T.&nbsp;impatiens'', typically 6.6–9.7 by 4.1–5.8&nbsp;μm.<ref name=Burel2004/> ''Tulosesus&nbsp;eurysporus'' is similar to ''C.&nbsp;disseminatus'' but usually grows in groups on fallen branches, and has broader spores that measure 8.3–10.3 by 6.7–8.4&nbsp;μm.<ref name="urlCoprinus eurysporus ((NL: ))"/> [[Tulosesus hiascens|''T.&nbsp;hiascens'']] usually grows in small dense clumps, has narrower spores (typically 9–11 by 4.5–5.5&nbsp;μm, and produces smaller fruit bodies.<ref name=Orton1979/>

==Habitat and distribution==
''Tulosesus impatiens'' is found in North America and Europe<ref name="urlRogers"/> (including Germany,<ref name=Enderle1986/> Poland,<ref name=Sadowska1988/> and Ukraine<ref name=Prydiuk2003/>) including northern Turkey.<ref name=Turkekul2003/> In the [[Pacific Northwest]] region of the United States, it is found in [[Oregon]] and [[Idaho]].<ref name="urlwww.svims.ca"/> Fruit bodies grow solitarily, or rarely in small bundles, on [[forest floor|forest litter]] in [[deciduous forest]]s, especially ones dominated by [[beech]].<ref name="urlRogers"/><ref name=Lange1953/>
