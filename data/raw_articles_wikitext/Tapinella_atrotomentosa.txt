'''''Tapinella atrotomentosa''''', commonly known as the '''velvet roll-rim''' or '''velvet-footed tap''',<ref>{{Cite book |last1=Schwarz |first1=Christian |title=Mushrooms of the redwood coast: a comprehensive guide to the fungi of coastal northern California |last2=Siegel |first2=Noah |date=2016 |publisher=Ten Speed Press |isbn=978-1-60774-817-5 |location=Berkeley}}</ref> is a species of fungus in the family [[Tapinellaceae]]. Although it has [[lamella (mycology)|gills]], it is a member of the pored mushroom order [[Boletales]]. [[August Batsch]] described the species in 1783. It has been recorded from Eurasia and North America. Tough and inedible, it grows on tree stumps of [[conifer]]s. The mushroom contains several compounds that act as deterrents of [[fungivore|feeding by insects]].

==Taxonomy==
''Tapinella atrotomentosa'' was originally [[species description|described]] as  ''Agaricus atrotomentosus'' by German naturalist [[August Batsch]] in his 1783 work ''Elenchus Fungorum'',<ref name="Batsch 1783"/> and given its current name by Josef Šutara in 1992.<ref name="Šutara 1992"/> It is [[common name|commonly]] known as the "velvet-footed pax",<ref name="Bessette 2007"/> and the "velvet rollrim".<ref name="BMS"/> Historical [[synonym (biology)|synonyms]] include ''Paxillus atrotomentosus'' by [[Elias Magnus Fries]] (1833),<ref name="Fries 1833"/> ''Rhymovis atrotomentosa'' by [[Gottlob Ludwig Rabenhorst]] (1844),<ref name="Rabenhorst 1844"/> and ''Sarcopaxillus atrotomentosus'' by Ivan Zmitrovich (2004).<ref name="urlMycoBank: Tapinella atrotomentosa"/><ref name="Zmitrovich 2004"/> The [[variety (botany)|variety]] ''bambusinus'' was described from Trinidad in 1951 by British mycologists [[Richard Eric Defoe Baker]] and [[William Thomas Dale]].<ref name="Baker 1951"/>

The [[botanical name|species name]] is derived from the [[Latin]] words ''atrotomentosus'', meaning "black-haired".<ref name="Nilper 1977"/> It is still commonly seen under its old name ''Paxillus atrotomentosus'' in guidebooks.<ref name="Buczacki 2012"/> ''Tapinella atrotomentosa'' and its relative ''[[Tapinella panuoides|T.&nbsp;panuoides]]'' were placed in a separate genus ''[[Tapinella (fungus)|Tapinella]]'' on account of their habit of growing on (and rotting) wood, and microscopic differences including much smaller spore size, lack of [[cystidia]], and differing [[basidia]].<ref name="Šutara 1992"/> Their off-centre stipe also distinguished them from other members of the genus ''[[Paxillus]]'',<ref name="Hoiland 1987"/> and genetic analysis confirmed them as only distantly related.<ref name="Bresinsky 1999"/>

==Description==
The [[basidiocarp|fruit body]] is squat mushroom with a [[pileus (mycology)|cap]] up to {{convert|20|cm|abbr=on|frac=2}} wide,<ref name="Arora1986">{{Cite book |last=Arora |first=David |author-link=David Arora |url=https://archive.org/details/arora-david-mushrooms-demystified-a-comprehensive-guide-to-the-fleshy-fungi-ten-speed-press-1986/page/478/mode/2up |title=Mushrooms Demystified: A Comprehensive Guide to the Fleshy Fungi |publisher=[[Ten Speed Press]] |year=1986 |isbn=978-0-89815-170-1 |edition=2nd |location=Berkeley, CA |pages=478–79 |orig-date=1979}}</ref> sepia- or walnut brown in colour with a rolled rim and depressed centre. The [[lamella (mycology)|gills]] are cream-yellow and forked. The thick [[Stipe (mycology)|stipe]] juts out sidewards from the mushroom;<ref name="Breitenbach 1991" /> it is up to 12&nbsp;cm long and dark brown, covered with dark brown velvety fur.<ref name="Arora1986" />

The flesh is yellowish,<ref name=":0">{{cite book|last1=Trudell|first1=Steve|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=WevHvt6Tr8kC|title=Mushrooms of the Pacific Northwest|last2=Ammirati|first2=Joe|publisher=Timber Press|year=2009|isbn=978-0-88192-935-5|series=Timber Press Field Guides|location=Portland, OR|pages=185}}</ref> and has been described as appetising in appearance, and is little affected by insects;<ref name="Haas 1969" /> the taste however is acrid.<ref name=":0" /> The [[spore print]] is yellowish to brownish<ref name="Arora1986" /> and the spores are round to oval, measuring 5–6&nbsp;[[μm]] long.<ref name="Zeitlmayr l976" />

==Distribution and habitat==
It is a [[saprobic]] fungus found growing on tree stumps of [[conifer]]s in North America, Europe,<ref name="Roberts 2011"/> Central America (Costa Rica),<ref name="Gómez-Pignataro 1992"/> east into Asia where it has been recorded from Pakistan<ref name="Sarwar 2007"/> and China.<ref name="Zang 1978"/> The fruit bodies appear in summer and autumn, even in drier spells when other mushrooms are not evident.<ref name="Haas 1969"/>

==Toxicity==
The mushrooms are not generally considered [[edible mushroom|edible]],<ref name="Nilper 1977" /> but have been used as a food source in parts of eastern Europe.<ref name="Roberts 2011" /> The species contains toxins which may cause [[gastrointestinal upset]].<ref name="Miller 20062">{{cite book|last1=Miller Jr.|first1=Orson K.|title=North American Mushrooms: A Field Guide to Edible and Inedible Fungi|last2=Miller|first2=Hope H.|publisher=[[FalconGuide]]|year=2006|isbn=978-0-7627-3109-1|location=Guilford, CN|pages=288|author-link=Orson K. Miller Jr.}}</ref> There have been cases of poisoning reported in European literature.<ref name="Musselius 2002"/> Linus Zeitlmayr reports that young mushrooms are edible, but warns than older ones have a foul bitter or inky flavour and are possibly poisonous.<ref name="Zeitlmayr l976" /> The bitter flavour is allegedly improved by boiling the mushrooms and discarding the water, but is indigestible to many.<ref name="Haas 1969" />

Tests on the chemical composition and free [[amino acid]] levels of the mushroom suggest that they are not considerably different from other edible gilled mushrooms such as ''[[Armillaria mellea]]''.<ref name="Zhuk 1982" />

==Chemistry==
''Tapinella atrotomentosa'' has a wound-activated [[Plant defense against herbivory|defence mechanism]] whereby injured fruit bodies convert chemicals known as leucomentins into [[atromentin]], [[butenolide]], and the feeding deterrent osmundalactone.<ref name="Spiteller 2002"/> Atromentin had previously been identified as the [[biological pigment|pigment]] producing the brown colour of the cap,<ref name="Kögl 1928"/> but was not characterized as a chemical defence compound until 1989.<ref name="Holzapfel 1989"/>  Other compounds produced by the fungus include the orange-yellow flavomentins and violet spiromentin pigments.<ref name="Besl 1989"/> A novel dimeric [[lactone]], bis-osumundalactone, was isolated from the variety ''bambusinus''.<ref name="Hashimoto 2002"/>

Several [[phytoecdysteroids]] (compounds related to the insect moulting hormone [[ecdysteroid]]) have been identified from the fungus, including paxillosterone, 20,22-''p''-hydroxybenzylidene acetal, atrotosterones A, B, and C, and 25-hydroxyatrotosterones A and B.<ref name="Vokáč 1998"/>
