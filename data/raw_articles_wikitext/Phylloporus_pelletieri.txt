'''''Phylloporus pelletieri''''', commonly known as the '''golden-gilled bolete''', is a species of [[fungus]] in the family [[Boletaceae]].

==Taxonomy==
The species was first described by French mycologist [[Joseph-Henri Léveillé]] in 1867 under the name ''Agaricus pelletieri''.<ref name="Leveille1867" /> [[Lucien Quélet]] transferred it to ''[[Phylloporus]]'' in 1888.<ref name="Quelet1888" />

==Description==
The underside of its [[Pileus (mycology)|cap]] bears [[lamella (mycology)|lamellae]] (gill-like structures) rather than the pores common in the [[Boletales]]. The reddish, domed cap is smooth with a velvety texture, while the lamellae are bright yellow.<ref>Pegler, D. & Spooner, B. (1992) The Mushroom Identifier. Apple Press, London.</ref> The [[Stipe (mycology)|stem]] supporting the cap is also yellow with a red-brown [[Veil (botany)|veil]].<ref>[http://www.grzyby.pl/index.html Fungi of Poland] (July 2003).</ref>{{Unreliable source?|date=August 2011}}

==Distribution and habitat==

The golden-gilled bolete forms [[mycorrhiza]]l relationships with broadleaved trees such as [[beech]] and [[conifer]]ous trees such as [[fir]] or [[pine]].<ref name=coe/> The [[basidiocarp|fruit bodies]] are produced in summer and autumn.<ref name=coe/>

Although rare, the golden-gilled bolete has a widespread distribution in Europe and reaches into Asia.<ref name=coe>[http://www.nature.coe.int/CP21/tpvs34e.htm European Council for Conservation of Fungi (2001) Datasheets of threatened mushrooms of Europe, candidates for listing in Appendix I of the Convention. Bern Convention Standing Committee.]</ref> It inhabits [[Temperate broadleaf and mixed forests|broadleaf]] or coniferous forests in [[montane]] or [[sub-alpine]] regions, where it is associated with [[acidic soil|acidic]] or sandy soils.<ref name=coe/>

==Ecology==

The species is threatened by air pollution and forestry plantations, which can destroy its natural habitat.<ref name="coe" /> It is short-listed for inclusion in Appendix&nbsp;I of the [[Convention on the Conservation of European Wildlife and Natural Habitats]], otherwise known as the Bern Convention, by the [[European Council for Conservation of Fungi]] (ECCF), and included on the [[Regional Red List|Red List]]s of 12 European countries.<ref>[http://www.artdata.slu.se/Bern_Fungi/Bern_Fungi.htm The distribution, status and habitat requirement of the 33 fungal candidates for listing in Appendix I of the Bern Convention] (June 2003)</ref> Other conservation recommendations include the mapping of existing sites and a reduction in air pollution, together with restrictions on forestry practice at known locations.<ref name="coe" />
