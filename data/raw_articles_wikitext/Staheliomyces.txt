'''''Staheliomyces''''' is a fungal [[genus]] in the [[Phallaceae|stinkhorn family]]. The genus was considered [[monotypic]] for over 100 years, containing the single [[neotropical]] species '''''Staheliomyces cinctus''''', until a 2022 study revealed four additional, cryptic species. Members are colloquially known as the '''strangled stinkhorns'''. The genus is found in Central America and northern South America.<ref name=Zeller1948/> The fruit body of the fungus is a hollow, whitish, cylindric [[stipe (mycology)|stalk]] up to {{convert|16|cm|in|abbr=on}} tall, with conspicuous pits and holes. Near the top of the stalk is a pinched-off zone covered with unpleasant-smelling slimy [[spore]] mass called [[gleba]]. The gleba attracts [[stingless bees]] that help disseminate the spores.

==Taxonomy==
The genus was first described scientifically by the German mycologist [[Eduard Fischer (mycologist)|Eduard Fischer]] in 1921. The [[specific name (botany)|specific epithet]] honors Gerold Stahel (1887 - 1955) a Swiss botanist who collected the [[type (biology)|type]] specimen in [[Suriname]].<ref name=Fischer1921/> The fungus has been called the "strangled stinkhorn".<ref name="Kibby 2015"/>

==Description==
Like all stinkhorns, the [[basidiocarp|fruit body]] begins its development as a partially submerged "egg" form attached to [[rhizomorph]]s. The egg is roughly ovoid, measuring {{convert|1.5|to|2|cm|in|1|abbr=on}} by {{convert|1.2|–|1.6|cm|in|1|abbr=on}}.<ref name="urlRogers"/> The fruit body consists of a spongy, hollow white [[stipe (mycology)|stalk]] between {{convert|12|–|16|cm|in|abbr=on}} tall,<ref name="urlMushroomExpert.Com"/> with a collar of greenish-brown [[gleba]] positioned above the center, but below the apex. The glebal band is constricted compared to the width of the remainder of the stalk, between {{convert|1|and|2|cm|in|1|abbr=on}} thick. The [[pileus (mycology)|cap]], or receptaculum, is at the top of the stalk; it is spongy, conical, and chambered, free from gleba. A [[volva (mycology)|volva]] sits at the base of the stem, and has [[rhizomorph]]s attached to it.<ref name=Miller1988/> The [[spore]]s are elliptical, [[hyaline]] (translucent), and measure 2.5–3 by 1.2–1.5 [[micrometre|μm]].<ref name=Leite2007/> The [[edible mushroom|edibility]] of the mushroom is unknown.<ref name="urlRogers"/>

==Habitat and distribution==
[[image:Irapuã_-_REFON.jpg|thumb|150px|left|''[[Trigona spinipes]]'']]
''Staheliomyces'' is a [[saprobic]] genus, typically found in soil rich with decomposing plant remains.<ref name="urlMushroomExpert.Com"/> It is distributed from southern Mexico to southern Brazil,<ref name=Leite2007/><ref name=Saenz1982/> where it appears during [[rainy season]]s.<ref name="urlMushroomExpert.Com"/>

A [[field study]] from Ecuador reported that the fruit bodies of ''Staheliomyces'' are visited by species from the [[stingless bee]] genus ''[[Trigona (genus)|Trigona]]''.<ref name="Burr1996" /> The bee visits are several minutes in duration, during which time small portions of the gleba are collected and stored in the [[pollen basket]] (corbiculae) of the hind legs. The authors determined that the corbiculae of approaching bees are always empty. Bee corbiculae are normally used to carry food and nest-building material, and the gleba of ''Staheliomyces'' may be used for one of these purposes, but the authors did not observe the bees after they had collected the gleba.<ref name=Burr1996/>
