'''''Hydnellum compactum''''' is a rare [[Hydnoid fungi|tooth fungus]] in the family [[Bankeraceae]]. It is characterised by its astringent taste, yellowish flesh. It is found in Europe, where it grows in [[Deciduous#Regions|deciduous forest]], typically under [[beech]]. [[Basidiocarp|Fruit bodies]] of the fungus grow singly or in groups.

They are [[Spinning top|top]]-shaped, with convex or flattened upper surfaces up to {{convert|10|cm|in|0|abbr=on}} in diameter. The surface texture is initially felt-like before becoming pitted and rough in age. The [[stipe (mycology)|stipe]], roughly the same color as the cap, is solid and measures {{convert|2|–|4|cm|1|abbr=on}} long by {{convert|1|–|3|cm|in|1|abbr=on}} thick. On the fertile cap underside (the [[hymenium]]), there are white to purple-brown, curved spines up to 5&nbsp;mm long. The color is whitish at first but gradually turns dark brown to blackish. The [[basidiospore|spores]] measure 5.5–6 by 3.5–4.5&nbsp;[[micrometre|μm]], and feature [[tubercle]]s that sometimes have a sunken tip.<ref name="Ellis 1990" />{{mycomorphbox

''Hydnellum compactum'' is ectomycorrhizal with [[Quercus petraea|Sessile Oak]] (''Quercus petraea''), [[Quercus robur|Common Oak]] (''Q. robur'') and Beech (''Fagus sylvatica'') as well as [[Castanea sativa|Sweet Chestnut]] (Castanea sativa) occasionally. It prefers in nutrient poor, dry to moist, loamy and sandy soils, and sunny, mosaic, old-growth forests.<ref name="Ellis 1990" />

It is considered [[endangered species|endangered]] is [[Switzerland]], and listed as vulnerable on the IUCN red list.<ref name="Ellis 1990" /> It is estimated there are less than 4000 mature individuals of this species left in Europe. Its presence has particularly decreased in the Netherlands.<ref>{{Cite journal |last1=Gulden |first1=G. |last2=Hanssen |first2=E. W. |date=1992-02-01 |title=Distribution and ecology of stipitate hydnaceous fungi in Norway, with special reference to the question of decline |journal=Sommerfeltia |language=en |volume=13 |issue=1 |page=1 |doi=10.2478/som-1992-0001|doi-access=free }}</ref> Its main threats are logging, as old-growth forests are decreasing across northern and central Europe. In western Europe, this fungus is mainly threatened by air pollution, such as [[Acid rain|acidificiation]] and nitrogen deposition.<ref name="Ellis 1990" />
