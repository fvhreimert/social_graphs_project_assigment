'''''Amanita albocreata''''', also called the '''ringless panther'''<ref name=Tulloss>{{cite web|last=R. E.|first=Tulloss|title=Amanita albocreata G. F. Atk. "Ringless Panther"|url=http://www.njcc.com/~ret/amanita/species/albocrea.html}}</ref> or the '''ringless panther amanita''',<ref name=NGSWG>{{NGSWG}}</ref> is a species of [[fungus]] in the family [[Amanitaceae]]. It was discovered in 1944, by [[William Murrill]]. It is commonly found in the northeastern United States and parts of southeastern Canada. It normally grows between the rainy months of June and August.<ref name="Rogers mushrooms">{{cite news|first=Roger|last=Phillips|title=Amanita albocreata|publisher=Rogers Mushrooms}}</ref>

==Taxonomy==
First described in 1902 by [[George Francis Atkinson]] under the name ''Amanitopsis albocreata'',<ref name="Atkinson1902" /> the species was then transferred to ''[[Amanita]]'' in 1941 by one Jean-Edouard Gilbert.<ref name="Gilbert1941" />

==Description==
* '''Cap''': The lengths of the cap can vary from {{Convert|2-8|cm|abbr=off|frac=2}}. It can appear convex or shield-shaped. The cap's disc has been seen colored white to pale yellow, with easily removed flaky patches or warts of whitish volva remnants.<ref name="Rogers mushrooms"/> The center can be tan or creamy yellow in color. Akin to its relative ''[[Amanita frostiana]]'', the cap feels smooth and sticky when moist.<ref>{{cite book|last=Kauffman C. H.|title=The Agaricaceae of Michigan|year=1918|publisher=W.H. Crawford, state printers|pages=622|isbn=9780384287808 |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=f6PhAAAAMAAJ&q=amanita+albocreata|edition=1}}</ref>
* '''Gills''': Gills can be free or slightly adnate. They are about {{Convert|3–10.5|mm|frac=8}} broad, with a minutely flocculose edge. The short gills are truncate to excavate-truncate with or without an attenuate "tooth" at the juncture with the flesh of the cap. They are cream to pale cream in color.
* '''Stipe''': The stem, or stipe, measures 80–120 x 6–8 mm. It lacks a ring but consists of a volva. The notable bulb (dimensions 15–22 x 12–20 mm) bears a distinct white collar as do some species with annulate stems, like other ''[[Amanita]]'' species ''[[Amanita multisquamosa|A. multisquamosa]]'', ''[[Amanita velatipes|A. velatipes]]'' and ''[[Amanita pantherina|A. pantherina]]''.
* '''Spores and microscopic features''': The spores measure (7.3-) 7.7–9.5 (-11.6) x 6.6–8.4 (-9.4) μm and are globose to subglobose or occasionally broadly ellipsoid and inamyloid. Clamps are rare at bases of basidia.<ref name=Metzler>{{cite book|last=Metzler S.|first=Metzler V.|title=Texas Mushrooms-a field guide|year=1992|publisher=University of Texas Press|location=Texas|isbn=978-0-292-75126-2|pages=331|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=HRtfvVigMmsC&q=amanita+albocreata&pg=PA331}}</ref>
* '''Flesh''': This mushroom has thin and sticky flesh under the cap.<ref name="Rogers mushrooms"/>

==Distribution and habitat==
This fungus is found in the hardwood-hemlock ([[Tsuga]]) forest of the [[northeastern United States]] and southeastern Canada and of [[boreal forest]] at least as far north as the [[Island of Newfoundland]]. Commonly it is found in coniferous and deciduous forests or open lush green grasslands.<ref name="Rogers mushrooms" />

==Edibility==
This species is of unknown edibility and is possibly [[Mushroom poisoning|poisonous]].<ref>{{cite book |last=Phillips |first=Roger |title=Mushrooms and Other Fungi of North America |year=2010 |publisher=Firefly Books |location=Buffalo, NY |isbn=978-1-55407-651-2 |page=18}}</ref><ref name=Tulloss/>
