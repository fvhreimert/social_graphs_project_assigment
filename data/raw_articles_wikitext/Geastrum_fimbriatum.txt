'''''Geastrum fimbriatum''''', commonly known as the '''fringed earthstar''' or the '''sessile earthstar''', is an inedible species of [[mushroom]] belonging to the genus ''[[Geastrum]]'', or earthstar fungi.

First described in 1829, it is distinguished from other earthstars by the delicate fibers that line the circular pore at the top of its spore sac. The species has a widespread distribution, being found in Eurasia and the Americas.

==Taxonomy==
[[Elias Magnus Fries]] described ''Geastrum fimbriatum'' (as ''Geaster fimbriatus'') in his 1829 ''Systema mycologicum''.<ref name="Fries 1829"/>

It is [[common name|commonly]] known as the fringed earthstar<ref name="McKnight 1987"/> or the sessile earthstar.<ref name="Mitchell 2006"/> The [[botanical name|specific epithet]] ''fimbriatum'' means "fringed", referring to the characteristic edge of the apical spore of the spore sac.<ref name="Roody 2003"/>

==Description==
The fruit bodies of ''G.&nbsp;fimbriatum'' start out roughly spherical and [[hypogeous]]. As it matures, it pushed up through the soil and the other layer of the spore case ([[exoperidium]]) splits open to form between 5 and 8 rays that curve downward. The fully expanded fruit body has a diameter of up to {{convert|3|cm|frac=4}}. Before expansion, the outer surface has a cottony surface with adherent soil particles; this ultimately peels off to reveal a smooth, grayish-brown surface. The inner spore sac is yellowish brown and features a small conical pore with fringed edges. Unlike other similar earthstar fungi, the edges of this pore are not sharply delimited from the rest of the spore sac, and do not have grooves.<ref name="McKnight 1987"/> The fruit bodies have no distinctive taste or odor.<ref name="Jordan 2004"/>

The [[spore]]s are spherical, roughened by many small points or warts, and measure 2.4–4&nbsp;[[μm]]. The [[capillitium]] is thick-walled, unbranched, and 4–7&nbsp;μm thick.<ref name="McKnight 1987"/>

=== Similar species ===

Similar species include ''[[Geastrum saccatum|G.&nbsp;saccatum]]'', which is larger – up to {{convert|5|cm|in|abbr=on|frac=4}} across – and has a clearly delimited ring-like area around the pore opening. ''[[Geastrum rufescens|G.&nbsp;rufescens]]'' has reddish tones that are absent from ''G.&nbsp;fimbriatum''.<ref name="McKnight 1987"/>
==Habitat and distribution==
''Geastrum fimbriatum'' is a [[saprobic]] species,<ref name="Roody 2003"/> and it fruit bodies grow on the ground in groups or clusters, usually near the stumps of [[hardwood]] trees. It is found in Europe,<ref name="Jordan 2004" /> Asia (India<ref name="Sanjeev Kumar 2011"/> and Mongolia),<ref name="Kreisel 1975"/> eastern North America<ref name="McKnight 1987"/> (including Mexico),<ref name="Esqueda 2003"/> Central America (Costa Rica),<ref name="Calonge 2005"/> and South America (Brazil).<ref name="Trierveiler-Pereira 2011"/>

== Uses ==
Although typically listed by field guides as an inedible species,<ref name="Roody 2003" /> it is eaten by the tribal peoples of [[Madhya Pradesh]].<ref name="De Roman 2010" />

== In culture ==
The species was depicted in a Nigerian postage stamp in 1985.<ref name="Moss 1998"/>
