'''''Entoloma abortivum''''', commonly known as the '''aborted entoloma'''<ref name=Spahr2009/> or '''shrimp of the woods''', is an mushroom in the [[Entolomataceae]] family of [[fungi]]. First named ''Clitopilus abortivus'' by [[Miles Joseph Berkeley]] and [[Moses Ashley Curtis]], it was given its current name by the Dutch mycologist [[Marinus Anton Donk]] in 1949.<ref name=Donk1949/>

The grayish cap is up to {{Convert|8|cm}} wide. The stem is up to 9&nbsp;cm long and {{Convert|1.4|cm|abbr=on|frac=8}} thick. The smell is mealy and the [[spore print]] is pinkish.<ref name="audu">{{Cite book |last=[[Audubon]] |title=Mushrooms of North America |publisher=[[Knopf]] |year=2023 |isbn=978-0-593-31998-7 |pages=508}}</ref>

It was once believed that the honey mushroom, ''[[Armillaria mellea]]'', was parasitizing the ''Entoloma'', but research<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.cybertruffle.org.uk/cyberliber/59350/0093/005/0841.htm|title = Mycologia 93 (5): 841, 2001}}</ref> has indicated that the inverse is true—the ''Entoloma'' parasitizes the honey mushroom.

In Mexico it is called ''Totlcoxcatl'' ('turkey wattle') due to its irregular shape.<ref>{{cite book |last1=Dorr |first1=Alex |title=The Little Book of Mushrooms |date=2023 |publisher=Adams Media |isbn=978-1-5072-1959-1}}</ref> Caution should be used in identifying the species before eating,<ref>{{cite book |last=Phillips |first=Roger |title=Mushrooms and Other Fungi of North America |publisher=Firefly Books |year=2010 |isbn=978-1-55407-651-2 |location=Buffalo, NY |page=157}}</ref> with similar species such as ''[[Entoloma sinuatum]]'' being poisonous.<ref name="Miller 2006">{{cite book |last1=Miller Jr. |first1=Orson K. |author-link=Orson K. Miller Jr. |title=North American Mushrooms: A Field Guide to Edible and Inedible Fungi |last2=Miller |first2=Hope H. |publisher=[[FalconGuide]] |year=2006 |isbn=978-0-7627-3109-1 |location=Guilford, CN |pages=215}}</ref>
