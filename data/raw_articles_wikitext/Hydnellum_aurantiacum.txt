'''''Hydnellum aurantiacum''''' is a species of [[fungus]], commonly known as the '''orange spine''' or '''orange hydnellum''' for its reddish orange or rusty red colored [[basidiocarp|fruit bodies]]. Like other [[tooth fungi]], it bears a layer of spines rather than [[lamella (mycology)|gills]] on the underside of the [[pileus (mycology)|cap]]. The species is listed as [[critically endangered]] in the United Kingdom. It is inedible.

==Taxonomy==
''Hydnellum aurantiacum'' was first [[species description|described]] by the German naturalist [[August Batsch]] in 1789, with the name ''Hydnum suberosum'' var. ''aurantiacum''.<ref name="Batsch 1789"/> It was given its current scientific name by [[Petter Karsten]], who transferred it to ''[[Hydnellum]]'' in 1879.<ref name="Karsten 1879"/> ''Hydnellum aurantiacum'' has acquired several [[Synonym (biology)|synonyms]] in its [[taxonomy (biology)|taxonomic]] history, including ''Hydnum stohlii'', published by [[Gottlob Ludwig Rabenhorst]] in 1873, and ''Hydnellum complectipes'', published by Hall in 1972.<ref name="Hall 1972"/> Additional synonyms resulting from generic transfers include ''Hydnum aurantiacum'' ([[Johannes Baptista von Albertini]] and [[Lewis David de Schweinitz]], 1825); ''Calodon aurantiacus'' (Karsten, 1881); and ''Phaeodon aurantiacus'' ([[Joseph Schröter]], 1888).<ref name="urlFungorum synonymy: Hydnellum aurantiacum"/>

The [[specific name (botany)|specific epithet]] ''aurantiacum'' is derived from the [[Latin]] for "orange". [[Common name]]s for the fungus include "orange spine",<ref name="McKnight 1987"/> "orange corky spine fungus",<ref name="Pegler 1997"/> and the [[British Mycological Society]] approved English name "orange Hydnellum".<ref name="BMS common names"/>

==Description==


The fruitbodies are shallowly funnel-shaped ([[wikt:infundibuliform|infundibuliform]]) and up to {{convert|15|cm|frac=4}} in diameter.<ref name="Arora 1986"/> The upper surface is orange or orange-brown in the centre, with a lighter margin. It may be velvety or [[tomentose]] when young, but will become wrinkled or lumpy in age. The flesh is tough and woody, pale to dark orange-brown in color, without any distinctive odor but a bitter or mealy taste.<ref name="Orr 1980"/> The teeth are short (up to 5&nbsp;mm long), white, but the tips gradually turn brown with age. The [[stipe (mycology)|stipe]] is up to 4&nbsp;cm long and 0.5–2&nbsp;cm thick, orange to dark brown in color, with a velvety surface.<ref name="Ellis 1990"/> The [[spore print]] is brown. This species is inedible<ref>{{cite book |last=Phillips |first=Roger |title=Mushrooms and Other Fungi of North America |url=https://archive.org/details/mushroomsotherfu0000phil |url-access=registration |year=2010 |publisher=Firefly Books |location=Buffalo, NY |isbn=978-1-55407-651-2 |page=[https://archive.org/details/mushroomsotherfu0000phil/page/320 320]}}</ref> due to the toughness of the flesh and its poor taste.<ref name="Miller 20062">{{cite book|last1=Miller Jr.|first1=Orson K.|title=North American Mushrooms: A Field Guide to Edible and Inedible Fungi|last2=Miller|first2=Hope H.|publisher=[[FalconGuides]]|year=2006|isbn=978-0-7627-3109-1|location=Guilford, CN|pages=406|author-link=Orson K. Miller Jr.}}</ref>

[[Basidia]] (the spore-bearing cells) are between 35 and 46 by 8–11&nbsp;[[μm]], club-shaped ([[wikt:clavate|clavate]]), without [[clamp connection]]s, and four-spored. The [[sterigmata]] (extensions of the basidia bearing spores) may be up to 6&nbsp;μm long. The [[Basidiospore|spore]]s are roughly spherical in shape, with rough warty outgrowths ([[wikt:tubercle|tubercles]]), [[nonamyloid]], and have dimensions of 5.5–8 by 5.5–6.5&nbsp;μm.<ref name="Baird 1986"/>

===Similar species===

The species resembles the polypore ''[[Phaeolus schweinitzii]]'' when viewed from the top of the cap surface, but it has teeth instead of pores on the [[hymenium]]. Closely related and [[morphology (biology)|morphologically]] similar species in the genus ''Hydnellum'' include ''[[Hydnellum auratile|H.&nbsp;auratile]]'' (has more uniformly coloured flesh), ''[[Hydnellum caeruleum|H.&nbsp;caeruleum]]'' (may look similar in age),<ref>{{cite book|last1=Trudell|first1=Steve|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=WevHvt6Tr8kC|title=Mushrooms of the Pacific Northwest|last2=Ammirati|first2=Joe|publisher=Timber Press|year=2009|isbn=978-0-88192-935-5|series=Timber Press Field Guides|location=Portland, OR|pages=231–232|language=en}}</ref> ''H.&nbsp;congenum'' (has thin flesh in the cap), ''H.&nbsp;ferrugipes'', ''[[Hydnellum earlianum|H.&nbsp;earlianum]]'' (has a smoother cap, and spines have sulfur-yellow tips, not white).<ref name="McKnight 1987"/><ref name="Arora 1986"/>

==Distribution and habitat==
This species is typically found growing solitary or in clusters on the ground in [[coniferous forest|conifer]] and [[Temperate broadleaf and mixed forests|mixed woods]]. Rarely, fruit bodies may have their stipes fused together.<ref name="Hall 1972"/> ''Hydnellum aurantiacum'' has been reported from Australia,<ref name="May 2003"/> Europe, North America,<ref name="Miller 2006"/> and Asia, including China<ref name="Dai 2010"/> India,<ref name="Lyngdoh 2014"/> and Korea.<ref name="Lee 2005"/> It is one of the most frequently encountered Thelephorales species found in the [[Sverdlovsk region]] of Russia.<ref name="Shiryaev 2008"/>

==Conservation==

Due to substantial declines in sightings, this species is listed as [[critically endangered]] in the United Kingdom.<ref name="urlTrees for Life - Species Profile: Pinewood tooth fungi">{{cite web |url=http://www.treesforlife.org.uk/tfl.tooth_fungi.html |title=Trees for Life - Species Profile: Pinewood tooth fungi |access-date=2009-01-05 |archive-date=2008-12-06 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20081206153305/http://www.treesforlife.org.uk/tfl.tooth_fungi.html |url-status=dead }}</ref><ref name="urlAction plan for Grouped plan for tooth fungi">{{cite web|url=http://www.ukbap.org.uk/UKPlans.aspx?ID=338 |title=Action plan for Grouped plan for tooth fungi |access-date=2009-01-05 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20081008044539/http://www.ukbap.org.uk/UKPlans.aspx?ID=338 |archive-date=2008-10-08 }}</ref>

==Chemistry==
 of aurantiacin]]

The [[pigment]] responsible for the characteristic orange color of ''H.&nbsp;aurantiacum'' has been identified as the ''p''-[[terphenyl]] compound named aurantiacin.<ref name="Gripenberg 1956"/> This dark red pigment, a [[derivative (chemistry)|derivative]] of the compound [[atromentin]], has subsequently been identified in other species of ''Hydnellum''.<ref name="Velíšek 2011"/> The compounds dihydroaurantiacin dibenzoate and [[thelephoric acid]] have also been reported.<ref name="Gripenberg 1958"/>

''Hydnellum aurantiacum'' is used in [[mushroom dye]]ing, in which it produces grayish to greenish-gray colors depending on the [[mordant]] used.<ref name="Bessette 2001"/>
