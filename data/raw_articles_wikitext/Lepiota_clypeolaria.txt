'''Lepiota clypeolaria''', commonly known as the '''shield dapperling''' or the '''shaggy-stalked Lepiota''', is a common species of mushroom in the genus ''[[Lepiota]]''. The [[Basidiocarp|fruit bodies]] have a brownish [[pileus (mycology)|cap]], a shaggy [[stipe (mycology)|stipe]] with a collapsed, sheathing [[annulus (mycology)|ring]] or ring zone, and spindle-shaped [[spore]]s.

It is widely distributed in northern [[temperate]] zones, where it grows in [[deciduous forest|deciduous]] and [[conifer]]ous forests.

==Taxonomy==
The species was first [[species description|described]] in 1789 as ''Agaricus clypeolarius'' by French mycologist [[Jean Baptiste Francois Bulliard]]. [[Paul Kummer]] transferred it to ''[[Lepiota]]'' in 1871.<ref name="urlFungorum: Lepiota clypeolaria"/> It is [[common name|commonly]] known as the "shaggy-stalked Lepiota".<ref name="Laessoe 2002"/>

''L. clypeolaria'' is probably the best known of the section ''Fusisporae'' within genus ''Lepiota'', whose members are characterized by long spindle-shaped spores and a fluffy stem beneath the ring.<ref name="Courtecuisse 2013"/>

The [[pileus (mycology)|cap]] is egg-shaped when young, soon broadly bell-shaped and has pale straw- or orange-brown scales on a pale background. The central [[umbo (mycology)|umbo]] is covered with a well-delimited uniform disk of the same colour as the scales. It grows to a diameter of {{convert|4–7|cm|abbr=on}}. The [[lamella (mycology)|gills]] are white, crowded, free from attachment to the stipe.<ref name="Courtecuisse 1999"/>

The white [[stipe (mycology)|stem]] has an indistinct [[annulus (mycology)|ring]], below which it is coarsely woolly, giving an appearance which is sometimes described as "booted". The stipe, which measures {{convert|5–12|cm|abbr=on}} long by {{convert|0.3–1|cm|abbr=on}} thick, is hollow and slender, expanding slightly at the base into a club shape. The [[trama (mycology)|flesh]] is white and has an unpleasant smell.<ref name="Courtecuisse 1999"/>

The [[spore print]] is white. The [[Basidiospore|spores]] are fuse-shaped (fusiform), meaning that they are tapered at both ends.  They have dimensions of 12–16 by 5–6&nbsp;[[μm]]. [[Cystidia]] on the gill edge (cheilocystidia) are club-shaped to cylindrical, and measure 20–40 by 5–15&nbsp;μm.<ref name="Courtecuisse 1999"/>

===Similar species===


''[[Lepiota ochraceosulfurescens]]'' may be distinguished as having a less defined dark area in the cap centre, a smell of rubber or melted butter, and yellow flesh in the stipe base,<ref name="Eyssartier"/> but Species Fungorum and Funga Nordica regard this name as a synonym.<ref name="urlFungorum: Lepiota clypeolaria"/><ref name="FungaNordica"/>

''[[Lepiota magnispora]]'' is similar in appearance but has brighter colours with a more intensely coloured cap center and longer spores.<ref name="urlMushroomExpert"/>

==Habitat and distribution==
The fruit bodies grow singly or in small groups on the ground in [[deciduous forest|deciduous]] and [[conifer]]ous forests. A common species, it is widespread in [[temperate]] regions of the [[Northern Hemisphere]], and has been reported from Asia, Europe, North America, and South America.<ref name="Zheng 1993"/> Fruiting occurs in autumn.<ref name="Laessoe 2002"/> In China, it is known from the provinces of [[Heilongjiang]], [[Jilin]], [[Liaoning]], [[Jiangsu]], [[Guangdong]], and [[Yunnan]].<ref name="Zheng 1993"/>

==Toxicity==

''L. clypeolaria'' is considered [[Mushroom poisoning|poisonous]],<ref>{{cite book|last=Phillips|first=Roger|title=Mushrooms and Other Fungi of North America|publisher=Firefly Books|year=2010|isbn=978-1-55407-651-2|location=Buffalo, NY|page=35}}</ref> but more importantly, it resembles some of the more deadly species of its genus.<ref name="Miller2006">{{cite book|last=Miller Jr.|first=Orson K.|title=North American Mushrooms: A Field Guide to Edible and Inedible Fungi|last2=Miller|first2=Hope H.|publisher=[[FalconGuide]]|year=2006|isbn=978-0-7627-3109-1|location=Guilford, CN|pages=57}}</ref>
