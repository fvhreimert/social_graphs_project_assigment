'''''Agaricus abruptibulbus''''' is a species of [[mushroom]] in the [[genus]] ''[[Agaricus]]''. It is commonly known as the '''abruptly-bulbous agaricus'''<ref name=Roody2003/> or the '''flat-bulb mushroom'''.<ref name=McKnight1987/> First described by the [[Mycology|mycologist]] [[Charles Horton Peck]],<ref name=Peck1905/> this bulbous-stemmed [[edible mushroom|edible]] species smells slightly of [[anise]] or bitter almond,<ref name=":0">{{Cite journal |last1=Gui |first1=Yang |last2=Zhu |first2=Guo-S. |last3=Callac |first3=Philippe |last4=Hyde |first4=Kevin-D. |last5=Parra |first5=Luis-A. |last6=Chen |first6=Jie |last7=Yang |first7=Tong-J. |last8=Huang |first8=Wan-B. |last9=Gong |first9=Guang-L. |last10=Liu |first10=Zuo-Y. |date=2015-03-01 |title=Agaricus section Arvenses: three new species in highland subtropical Southwest China |url=https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1878614614001585 |journal=[[Fungal Biology]] |language=en |volume=119 |issue=2 |pages=79–94 |doi=10.1016/j.funbio.2014.10.005 |pmid=25749361 |issn=1878-6146|url-access=subscription }}</ref> and turns yellow when bruised or cut. The mushroom is medium-sized, with a white, yellow-staining cap on a slender [[stipe (mycology)|stipe]] that has a wide, flat bulb on the base.

==Taxonomy and classification==
The species was originally named ''Agaricus abruptus'' by American mycologist [[Charles Horton Peck]] in 1900.<ref name="urlMycoBank: Agaricus abruptus"/> In his 1904 publication ''Report of the State Botanist'', he changed the name to ''Agaricus abruptibulbus''. He explained that [[Elias Magnus Fries]] had earlier named a species in the [[subgenus]] ''Flammula'', which he called ''Agaricus abruptus''; the subgenus was later raised to the rank of genus, and the species was given the name ''Flammula abruptus''. Under the transitioning [[Nomenclature#Scientific nomenclature|nomenclatural]] conventions of the time, it was unclear if ''Agaricus abruptus'' would remain available for use, so Peck changed the name.<ref name=Peck1905/>

''Agaricus abruptibulbus'' belongs to ''Agaricus'' section ''Arvenses,<ref name=":0" />'' a [[cladistics|clade]] within the genus ''Agaricus.'' Along with ''A. abruptibulbus'', section ''Arvenses'' contains the species ''A.&nbsp;silvicola'', ''A.&nbsp;arvensis'', and formerly also ''[[Agaricus semotus|A.&nbsp;semotus]]''.<ref name=Mitchell1999/>

Some American authors consider this species to be [[synonym (biology)|synonymous]] with ''A.&nbsp;silvicola'',<ref name=Murrill1922/><ref name=Hotson1938/> while some in Europe have synonymized it with the similar species ''[[Agarius essettei|A.&nbsp;essettei]]''. American mycologists Steve Trudell and Joseph Ammirati noted in a 2009 field guide: "The name ''A.&nbsp;abruptibulbus'' has been applied to forms with bulbous stipe bases, but variation in stipe shape is so great that the use of this name has been largely abandoned."<ref name=Ammirati2009/>

==Description==

The [[Pileus (mycology)|cap]] is whitish in color and convex in shape, reaching up to {{convert|8|cm|in|abbr=on}} in diameter, sometimes with an [[umbo (mycology)|umbo]]. After being scratched, cut, or bruised, the cap turns yellow. The [[Stipe (mycology)|stipe]] is {{convert|8|–|12|cm|in|abbr=on}} long by {{convert|1|–|3|cm|in|abbr=on}} thick and bulbous at the base. A large, white [[annulus (mycology)|annular ring]] is present on the stipe. The [[lamella (mycology)|gill]] attachment is free, and the color is initially grayish but turns brownish after the [[spore]]s have developed. Specimens smell slightly of [[anise]]. The [[spore print]] is brown to purple-brown. Spores are elliptical in shape, and are 6–8 by 4–5&nbsp;[[micrometre|μm]]. 

The surface of the cap will stain yellow if a drop of dilute [[potassium hydroxide]] is applied.<ref name="Bessette2007" /> This species has a positive [[Chemical test in mushroom identification|Schäffer's reaction]], resulting in an orange color.<ref name=":0" />

===Similar species===
''[[Agaricus silvicola]]'' is very similar in appearance and also grows in woodlands, but it may be distinguished by the lack of an abruptly bulbous base.<ref name=Bessette2007/> ''[[Agaricus arvensis]]'' has a more robust stature, lacks the bulbous base, and grows in grassy open areas like meadows and fields. It has larger spores than ''A.&nbsp;abruptibulbus'', typically 7.0–9.2 by 4.4–5.5&nbsp;μm.<ref name=Miller2006/>

==Cadmium bioaccumulation==

''Agaricus abruptibulbus'' is known to [[bioaccumulation|bioaccumulate]] the toxic element [[cadmium]]—in other words, it absorbs cadmium faster than it loses it—so specimens collected in the wild often have higher concentrations of this element than the soil in which they are found.<ref name=Meisch1981/><ref name="urlenvironment.fi—FE17/2006"/> Furthermore, when cultivated in the laboratory, the presence of cadmium in the culture medium stimulates growth up to 100% in the presence of 0.5&nbsp;mg cadmium per liter of nutrient medium.<ref name=Meisch1979/> It is believed that the cadmium-binding ability comes from a low [[molecular weight]] metal-binding [[protein]] named cadmium-mycophosphatin.<ref name=Meisch1986/><ref name=Jablonski1981/>

==Distribution==

The fungus has been reported in [[New York (state)|New York]],<ref>{{cite journal | year = 1992 | title = Field Trip Reports | journal = Bulletin of the Torrey Botanical Club | volume = 119 | issue = 1| page = 101 }}</ref> [[Mississippi]],<ref name=Overholts1938/> [[Quebec]], [[Canada]],<ref name=Pomerleau1964/> [[Germany]],<ref name="Gerhardt1990" /> China,<ref name=":0" /> and India.<ref>{{Cite journal |last1=Kaur |first1=Munruchi |last2=Kaur |first2=Harwinder |last3=Malik |first3=Nazir Ahmad |date=2016 |title=Identification and characterization of six new taxa of genus Agaricus L.:FR. (Agaricaceae Chevall.) from India. |url=https://www.researchgate.net/publication/305770426 |journal=World Journal of Pharmacy and Pharmaceutical Sciences |volume=5 |issue=7}}</ref>
