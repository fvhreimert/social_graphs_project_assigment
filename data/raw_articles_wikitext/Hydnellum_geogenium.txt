'''''Hydnellum geogenium''''' is a species of [[tooth fungus]] in the family [[Bankeraceae]]. The fungus is found in Europe and North America, where it grows in [[coniferous forest|coniferous woods]]. It is [[edible mushroom|inedible]].<ref name="urlRogers: H.geogenium"/> [[Basidiocarp|Fruitbodies]] of the fungus contain a yellow [[biological pigment|pigment]] compound called geogenin.<ref name="Jendrny 1979"/>

==Taxonomy==

The fungus was [[species description|described]] as new to science in 1852 by [[Elias Magnus Fries]]. [[Howard James Banker]] transferred it to the genus ''[[Hydnellum]]'' in 1913.<ref name="Banker 1913"/> 

==Description==

''Hydnellum geogenium'' produces solitary or often confluent [[basidiocarp]]s with caps ([[pileus (mycology)|pilei]]) up to 10&nbsp;cm wide. The cap surface may be divided into irregular lobes of varying lengths, bearing small wart-like bumps (tuberculate) or radial furrows. Its colour ranges from lemon-yellow to sulphur-yellow or pale yellow, and in damp conditions the surface frequently exudes droplets of reddish fluid. Underneath, the [[hymenophore]] (the fertile, spore-bearing surface) consists of downward-pointing spines up to 3&nbsp;mm long, characteristic of [[tooth fungus|hydnoid fungi]]. The flesh ([[trama (mycology)|context]]) is initially yellow, darkening to brown with age, and the fruit bodies emit a mild aromatic odour and retain a gentle taste.<ref name="Ryvarden 2024"/>

The [[stipe (mycology)|stipe]] is cylindrical to tapering, reaching up to 3&nbsp;cm in height and 2&nbsp;cm in width, and shares the cap's citric yellow hue. Microscopically, the fungal tissue comprises [[hypha]]e 2–6&nbsp;[[micrometre]]s (μm) wide that bear [[clamp connections]], ensuring proper nuclear distribution during [[cell division]]. The ascospores are roughly spherical (subglobose), measuring 4–5 × 3–3.5&nbsp;μm, and feature a tuberculate surface that may aid in spore dispersal.<ref name="Ryvarden 2024"/>

==Habitat and distribution==

''Hydnellum geogenium'' is widely distributed in [[temperate]] regions of northern hemisphere, with its northern range extending to the [[Polar circle]]. It forms [[mycorrhiza]]e with ''[[Picea]]'' trees.<ref name="Ryvarden 2024"/> In Europe, the northern extent of its range is southeast Norway. It appears on the [[Regional Red List|red lists]] of Germany, Poland, and Sweden.<ref name="Gulden 1992"/> ''H.&nbsp;geogenium'' is considered [[endangered species|endangered]] in Switzerland.<ref name="Senn-Irlet 2007"/>
