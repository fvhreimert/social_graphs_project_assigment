'''''Neofavolus alveolaris''''', commonly known as the '''hexagonal-pored polypore''',<ref name="urlPolyporus alveolaris" /> is a species of [[fungus]] in the family [[Polyporaceae]]. It causes a [[white rot]] of dead [[hardwood]]s. Found on sticks and decaying logs, its distinguishing features are its yellowish to orange scaly [[pileus (mycology)|cap]], and the hexagonal or diamond-shaped pores. It is found in Eurasia and Australia; once thought to be widely distributed in North America, these specimens may belong to ''[[Neofavolus americanus]]''.

==Taxonomy==
The first scientific [[species description|description]] of the fungus was published in 1815 by [[Augustin Pyramus de Candolle]], under the name ''Merulius alveolaris''.<ref name="DeCandolle1815" /> In 1821, it was [[sanctioned name|sanctioned]] by [[Elias Magnus Fries]] as ''Cantharellus alveolaris''. It was transferred to the genus ''Polyporus'' in a 1941 publication by [[Appollinaris Semenovich Bondartsev]] and [[Rolf Singer]].<ref name="Bondartsev1941" /> It was transferred to its current genus in 2012.

The [[genus]] name is derived from the [[Greek language|Greek]] meaning "many pores", while the [[specific name (botany)|specific epithet]] ''alveolaris'' means "with small pits or hollows".<ref name="urlMushroomExpert" />

==Description==

The kidney-shaped [[Pileus (mycology)|caps]] are cream with reddish scales, measuring {{convert|1.5–6|cm|1|abbr=on|frac=2}} across and {{Convert|5-10|mm|frac=4}} thick near the base; the light flesh hardens with age.<ref name="audu">{{Cite book |last=[[Audubon]] |title=Mushrooms of North America |publisher=[[Knopf]] |year=2023 |isbn=978-0-593-31998-7 |pages=279}}</ref> The whit
[[]]
ish [[stipe (mycology)|stipe]] widens into the cap, measuring 0.5–1&nbsp;cm long and 3–7&nbsp;mm thick.<ref name="audu" /> The pores are whitish, maturing to yellow, and cover the stipe (decurrent); they are 0.5–3&nbsp;mm across and rectangular to hexagonal, being rounder near the margin.<ref name="audu" /> The [[spore print]] is white.<ref name="audu" />

===Microscopic features===
The [[Basidiospore|spores]] are narrowly elliptical and smooth, [[hyaline]], with dimensions of 11–14.5&nbsp;×&nbsp;4–5&nbsp;[[μm]]. The [[basidia]] are club-shaped and four-spored, with dimensions of 28–42&nbsp;×&nbsp;7–9&nbsp;μm.<ref name="urlEoL" />

===Similar species===
North American specimens may be ''[[Neofavolus americanus]]'', with [[DNA sequencing]] possibly the only method of distinction.<ref name="audu" />

''[[Polyporus craterellus]]'' bears a resemblance to ''P.&nbsp;alveolaris'', but the former species has a more prominent stalk and does not have the reddish-orange colors observed in the latter.<ref name="Roody2003" /> ''[[Polyporus mcmurphyi]]'' and related species have larger caps and a more defined stem.<ref name="audu" /> ''[[Favolus brasiliensis]]'' lacks orange tones and ''[[Cerioporus squamosus]]'' is larger.<ref name="audu" />

 growing on a [[twig]]]]

==Habitat and distribution==
''Neofavolus alveolaris'' is found growing singly or grouped together on branches and twigs of [[hardwood]]s, commonly on [[shagbark hickory]] in the spring and early summer.<ref name="Healy2003" /> It has been reported growing on the dead hardwoods of genera ''[[Acer (genus)|Acer]]'', ''[[Castanea (genus)|Castanea]]'', ''[[Cornus (genus)|Cornus]]'', ''[[Corylus]]'', ''[[Crataegus]]'', ''[[Erica (plant)|Erica]]'', ''[[Fagus (tree)|Fagus]]'', ''[[Fraxinus]]'', ''[[Juglans]]'', ''[[Magnolia]]'', ''[[Morus (plant)|Morus]]'', ''[[Populus]]'', ''[[Pyrus]]'', ''[[Robinia]]'', ''[[Quercus]]'', ''[[Syringa]], [[Tilia]]'', and ''[[Ulmus]]''.<ref name="Ryvarden1993" />

This species has been collected in Europe (Czechoslovakia,<ref name="Kotlaba1957" /> Italy,<ref name="Govi1970" /> and Portugal),<ref name="Melo1978" /> China,<ref name="Zhuang2001" /> and Australia.<ref name="May2003" /> It was once thought to be widely distributed in eastern North America, but these specimens may belong to ''N.&nbsp;americanus''.<ref name="urlMushroomExpert" /><ref name="Healy2003" /><ref name="audu" /> 

==Uses==

This mushroom is [[Edible mushroom|edible]] when young.<ref name="Miller 2006">{{cite book|last=Miller Jr.|first=Orson K.|title=North American Mushrooms: A Field Guide to Edible and Inedible Fungi|last2=Miller|first2=Hope H.|publisher=[[FalconGuides]]|year=2006|isbn=978-0-7627-3109-1|location=Guilford, CN|pages=418|author-link=Orson K. Miller Jr.}}</ref> It has been described as "edible but tough",<ref name="emberger" /> with toughness increasing with age, and not having "all that distinctive of a flavor".<ref name="video" /> Another reference lists the species as inedible.<ref name="bessette" />

A [[polypeptide]] with [[antifungal]] properties has been isolated from the fresh fruit bodies of this species. Named alveolarin, it inhibits the growth of the species ''[[Botrytis cinerea]]'', ''[[Fusarium oxysporum]]'', ''[[Mycosphaerella arachidicola]]'', and ''[[Physalospora piricola]]''.<ref name="Wang2004" />
