'''''Lactarius pubescens''''', commonly known as the '''downy milk cap''', is a species of [[fungus]] in the family [[Russulaceae]]. It is a medium to large [[agaric]] with a creamy-buff, hairy [[pileus (mycology)|cap]], whitish [[lamella (mycology)|gills]] and short stout [[stipe (mycology)|stem]]. The fungus has a [[cosmopolitan distribution]], and grows solitarily or in scattered groups on sandy soil under or near [[birch]].

Edibility: Ambiguous and controversial. In Russia is consumed after prolonged boiling followed by a marinating process. However it is reported to have caused gastro-intestinal upsets. Therefore, its consumption should not be recommended and this species considered toxic.

The species was first named by German botanist [[Heinrich Schrader (botanist)|Heinrich Schrader]] as ''Agaricus pubescens'' in 1794.<ref name=Schrader1794/> [[Elias Magnus Fries]] gave it its current name in 1838.<ref name=Fries1838/> The species has also been treated as a [[variety (botany)|variety]] of ''Lactarius controversus'' (as ''L.&nbsp;controversus'' var. ''pubescens'' by Gillet in 1876)<ref name=Gillet1876/> and as both a [[subspecies]] (as ''Lactarius torminosus'' subsp. ''pubescens'' by [[Paul Konrad]] and [[André Maublanc]] in 1935)<ref name=Konrad1935/> and a variety (as ''L.&nbsp;torminosus'' var. ''pubescens'' by Lundell in 1956)<ref name=Lundell1956/> of ''[[Lactarius torminosus]]''.<ref name="urlFungorum - Species synonymy"/>

''Lactarius pubescens'' is [[classification (biology)|classified]] in the [[section (biology)|section]] ''Piperites'', subsection ''Piperites''. This includes related ''Lactarius'' species characterized by having a latex that does not become yellow upon exposure to air, and which does not stain freshly cut surfaces of the fruit body yellow.<ref>Hesler & Smith, 1979, p. 237.</ref> Based on [[phylogenetic]] analysis published in 2004, ''L.&nbsp;pubescens'' is most closely related to ''[[Lactarius scoticus|L.&nbsp;scoticus]]'' and ''[[Lactarius tesquorum|L.&nbsp;tesquorum]]''.<ref name=Nuytinck2004/>

The mushroom is [[common name|commonly]] known as the "downy milkcap". The [[specific name (botany)|specific epithet]] ''pubescens'' is derived from [[Latin]], and means "becoming downy".<ref name=Schalkwijk-Barendsen1991/>

==Description==

The [[pileus (mycology)|cap]] is {{convert|2.5|–|10|cm|in|1|abbr=on}} wide, obtuse to convex, becoming broadly convex with a depressed center. The margin (cap edge) is rolled inward and bearded with coarse white hairs when young. The cap surface is dry and fibrillose except for the center, which is sticky and smooth when fresh, azonate, white to cream, becoming reddish-orange to [[wine (color)|vinaceous]] (red wine-colored) on the disc with age. The [[lamella (mycology)|gills]] are attached to slightly decurrent, crowded, seldom forked, whitish to pale yellow with pinkish tinges, slowly staining brownish ochraceous when bruised.

The [[stipe (mycology)|stem]] is {{convert|2|–|6.5|cm|in|1|abbr=on}} long, {{convert|6|–|13|mm|in|abbr=on}} thick, nearly equal or tapered downward, silky, becoming hollow with age, whitish when young, becoming ochraceous from the base up when older, apex usually tinged pinkish, often with a white basal [[mycelia|mycelium]]. The [[trama (mycology)|flesh]] is firm and whitish;<ref name="audu">{{Cite book |last=[[Audubon]] |title=Mushrooms of North America |publisher=[[Knopf]] |year=2023 |isbn=978-0-593-31998-7 |pages=180}}</ref> the odor is faintly like geraniums or sometimes pungent, and taste is acrid. The [[latex]] is white upon exposure, unchanging, not staining tissues, taste acrid. The [[spore print]] is cream with a pinkish tint. The [[edible mushroom|edibility]] of ''Lactarius pubescens'' has been described as unknown,<ref name="Bessette2009" /> [[mushroom poisoning|poisonous]],<ref>{{cite book |last=Phillips |first=Roger |title=Mushrooms and Other Fungi of North America |url=https://archive.org/details/mushroomsotherfu0000phil |url-access=registration |year=2010 |orig-year=2005 |publisher=Firefly Books |location=Buffalo, NY |isbn=978-1-55407-651-2 |page=[https://archive.org/details/mushroomsotherfu0000phil/page/112 112]}}</ref> and even edible.<ref name="Boa2004" />

The [[spore]]s are 6–8.5 by 5–6.5&nbsp;[[micrometre|μm]], elliptic, ornamented with warts and ridges that sometimes form a partial reticulum, prominences up to 1.5&nbsp;μm high, [[hyaline]] (translucent), and [[amyloid (mycology)|amyloid]]. The [[pileipellis|cap cuticle]] is a layer of thin-walled [[hypha]]e.<ref name=Bessette2009/>

===Ectomycorrhizae===
The ectomycorrhizae that ''L.&nbsp;pubescens'' forms in association with ''[[Betula pendula]]'' and ''[[Populus tremuloides]]'' has been grown in pure culture and described scientifically.<ref name=Godbout1985/><ref name=Ingleby1990/>

===Similar species===
''Lactarius scoticus'' Berk. & Broome is a small [[morphology (biology)|morphological]] mimic of ''L.&nbsp;pubescens'', growing in arctic-alpine birch.<ref name=Jahn1982/> ''L.&nbsp;pubescens'' is often mistaken for ''L.&nbsp;torminosus'' which has larger spores (7–10 by 6–8&nbsp;μm).<ref name=Arora1986/> Certain identification may require microscopic or genetic analysis.<ref name="audu" />

==Habitat and distribution==
The fruit bodies of ''L.&nbsp;pubescens'' are found scattered or in groups on the ground in wet areas under [[birch]] and other [[hardwood]]s from  August to October. The fungus is common all over temperate Europe and has been reported from eastern North America, the [[Pacific Northwest]], [[Arizona]], [[California]], [[Colorado]], [[Idaho]], and western Canada; its frequency of appearance is occasional.<ref name=Bessette2009/> It is also found in Greenland,<ref name=Knudsen1994/> and was reported for the first time in Rome, Italy, in 1997.<ref name=Perrone1997/>

==Bioactive compounds==
The marasmane [[sesquiterpenoid]] pubescenone and the [[sesquiterpene]] aldehyde lactaral have been isolated from the fruit bodies of ''L.&nbsp;pubescens''.<ref name=Shao2007/>
