'''''Discina ancilis''''', commonly known as '''pig's ears'''<ref>{{cite web|url= https://inaturalist.nz/taxa/950530-Gyromitra-ancilis |title= Pig's Ears (Gyromitra ancilis) |access-date=2021-05-21 |work= Ninaturalist.nz }}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url= https://eol.org/pages/189085 |title= Discina ancilis (Pers.) Sacc. 1889 |access-date=2021-05-21 |work= EOL }}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url= https://eol.org/pages/47393865 |title= Discina perlata |access-date=2021-05-21 |work= EOL }}</ref> is a brown to tannish, wrinkled, cup- or ear-shaped [[fungus]], sometimes with short, stout [[Stipe (mycology)|stalk]].

== Taxonomy ==
The [[spore]]s of ''D.&nbsp;ancilis'' are quite similar to those of mushrooms in the genus ''[[Gyromitra]]'', so some [[mycologist]]s classify it there.{{Citation needed|date=July 2025}}

== Description ==
The cup measures {{convert|2–10|cm|abbr=off|frac=4}} wide,<ref name=":0">{{Cite book|last1=Davis|first1=R. Michael|title=Field Guide to Mushrooms of Western North America|last2=Sommer|first2=Robert|last3=Menge|first3=John A.|publisher=[[University of California Press]]|year=2012|isbn=978-0-520-95360-4|location=Berkeley|pages=404–405|oclc=797915861}}</ref> with a disc-like, whitish exterior, and a dark brown to tan interior. The cup is often wrinkled to convoluted, with the edges turned downward. The flesh is brittle.<ref name="Arora1986">{{Cite book |last=Arora |first=David |author-link=David Arora |url=https://archive.org/details/arora-david-mushrooms-demystified-a-comprehensive-guide-to-the-fleshy-fungi-ten-speed-press-1986/page/798/mode/2up |title=Mushrooms Demystified: A Comprehensive Guide to the Fleshy Fungi |publisher=[[Ten Speed Press]] |year=1986 |isbn=978-0-89815-170-1 |edition=2nd |location=Berkeley, California |pages=798–99 |orig-date=1979}}</ref> The stipe (when present) is up to {{convert|1|cm|abbr=on|frac=8}} long and 3&nbsp;cm thick; it is whitish to brownish in color.<ref name="Arora1986" />

=== Microscopic characteristics ===
The spores are 25–35 x 11–16&nbsp;[[μm]], spindle shaped, minutely roughened, with a large central oil drop and two smaller ones at each end.<ref name="Arora1986" />

=== Similar species ===
''[[Disciotis venosa]]'' is more deeply veined, and has smooth spores; it is typically found in deciduous woods.  Other similar species of ''[[Discina (fungus)|Discina]]'' must be differentiated microscopically. ''[[Peziza repanda]]'' is also similar.<ref name=":0" /> In the [[Pacific Northwest]], ''D.&nbsp;leucoxantha'' and ''D.&nbsp;olympiana'' are similar.<ref name=":1">{{cite book|last1=Trudell|first1=Steve|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=WevHvt6Tr8kC|title=Mushrooms of the Pacific Northwest|last2=Ammirati|first2=Joe|publisher=Timber Press|year=2009|isbn=978-0-88192-935-5|series=Timber Press Field Guides|location=Portland, OR|pages=289|language=en}}</ref> It also resembles ''[[Gyromitra melaleucoides]]''.<ref name="Arora1986" />

== Distribution and habitat ==
It is found singularly or in groups, on [[humus]] or rotten wood in [[conifer]]ous areas; near melting snowbanks in western mountains. It is found in temperate areas of North America.<ref name=":1" /> It comes into fruiting from May–July.

== Edibility ==
It is considered [[Edible mushroom|edible]] by some authors,<ref name="Miller 2006">{{cite book |last1=Miller Jr. |first1=Orson K. |title=North American Mushrooms: A Field Guide to Edible and Inedible Fungi |last2=Miller |first2=Hope H. |publisher=[[FalconGuides]] |year=2006 |isbn=978-0-7627-3109-1 |location=Guilford, CN |pages=506 |author-link=Orson K. Miller Jr.}}</ref> but not others;<ref>{{cite book |last=Phillips |first=Roger |url=https://archive.org/details/mushroomsotherfu0000phil |title=Mushrooms and Other Fungi of North America |publisher=Firefly Books |year=2010 |isbn=978-1-55407-651-2 |location=Buffalo, NY |page=[https://archive.org/details/mushroomsotherfu0000phil/page/364 364] |url-access=registration}}</ref> it can be confused with other potentially toxic species.<ref name="Miller 2006" /> It should be cooked before consumption.<ref name=":0" />
