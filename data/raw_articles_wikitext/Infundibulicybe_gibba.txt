'''''Infundibulicybe gibba''''' (also known as '''''Clitocybe gibba'''''), and commonly known as the '''common funnel'''<ref name="firstNature" /> or '''funnel cap''',<ref name="Arora1986">{{Cite book |last=Arora |first=David |author-link=David Arora |url=https://archive.org/details/arora-david-mushrooms-demystified-a-comprehensive-guide-to-the-fleshy-fungi-ten-speed-press-1986/page/157/mode/2up |title=Mushrooms Demystified: A Comprehensive Guide to the Fleshy Fungi |date=1986 |publisher=[[Ten Speed Press]] |isbn=978-0-89815-170-1 |edition=2nd |location=Berkeley, CA |pages=157 |orig-date=1979}}</ref> is a species of [[Lamella (mycology)|gilled]] mushroom which is common in European woods.

==Taxonomy==
The epithet ''gibba'' comes from the Latin adjective "gibbus", meaning "humped" or "[[wikt:gibbous|gibbous]]".<ref name=Lewis1/>

This species was originally described by the mycologist [[Christiaan Hendrik Persoon]] in 1801 as ''Agaricus gibbus'', at a time when [[Lamella (mycology)|gilled]] mushrooms were generally all assigned to genus ''Agaricus''.<ref name=SF1/> In 1871, [[Paul Kummer]] allocated the species to the genus ''[[Clitocybe]]'', which previously (according to the system of [[Elias Magnus Fries]]) had only been a [[Tribe (biology)|tribe]] within genus ''Agaricus''.<ref name=Kummer/>

In 2003 Harri Harmaja created the new genus ''[[Infundibulicybe]]'' for some of the larger members of the former ''Clitocybe'' and he included ''I.&nbsp;gibba'' as the type species.<ref name=Eyssartier/><ref name=FN/><ref name=SF1/><ref name="Harmaja 2003"/>

===''Clitocybe catinus''===


''Clitocybe catinus'' is described as differing from ''C.&nbsp;gibba'' by having white cap with occasionally some pink tonality and its slight smell of flour.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.funghiitaliani.it/topic/15081-infundibulicybe-gibba/|title=Infundibulicybe gibba|website=Funghi in Italia - Fiori in Italia - Forum Micologia e Botanica|date=8 April 2016 |accessdate=23 October 2018}}</ref>

Harmaja (2003) gave it{{What|reason=Which taxa?|date=May 2025}} a new name, ''Infundibulicybe catinus''.<ref name="Harmaja 2003"/>

Vizzini et al. (2011) listed it{{What|reason=Which taxa?|date=May 2025}} a color variant of ''Infundibulicybe gibba''.<ref name="Vizzini et al 2011" />

===''Clitocybe infundibuliformis''===
The older name ''Clitocybe infundibuliformis'' is often identified as a synonym of ''I.&nbsp;gibba'',<ref name=Bon/><ref name=Courtecuisse/><ref name=FN/> but according to [[Species Fungorum]] that use was incorrect and the original ''C.&nbsp;infundibuliformis'' was a different taxon.<ref name=SF1/>

===Etymology===
The name ''infundibuliformis'' derives from the Latin "infundibulum", a funnel, with the suffix "-formis" - so it means "funnel-shaped".<ref name=Lewis2/>

== Description ==
The matt slightly felted [[Pileus (mycology)|cap]] grows from about {{Convert|3 to 9|cm|frac=4}}, and is beige to tan, also sometimes with a pink tinge.<ref name=":0">{{Cite book|last1=Davis|first1=R. Michael|title=Field Guide to Mushrooms of Western North America|last2=Sommer|first2=Robert|last3=Menge|first3=John A.|publisher=[[University of California Press]]|year=2012|isbn=978-0-520-95360-4|location=Berkeley|pages=153–154|oclc=797915861}}</ref> It may have a small depression in the centre and soon becomes funnel-shaped.<ref name="Arora1986" />

There is no ring or other veil remnant. The stem is white or whitish and about 2–8&nbsp;cm long and 1&nbsp;cm in diameter.<ref name=":0" />

The white gills are crowded and very [[decurrent]] (running down the stem).<ref name=":0" />

It has a faint "cyanic" smell, like new-mown hay, and the flavour is mild, but the central European variety ''adstringens'' has an unpleasant taste.<ref name="Courtecuisse" />

The tear-shaped spores are white<ref name=":0" /> and around 5.5–8&nbsp;[[μm]] by 4–5&nbsp;μm. The [[spore print]] is white.<ref name="Arora1986" />

=== Similar species ===
The species resembles ''[[Infundibulicybe squamulosa]]'', ''[[Pseudoclitocybe cyathiformis]]'',<ref name=":0" /> ''[[Bonomyces sinopicus]]'', and ''[[Singerocybe adirondackensis]]''.<ref name="audu">{{Cite book |last=[[Audubon]] |title=Mushrooms of North America |publisher=[[Knopf]] |year=2023 |isbn=978-0-593-31998-7 |pages=481}}</ref>

== Distribution and habitat ==
This gregarious [[saprobic]] mushroom grows on soil in deciduous or (less commonly) coniferous<ref name="Arora1986" /> woods and may be found from summer to autumn. It sometimes forms [[fairy rings]].

It is very common throughout Europe, and occurs in North America and Japan.<ref name=GBIF/><ref name=Courtecuisse/>

== Uses ==
It is [[Edible mushroom|edible]] when young, but said to be of mediocre quality. It can be fried or used in risottos or soups etc. The stems are tough and may be discarded.<ref name=ElRoyo/><ref name=Bon/><ref name=firstNature/> The species resembles some which are [[Mushroom poisoning|poisonous]].<ref name=":0" />

An [[extract]] of ''I. gibba'' exhibits inhibitory activity on [[thrombin]].<ref name="Doljak 2001"/>
