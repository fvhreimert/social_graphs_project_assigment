'''''Pleurotus pulmonarius''''', commonly known as the '''Indian oyster''', '''Italian oyster''', '''phoenix mushroom''', or the '''lung oyster''', is a mushroom very similar to ''[[Pleurotus ostreatus]]'', the pearl oyster, but with a few noticeable differences. The caps of ''P.'' ''pulmonarius'' are much paler and smaller than ''P.'' ''ostreatus'' and develop more of a stem. ''P. pulmonarius'' also prefers warmer weather than ''P.'' ''ostreatus'' and will appear later in the summer. The taste and cultivation of the two species is generally described as largely the same.<ref name="stamets00"/> Another similar species, [[North America]]'s ''[[Pleurotus populinus]]'', is restricted to growing on aspen and cottonwood.

==Natural habitat==
''Pleurotus pulmonarius'' is widespread in temperate and subtropical forests throughout the world. In the eastern United States, this species is generally found on [[hardwood]]s while in the west it can also be found on [[conifer]]s.<ref name="stamets00" />

==Taxonomy==
*1821 First published as ''Agaricus pulmonarius'' by Fr.
*1975 Disambiguated from ''Pleurotus sajor-caju'' by Pegler

==Description==
*'''[[Pileus (mycology)|Pileus]]''': 5 — 20+&nbsp;cm, [[Wikt:convex|convex]], becoming broadly convex to flat
*'''[[Lamella (mycology)|Gills]]''': Whitish; decurrent if stipe is present; small beetles may be present<ref name="amm09" />
*'''Spore print''': White, greyish, or lilac
*'''[[Stipe (mycology)|Stipe]]''': If present, short and offset from the center of the cap; bald
*'''Microscopic features''': Spores white to lavender-gray when dense, more or less cylindrical, 7.5–11 × 3–4 μm.<ref name="stamets00" />
*'''Odour''': Pleasant, like anise<ref name="amm09" />

==Cultivation==
''Pleurotus pulmonarius'' is a commonly cultivated oyster mushroom species in [[Europe]] and North America. The most popular species for cultivation are the warm-weather varieties, sometimes mistakenly marketed by spawn manufacturers and cultivators under the name "''Pleurotus sajor-caju''" (Fr.) Singer, which is in fact a separate species, and was returned to the genus ''[[Lentinus]]'' by Pegler (1975), and is now called ''[[Lentinus sajor-caju]]'' (Fr.) Fries.<ref name="stamets00" />

''Pleurotus pulmonarius'' is commercially cultivated in [[New Zealand]],<ref name="LandcareNZ" /> sold as "Oyster mushrooms".<ref name="myotopia">{{cite web|url=http://www.mytopia-mushrooms.com/products.htm|title=Mytopia Mushrooms Products|publisher=Mytopia Mushrooms|location=[[Havelock North, New Zealand]]|access-date=3 February 2012|ref=myotopia}}</ref> The archetypal oyster mushroom, ''[[Pleurotus ostreatus]]'', cannot be imported into New Zealand due to perceived risks to its forestry industry.<ref name="IanRHall" />

The cultivation of ''Pleurotus pulmonarius'' is very similar to how one would cultivate other types of ''Pleurotus'' species, like ''P. ostreatus,'' by transferring mycelium from a petri plate onto grain and then transferring the resulting "grain spawn" after the mycelium colonizes it to substrates of straw, wood chips, sawdust, cardboard, coffee grounds, and other cellulose-based substrates.

An orally administered hot water extract of ''P. pulmonarius'' had a significant [[antihyperglycemic]] effect, halted the progression of [[diabetes]], and reduced the mortality of [[alloxan]] induced diabetic mice by approximately 50%. It showed a [[synergistic]] effect with the [[antidiabetic drug]] [[glibenclamide]], supporting the possibility of effective combination therapy of glibenclamide and ''P. pulmonarius'' for diabetes.<ref name="pmid18604261">{{cite journal|last1=Badole|first1=Sachin L.|last2=Patel|first2=Naimesh M.|last3=Thakurdesai|first3=Prasad A.|last4=Bodhankar|first4=Subhash L.|title=Interaction of Aqueous Extract of ''Pleurotus pulmonarius'' (Fr.) ''Pleurotus pulmonarius'' may be effective in the treatment of [[hay fever]] by inhibiting the release of [[histamine]]. Powdered ''P. pulmonarius'' mushrooms caused a significant reduction in sneezing and nasal rubbing when fed in water to sensitized mice, although the effect gradually builds up over a matter of weeks. When they were given 500&nbsp;mg/kg a day, a significant effect was observed after two weeks, and it was four weeks before a significant change was observed at 200&nbsp;mg/kg.<ref name="pmid17666820">{{cite journal |author1=Yatsuzuka, Rie |author2=Nakano, Yoshiyuki |author3=Jiang, Shuishi |author4=Ueda, Yuhki |author5=Kishi, Yuko |author6=Suzuki, Yuh |author7=Yokota, Emiko |author8=Rahman, Ashequr |author9=Ono, Rie Kohno, Isato |author10=Kamei, Chiaki |title=Effect of Usuhiratake (''Pleurotus pulmonarius'') on Sneezing and Nasal Rubbing in BALB/c Mice |journal=Biological and Pharmaceutical Bulletin |year=2007 |volume=30 |issue=8 |pages=1557–1560 |pmid=17666820 |doi=10.1248/bpb.30.1557 |issn=1347-5215 |id=[[International Standard Serial Number|Print ISSN]]: [http://www.worldcat.org/issn/0918-6158 0918-6158] |doi-access=free }}</ref>

Extracts of ''P. pulmonarius'' attenuated the development of [[colitis|acute colitis]] in a [[mouse model]], suggesting a possible clinical use in the treatment of colitis.<ref name="pmid19772681">{{cite journal|first1=Iris|last1=Lavi|first2=Dana|last2=Levinson|first3=Irena|last3=Peri|first4=Lili|last4=Nimri|first5=Yitzhak|last5=Hadar|first6=Betty|last6=Schwartz|title=Orally administered glucans from the edible mushroom ''Pleurotus pulmonarius'' reduce acute inflammation in dextran sulfate sodium-induced experimental colitis|journal=British Journal of Nutrition|date=February 2010|volume=103|issue=3|pages=393–402|pmid=19772681|doi=10.1017/S0007114509991760|doi-access=free}}</ref> A further study by the same authors concluded that the extracts also inhibit [[Colon (anatomy)|colon]] [[carcinogenesis|cancer formation]] associated with colitis in mice.<ref name="pmid22187166">{{cite journal|last1=Lavi|first1=Iris|last2=Nimri|first2=Lili|last3=Levinson|first3=Dana|last4=Peri|first4=Irena|last5=Hadar|first5=Yitzhak|last6=Schwartz|first6=Betty|title=Glucans from the edible mushroom ''Pleurotus pulmonarius'' inhibit colitis-associated colon carcinogenesis in mice|journal=Journal of Gastroenterology|date=December 21, 2011|pmid=22187166|doi=10.1007/s00535-011-0514-7|volume=47|issue=5|pages=504–18|s2cid=23013924}}</ref>

==Similar species==
''Pleurotus ostreatus'' is very similar, as is [[North America]]'s ''[[Pleurotus populinus]]'', which is restricted to growing on aspen and cottonwood (genus ''[[Populus]]'').<ref name="amm09" /> It may resemble a [[Clitocybe]], some of which are poisonous, when growing on the top of wood.<ref name="amm09" />
