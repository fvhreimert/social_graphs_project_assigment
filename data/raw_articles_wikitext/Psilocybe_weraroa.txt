'''''Psilocybe weraroa''''', formerly '''''Weraroa novae-zelandiae''''', is a [[secotioid]] fungus in the family ''[[Hymenogastraceae]]''.<ref>{{Cite web|date=2011|title=Index Fungorum - Names Record|url=https://www.indexfungorum.org/Names/NamesRecord.asp?RecordID=515728|url-status=live|access-date=2 October 2021|website=Index Fungorum|archive-date=1 October 2021|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20211001132817/https://www.indexfungorum.org/Names/NamesRecord.asp?RecordID=515728}}</ref> It is [[Endemism|endemic]] to New Zealand, where it grows in native forests from rotting wood and woody debris.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|title=Psilocybe weraroa Borovička, Oborník & Noordel.|url=https://www.nzor.org.nz/names/bfbbe483-5120-4425-b8a5-a3507d26f43a|url-status=live|access-date=2 October 2021|website=NZOR|archive-date=1 October 2021|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20211001131207/https://www.nzor.org.nz/names/bfbbe483-5120-4425-b8a5-a3507d26f43a}}</ref> Despite its pouch-like form this species is closely related to ''[[Psilocybe cyanescens]]'' and ''[[Psilocybe subaeruginosa]]''. <ref name="Borovicka2011" /> As a bluing member of the genus ''[[Psilocybe]]'' it contains the psychoactive compounds [[psilocin]] and [[psilocybin]].<ref name="Bridge2008" /> It has been cultivated commercially by Rua Bioscience with a view to researching potential medical applications, bringing together its traditional uses in [[Rongoā Māori]] with the recent interest in Western medicine into the medical applications of psilocin and psilocybin.<ref>[https://drugfoundation.org.nz/articles/the-tairawhiti-hapu-exploring-indigenous-psilocybin-to-treat-methamphetamine-addiction The Tairāwhiti hapū exploring indigenous psilocybin to treat methamphetamine addiction. ''New Zealand Drug Foundation'', 24 May 2023]</ref><ref>[https://mugglehead.com/maori-group-receives-first-psilocybin-cultivation-license-in-nz-to-treat-substance-use-disorders/ Dunne R. Māori group receives first psilocybin cultivation license in NZ to treat substance use disorders. ''Mugglehead Magazine'' 30 October 2023]</ref><ref>{{cite web | url = https://www.nzherald.co.nz/kahu/magic-mushroom-trialled-as-rongoa-to-treat-addiction-and-mental-health-issues/J7YVYQKFXVFZTJYPQCBRV7I7CI/ | vauthors = Hill N | title = Magic mushroom trialled as rongoa to treat addiction and mental health issues. | work = New Zealand Herald | date = 24 April 2024 }}</ref>

== Taxonomy and naming ==
The species was first described in the literature in 1924 by the New Zealand-based mycologist [[Gordon Heriot Cunningham]], under the name ''Secotium novae-zelandiae''.<ref name=Cunningham1924/> [[Rolf Singer]] transferred it to the genus ''[[Weraroa]]'' in 1958.<ref name=Singer1958/> [[Phylogenetic]] analysis by Moncalvo (2002)<ref name=Moncalvo2002/> and Bridge et al. (2008)<ref name=Bridge2008/> has demonstrated the close relationship between ''Weraroa novae-zelandiae'' and the hallucinogenic blue-staining group of ''Psilocybe'', particularly ''[[Psilocybe subaeruginosa]]''. [[molecular phylogenetics|Phylogenetic]] analysis published by Borovička and colleagues (2011) showed this species is very close to ''[[Psilocybe cyanescens]]''. Given this and the apparently distant relation with other species of ''[[Weraroa]]''  Borovička et al. (2011) suggested renaming the species ''Psilocybe weraroa''.<ref name=Borovicka2011/>

=== Etymology ===
The [[specific name (botany)|species epithet]] ''weraroa'' is taken from the former generic name, which refers to the [[Type locality (biology)|type locality]]. The binomial ''Psilocybe novae-zelandiae'' could not be used, as it had been applied to another species in 1978 by [[Gastón Guzmán]] and [[Egon Horak]] (now ''[[Deconica novae-zelandiae]]'').<ref name=Guzman1979/><ref name=Redhead2007/>

[[Stipe (mycology)|Stipe]] up to {{cvt|40|mm}} long by {{cvt|6|mm}} in diameter, slender, equal in width, whitish to french-grey, bruising blue or greenish with damage, yellowish-brown at the base, initially fibrillose but becoming smooth, polished and cartilaginous in age excluding at the base, hollow, flesh yellow-orange, thickening at the internal apex. [[Gleba]] sepia-brown to chocolate-brown, cellular, coarsely shaped, often elongated, laterally compressed, sparse, chambered and gill-like. [[Basidiospore|Spores]] 11–15(17) x 5–8 μm in size, smooth, [[sepia (color)|sepia]]-coloured to purple-brown, elliptic-ovate or elliptical in shape, rounded at one end with a thin [[epispore]].<ref name="Cunningham1924" />

==Habitat and distribution==

The species is endemic to New Zealand,<ref name=":0" /> and is fairly abundant in the early winter and spring months in lowland mixed rainforest near [[Wellington]] and [[Auckland]]. The mushroom can be difficult to see, often buried under leaves or eaten by slugs, and it is sometimes hard to find mature specimens that are not partially eaten.<ref name="Cunningham1924" /> The species is typically found on decaying wood buried in forest leaf litter (either solitary or crowded), often on the rotting branches of ''[[Melicytus ramiflorus]]''. It has also been found fruiting on rotted cabbage trees and associated with decaying tree-fern fronds, native to the forests of New Zealand.<ref name="Cunningham1924" />

== Similar species ==


''[[Clavogaster virescens]]'' is similar in appearance and habitat, but the gleba form a reddish brown chambered mass enclosed inside a sack-like structure within the peridium. The stem is stout, smooth and slippery rather than fibrous, off-white to yellow, enlarged at the top where it often smoothly transitions into the pouch, and tapering towards a yellower base. The fungus does not have a bluing reaction; it is naturally blue to greenish blue, and has no psychoactive properties.<ref name="Cunningham1924" />

== Ecology ==

A hypothesis for the species' development of pouches is that it evolved as a strategy to be eaten by the flightless extinct [[moa]], disguising the fruitbodies of the fungus as fallen berries, in order for the spores to be disbursed into new areas.<ref name="ForgottenForest">{{cite q|Q135894239|pp=31-33}}</ref>
