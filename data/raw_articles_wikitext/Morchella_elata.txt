'''''Morchella elata''''' is a species of [[fungus]] in the family [[Morchellaceae]]. It is one of many related species [[common name|commonly]] known as '''black morels''', and until 2012 the name ''M.&nbsp;elata'' was broadly applied to black morels throughout the globe.<ref name=odon11/><ref name=Kuo2012/>  Like most members of the genus, ''M.&nbsp;elata'' is a popular [[edible mushroom|edible fungus]] and is sought by many [[mushroom hunter]]s.

==Taxonomy==
The fruit bodies of ''[[Morchella]]'' species, including ''M.&nbsp;elata'', are highly [[Polymorphism (biology)|polymorphic]] in appearance, exhibiting variations in shape, color and size; this has contributed to uncertainties regarding taxonomy.<ref name=masa10/> Discriminating between the various species is complicated by uncertainty regarding which species are truly [[species problem|biologically distinct]]. [[Mushroom hunter]]s refer to them by their color as the species are very similar in appearance and vary considerably within species and age of individual.

Early [[phylogenetic]] analyses supported the hypothesis that the genus comprises only a few species with considerable phenotypic variation.<ref name="Ref_f"/><ref name="Ref_g"/>  More recent DNA work, however, has revealed more than a dozen distinct groups of morels in North America,<ref name=mdcp/> and over 60 worldwide.<ref name=du12/>  An extensive DNA study showed three discrete [[clade]]s, or genetic groups, consisting of the black morels (''M.&nbsp;elata'' and others), the yellow morels (''[[Morchella esculenta|M.&nbsp;esculenta]]'' and others), and the white morels (''[[Morchella rufobrunnea|M.&nbsp;rufobrunnea]]'' and ''[[Morchella anatolica|M.&nbsp;anatolica]]''). Species within the Elata clade (also referred to as [[Distantes]]), are characterised by the gradual darkening of their sterile ridges upon maturity, with the exception of ''[[Morchella tridentina|M.&nbsp;tridentina]]'' (=''[[Morchella frustrata|M.&nbsp;frustrata]]''), which has persistently pale ridges.<ref>{{cite journal | last1 = Loizides | first1 = Michael | last2 = Alvarado | first2 = Pablo | last3 = Clowez | first3 = Philippe | last4 = Moreau | first4 = Pierre-Arthur | last5 = de la Osa | first5 = Luis Romero | last6 = Palazón | first6 = Antonio | year = 2015 | title = Morchella tridentina, M. rufobrunnea, and M. kakiicolor: a study of three poorly known Mediterranean morels, with nomenclatural updates in section Distantes | journal = Mycol Progress | volume =  14| issue = 3| page = 13 | doi = 10.1007/s11557-015-1030-6 | bibcode = 2015MycPr..14...13L | s2cid = 16132175 }}</ref> Within the black and yellow clades, there are dozens of individual species, many [[endemic]] to individual continents or regions.<ref name=odon11/> This species-rich view is supported by studies in North America,<ref name=odon11/> [[Western Europe]],<ref name=kell05/> [[Turkey]],<ref name=task10/><ref name=taskin12/> [[Israel]],<ref name=mas09/> the [[Himalayas]],<ref name=kan10/> and [[China]].<ref name=du12/>

The scientific name ''Morchella elata'' was proposed by [[Elias Magnus Fries]] from Sweden in 1822.<ref name=Fries1822/>  DNA analysis in 2011 has shown North American black morels to be largely distinct from European species,<ref name=odon11/> therefore restricting the use of the ''M. elata'' name to Europe. In 2012, Kuo et al. provided names for many of the North American black morels that may have been referred to as ''M. elata'' in the past.<ref name=Kuo2012/> However, in a subsequent study by Richard et al. (2014) proposing a unified taxonomy for the genus, it is suggested that Fries' original description of ''Morchella elata'' may correspond to phylogenetic lineage ''Mel''-10, which has been since described from North America as ''[[Morchella importuna]]'', but later shown to have a widespread transcontinental distribution.

The variety ''M. elata'' var. ''purpurescens'', characterised by the purple- or pink-colored tinges of its ascocarps,<ref name="rogers"/> is now considered to belong to a phylogenetically distinct lineage (Richard et al. 2014).

==Description==

 viewed with [[phase contrast microscopy]]]]

''Morchella elata'' has operculate asci (i.e., asci opening by an apical lid to discharge the spores). It further has unicellular [[hyaline]] ascospores with polar oil droplets.<ref name=pl98/>

Despite the fact that many interpretations of ''M. elata'' exist throughout the years, most authors agree that Fries' original concept refers to a species with dark, conical ascocarps and more or less parallel vertical ridges, with horizontal interconnecting ridges arranged in a "ladderlike" pattern.<ref>Boudier E (1897) Révision analytique des morilles de France. ==Ecology and distribution==


''Morchella elata'' fruits during spring on soil. Fries' original description, which was based on a collection from a fir wood in Sweden, reported it as rare: "''In silvis abiegnis, praecipue locis humidis adustis, raro''". However, since the true identity of ''M. elata'' is not yet fully clarified, its exact ecological preferences and distribution remain unclear.

==Toxicity==
Morels may contain small amounts of [[monomethylhydrazine]]<ref name="Ref_k"/> (the presence of hydrazine based mycotoxins is controversial since there are no primary references of these substances having been detected in the genus ''Morchella'') or more likely an unknown toxin that is destroyed through cooking, so morel mushrooms should never be eaten raw.<ref name="Ref_l"/> It has been reported that even cooked morels can sometimes cause mild adverse effects when consumed with alcohol.<ref name="Ref_m"/>

==Uses==

This is an [[edible mushroom|edible]] species, although like other morels, some individuals may be allergic to it, so it must be cooked before being eaten.<ref>{{cite book |last=Phillips |first=Roger |title=Mushrooms and Other Fungi of North America |url=https://archive.org/details/mushroomsotherfu0000phil |url-access=registration |year=2010 |publisher=Firefly Books |location=Buffalo, NY |isbn=978-1-55407-651-2 |page=[https://archive.org/details/mushroomsotherfu0000phil/page/361 361]}}</ref> It can also be dried for later use.<ref name="Miller 2006">{{cite book|last1=Miller Jr.|first1=Orson K.|title=North American Mushrooms: A Field Guide to Edible and Inedible Fungi|last2=Miller|first2=Hope H.|publisher=[[FalconGuides]]|year=2006|isbn=978-0-7627-3109-1|location=Guilford, CN|pages=503|author-link=Orson K. Miller Jr.}}</ref> However, according to one field guide it may cause gastrointestinal disorder, and cannot easily be identified without a microscope.<ref>{{cite book|last=Whitney|first=Stephen|url=https://archive.org/details/westernforests00whit/page/497|title=Western Forests (The Audubon Society Nature Guides)|date=1985|publisher=Knopf|isbn=0-394-73127-1|location=New York|pages=497–98}}</ref>
