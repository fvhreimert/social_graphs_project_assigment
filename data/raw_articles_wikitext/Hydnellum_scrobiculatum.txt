'''''Hydnellum scrobiculatum''''', [[common name|commonly]] known as the '''rough hydnellum'''<ref name="Arora 1986"/> or '''ridged tooth''', is a [[tooth fungus]] in the family [[Bankeraceae]]. Widely distributed in the [[Northern Hemisphere]], it is found in Eurasia and North America.

==Taxonomy==
The fungus was first [[species description|described]] as a species of ''[[Hydnum]]'' by [[Elias Magnus Fries]] in his 1815 work ''Observationes mycologicae''.<ref name="Fries 1815"/> [[Petter Karsten]] transferred it to the genus ''[[Hydnellum]]'' in 1879.<ref name="Karsten 1879"/> Canadian mycologist Kenneth A. Harrison published the [[variety (botany)|variety]] ''H.&nbsp;scrobiculatum'' var. ''zonatum'' as a [[new combination]] in 1961,<ref name="Harrison 1961"/> but this is now considered to be [[synonym (taxonomy)|synonymous]] with ''[[Hydnellum concrescens]]''.<ref name="urlFungorum: Hydnellum scrobiculatum var. zonatum"/> [[Common name]]s given to the species include "pitted corky spine fungus",<ref name="Pegler 1997"/> and the [[British Mycological Society]] recommended name "ridged tooth".<ref name="BMS"/>

==Description==
The [[Basidiocarp|fruitbodies]] have centrally depressed [[pileus (mycology)|caps]] measuring {{convert|2–6|cm|1|abbr=on}} in diameter. These caps can fuse together to form concrescent fruitbodies. The caps have wavy edges, and an initially felty surface that becomes scaly with age. Young fruit bodies are white, then pinkish-brown, then purplish brown, sometimes with white margins.<ref name="Ellis 1990"/> The fruitbodies can become shiny in age.<ref name="Phillips 2013"/>

The spines on the cap underside are up to 4&nbsp;mm long. They are initially white, but become purplish brown in maturity.<ref name="Ellis 1990"/> The spines are [[decurrent]]—they run down the length of the stipe.<ref name="Huffman 2008"/> The [[stipe (mycology)|stipe]], which is roughly the same color as the cap, measures {{convert|2–3|cm|1|abbr=on}} long by {{convert|1–1.5|cm|1|abbr=on}} thick.<ref name="Ellis 1990"/> [[Mycelium]] at the base of the stipe envelops and grows around forest litter.<ref name="Huffman 2008"/> The [[trama (mycology)|flesh]] smells mealy (similar to freshly ground flour).<ref name="Pegler 1997"/> The [[edible mushroom|edibility]] of the fruitbody was previously unknown, but Roger Phillips calls it "poor".<ref>{{cite book |last=Phillips |first=Roger |title=Mushrooms and Other Fungi of North America |year=2010 |publisher=Firefly Books |location=Buffalo, NY |isbn=978-1-55407-651-2 |page=323}}</ref> In general, ''Hydnellum'' species are too acrid and woody to be palatable.<ref name="Arora 1986"/>

Like all ''Hydnellum'' species, ''H.&nbsp;scrobiculatum'' produces a brown [[spore print]].<ref name="Phillips 2013"/> Individual [[basidiospore|spores]] have a more or less spherical shape, with dimensions of 5.5–6.5 by 4–5.6&nbsp;[[μm]]. They have wart-like projections (tubercles) on the surface. The [[basidia]] (spore-bearing cells) are narrowly club-shaped, four-spored, and measure 23–29 by 5–6.5&nbsp;μm.<ref name="Pegler 1997"/>

===Similar species===
There are several species that ''Hydnellum scrobiculatum'' is often confused with&mdash;especially older specimens. These include ''[[Hydnellum spongiosipes|H.&nbsp;spongiosipes]]'', ''[[Hydnellum ferrugineum|H.&nbsp;ferrugineum]]'', and ''[[Hydnellum concrescens|H.&nbsp;concrescens]]''.<ref name="Polèse 2002"/> The latter species is particularly similar to ''H.&nbsp;scrobiculatum'', a fact that has been highlighted by conservation efforts in the UK.<ref name="Parfitt 2007"/><ref name="Ainsworth 2010"/>

==Habitat and distribution==
''Hydnellum scrobiculatum'' fruits singly, in clusters, or in fused groups in both [[conifer]]ous and [[mixed forest]]s. It is found in Europe, Asia, and North America.<ref name="Pegler 1997"/> In the United Kingdom, it is considered [[vulnerable species|vulnerable]] according to the [[biodiversity action plan]] for stiped hydnoid fungi.<ref name="ENRR 2004"/>
