'''''Pleurotus ostreatus''''' (commonly known the '''oyster mushroom''',<ref name="Arora1986">{{Cite book |last=Arora |first=David |author-link=David Arora |url=https://archive.org/details/arora-david-mushrooms-demystified-a-comprehensive-guide-to-the-fleshy-fungi-ten-speed-press-1986/page/134/mode/2up |title=Mushrooms Demystified: A Comprehensive Guide to the Fleshy Fungi |date=1986 |publisher=[[Ten Speed Press]] |isbn=978-0-89815-170-1 |edition=2nd |location=Berkeley, CA |pages=134–135 |orig-date=1979}}</ref> '''grey oyster mushroom''',<ref name="IanRHall">{{cite journal |last=Hall |first=Ian R. |date=April 2010 |title=Growing mushrooms: the commercial reality |url=http://www.trufflesandmushrooms.co.nz/April%20Lifestyle%20Farmer.pdf |journal=Lifestyle Farmer |pages=42–45 |access-date=26 January 2012}}</ref> '''oyster fungus''', '''hiratake''', or '''pearl oyster mushroom''').  Found in temperate and subtropical forests around the world, it is a popular [[edible mushroom]].

== Name ==
Both the [[Latin]] and [[common names]] refer to the shape of the fruiting body.<ref name="oxford">{{cite book |last=Davidson |first=Alan |title=The Oxford Companion to Food |last2=Jaine |first2=Tom |date=2014 |publisher=Oxford University Press |isbn=978-0-19-967733-7 |editor1-last=Jaine |editor1-first=Tom |edition=3rd |chapter=Oyster mushroom |doi=10.1093/acref/9780199677337.001.0001 |chapter-url=https://www.oxfordreference.com/view/10.1093/acref/9780199677337.001.0001/acref-9780199677337-e-1735}}</ref> The Latin ''pleurotus'' (side-ear) refers to the sideways growth of the stem with respect to the cap, while the Latin ''ostreatus'' (and the English common name, [[oyster]]) refers to the shape of the cap which resembles the bivalve of the same name.<ref name="oxford" /> The oyster reference may also derive from the slippery texture of the mushroom.<ref name="oxford" />

== Description ==
The [[Pileus (mycology)|cap]] is broad, fan or oyster-shaped, and {{Convert|2–30|cm|abbr=off|frac=2}} wide.<ref name="Miller 2006">{{cite book |last1=Miller Jr. |first1=Orson K. |author-link=Orson K. Miller Jr. |title=North American Mushrooms: A Field Guide to Edible and Inedible Fungi |last2=Miller |first2=Hope H. |publisher=[[FalconGuide]] |year=2006 |isbn=978-0-7627-3109-1 |location=Guilford, CN |page=142}}</ref> In the wild, it ranges from white to gray or brown; the margin is inrolled when young, smooth and often somewhat lobed or wavy. The [[Trama (mycology)|flesh]] is white, firm, and varies in thickness due to [[Stipe (mycology)|stipe]] arrangement. The stipe, if present, is up to {{Convert|4|cm|abbr=on|frac=4}} long and thick,<ref name="Arora1986" /> off-center and attaching laterally to wood. The [[Lamella (mycology)|gills]] are whitish, sometimes becoming yellowish, and decurrent if a stalk is present.<ref name="Arora1986" /> The [[spore print]] is white to lilac-gray, and best viewed on a dark background. The mushroom has the bittersweet aroma of [[benzaldehyde]] or bitter almonds.<ref name="OysterAlde">{{cite journal |doi=10.1021/jf960876i |title=Volatile Compounds Secreted by the Oyster Mushroom (Pleurotus ostreatus) and Their Antibacterial Activities |year=1997 |last1=Beltran-Garcia |first1=Miguel J. |last2=Estarron-Espinosa |first2=Mirna |last3=Ogura |first3=Tetsuya |journal=Journal of Agricultural and Food Chemistry |volume=45 |page=4049 |issue=10}}</ref>




=== Similar species ===
It is related to the similarly cultivated ''[[Pleurotus eryngii]]'' (king oyster mushroom).{{Citation needed|date=May 2025|reason=Why is this one species mentioned?}} Other similar species include ''[[Pleurotus columbinus|P.&nbsp;columbinus]]'', ''[[Pleurotus cornucopiae|P.&nbsp;cornucopiae]]'',<ref name="Arora1986" /> ''[[Hohenbuehelia petaloides]]'', the hairy-capped ''[[Phyllotopsis nidulans]]'', and the smaller ''[[Pleurocybella porrigens]]''.<ref>{{Cite book |last1=Davis |first1=R. Michael |title=Field Guide to Mushrooms of Western North America |last2=Sommer |first2=Robert |last3=Menge |first3=John A. |publisher=[[University of California Press]] |year=2012 |isbn=978-0-520-95360-4 |location=Berkeley |pages=132–3 |oclc=797915861}}</ref>

''[[Omphalotus nidiformis]]'' is a toxic lookalike found in Australia. In North America, potential lookalikes include the toxic [[muscarine]]-containing [[Omphalotus olivascens|''O.&nbsp;olivascens'']] (the western jack-o'-lantern mushroom) and ''[[Clitocybe dealbata]]'' (the ivory funnel mushroom). Some toxic ''[[Lentinellus]]'' species are similar in appearance, but have gills with jagged edges and finely haired caps.<ref>{{Cite book |last1=Davis |first1=R. Michael |title=Field Guide to Mushrooms of Western North America |last2=Sommer |first2=Robert |last3=Menge |first3=John A. |publisher=[[University of California Press]] |year=2012|isbn=978-0-520-95360-4 |location=Berkeley |page=30 |oclc=797915861}}</ref>

== Distribution and habitat ==
The oyster mushroom is widespread in many temperate and subtropical forests throughout the world, although it is absent from the [[Pacific Northwest]] of [[North America]], being replaced by ''[[Pleurotus pulmonarius|P.&nbsp;pulmonarius]]'' and ''[[Pleurotus populinus|P.&nbsp;populinus]]''.<ref name=amm09/> It is a [[saprotroph]] that acts as a primary decomposer of wood, especially deciduous trees, and beech trees in particular.<ref name="Phillips">{{cite book |last=Phillips |first=Roger |date=2006 |title=Mushrooms |isbn=0-330-44237-6 |page=266}}</ref> It is a [[wood-decay fungus#White rot|white-rot wood-decay]] fungus.

The oyster mushroom can be found in many habitats, year round in the [[United Kingdom]], while some related species (e.g. the branched oyster mushroom) grow only on trees.<ref name="tfb">{{Cite book |last=Francis-Baker |first=Tiffany |title=Concise Foraging Guide |date=2021 |publisher=[[Bloomsbury Publishing|Bloomsbury]] |isbn=978-1-4729-8474-6 |series=[[The Wildlife Trusts]] |location=London |page=129}}</ref>

== Ecology ==
While this mushroom is often seen growing on dying hardwood trees, it only appears to be acting [[saprophytically]], rather than parasitically. As the tree dies of other causes, ''P.&nbsp;ostreatus'' grows on the rapidly increasing mass of dead and dying wood. They actually benefit the forest by decomposing the dead wood, returning vital elements and minerals to the ecosystem in a form usable to other plants and organisms.<ref name="oxford" /> Additionally, oyster mushrooms [[bioaccumulate]] [[lithium]].<ref name="pmid23107736"> {{cite journal |last1=de Assunção |first1=Laélia Soares |last2=da Luz |first2=José Maria Rodrigues |last3=da Silva |first3=Marliane de Cássia Soares |last4=Vieira |first4=Patrícia Aparecida Fontes |last5=Bazzolli |first5=Denise Mara Soares |last6=Vanetti |first6=Maria Cristina Dantas |last7=Kasuya |first7=Maria Catarina Megumi |year=2012 |title=Enrichment of mushrooms: An interesting strategy for the acquisition of lithium |journal=Food Chemistry |publisher=Elsevier BV |volume=134 |issue=2 |pages=1123–7 |doi=10.1016/j.foodchem.2012.03.044 |issn=0308-8146 |pmid=23107736 |doi-access=free}}</ref>

Predatory behavior on nematodes has evolved independently in all major fungal lineages.<ref name="Lee">{{cite journal |last1=Lee |first1=Ching-Han |last2=Chang |first2=Han-Wen |last3=Yang |first3=Ching-Ting |last4=Wali |first4=Niaz |last5=Shie |first5=Jiun-Jie |last6=Hsueh |first6=Yen-Ping |title=Sensory cilia as the Achilles heel of nematodes when attacked by carnivorous mushrooms |journal=Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences |volume=117 |issue=11 |date=2020-03-02 |doi=10.1073/pnas.1918473117 |pages=6014–6022 |pmid=32123065 |pmc=7084146 |bibcode=2020PNAS..117.6014L |doi-access=free }}</ref> ''P.&nbsp;ostreatus'' is one of at least 700 known [[Nematophagous fungus|nematophagous]] mushrooms.<ref>{{cite journal |last1=Soares |first1=Filippe |title=Nematophagous fungi: Far beyond the endoparasite, predator and ovicidal groups |journal=Agriculture and Natural ResourcesAgriculture and Natural Resources |date=5 June 2018 |volume=52 |pages=1-8 |url=https://doi.org/10.1016/j.anres.2018.05.010 |access-date=10 November 2024}}</ref>  Its [[mycelia]] can kill and digest [[nematode]]s, which is believed to be a way in which the mushroom obtains [[nitrogen]].<ref name="Lee" />

== Uses ==
Commercial [[fungiculture|cultivation]] of this mushroom first began in Germany as a subsistence measure during [[World War&nbsp;I]],<ref>{{cite journal |vauthors=Eger G, Eden G, Wissig E |title=Pleurotus Ostreatus - breeding potential of a new cultivated mushroom |journal=Theor Appl Genet |volume=47 |issue=4 |pages=155–63 |date=July 1976 |pmid=24414617 |doi=10.1007/BF00278373 }}</ref> and it is now grown commercially around the world for food.

=== Culinary ===
The oyster mushroom is a choice [[Edible mushroom|edible]].<ref name="Miller 2006" /> Oyster mushrooms are used in [[Czech cuisine|Czech]], [[Polish cuisine|Polish]], and [[Slovak cuisine|Slovak]] contemporary cuisine in soups and stews in a similar fashion to meat, as well as breaded to become a vegetarian alternative to the [[Kotlet schabowy|''kotlet'']] in Polish dishes.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://dobruchut.azet.sk/hliva-ustricova |title=Slovak oyster mushroom recipes |publisher=Ringier Axel Springer SK |access-date=2015-07-21}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.przepisy.pl/blog/jak-przyrzadzic-boczniaki-jak-dlugo-gotowac-lub-smazyc-ten-rodzaj-grzybow |publisher=przepisy.pl |title=Jak przyrządzić boczniaki? Jak długo gotować lub smażyć ten rodzaj grzybów? |quote=Wyśmienicie smakują w wersji zarówno gotowanej, jak i smażonej. Można dorzucić je zup i gulaszów lub zamienić w wegetariański kotlet w chrupiącej panierce. |access-date=2024-01-19 |date=2021-11-15}}</ref> It is a [[delicacy]] in [[Japanese cuisine|Japanese]], [[Korean cuisine|Korean]] and [[Chinese cuisine]].{{Citation needed|date=January 2024}} It is frequently served on its own, in soups, stuffed, or in [[stir-fry]] recipes with [[soy sauce]]. Oyster mushrooms may be used in sauces, such as [[Oyster sauce#Vegetarian oyster sauce|vegetarian oyster sauce]]. The mushroom's taste has been described as mild with a slight odor similar to [[anise]]. It is best when picked young; as the mushroom ages, the flesh becomes tough and the flavor becomes acrid.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.forestwildlife.org/how-to-harvest-oyster-mushrooms/ |title=How To Harvest Oyster Mushrooms? |date=16 August 2022 |publisher=Forest Wildlife |access-date=2022-08-16}}</ref>

=== Other uses ===
The pearl oyster mushroom is also used to create mycelium bricks, [[MycoWorks#Technology|mycelium furniture]], and leather-like products.{{citation needed|date=June 2019}} 

Oyster mushrooms can also be used industrially for [[mycoremediation]] purposes. Oyster mushrooms were used to treat soil that had been polluted with diesel oil. The mushroom was able to convert 95% of the oil into non-toxic compounds.<ref>{{Cite journal |last=Rhodes |first=Christopher J. |date=January 2014 |title=Mycoremediation (bioremediation with fungi) – growing mushrooms to clean the earth |journal=Chemical Speciation & Bioavailability |language=en |volume=26 |issue=3 |pages=196–8 |doi=10.3184/095422914X14047407349335 |s2cid=97081821 |issn=0954-2299 |doi-access=free}}</ref> ''P.&nbsp;ostreatus'' is also capable of growing upon and degrading [[oxo-biodegradable]] plastic bags;<ref>{{cite journal |vauthors=da Luz JM, Paes SA, Nunes MD, da Silva Mde C, Kasuya MC |title=Degradation of oxo-biodegradable plastic by Pleurotus ostreatus |journal=PLoS One |volume=8 |issue=8 |article-number=e69386 |date=2013 |pmid=23967057 |pmc=3744528 |doi=10.1371/journal.pone.0069386 |doi-access=free}}</ref> it can also contribute to the degradation of [[renewable polyethylene]].<ref>{{cite journal |vauthors=da Luz JM, Paes SA, Ribeiro KV, Mendes IR, Kasuya MC |title=Degradation of Green Polyethylene by Pleurotus ostreatus |journal=PLoS One |volume=10 |issue=6 |article-number=e0126047 |date=2015 |pmid=26076188 |pmc=4468114 |doi=10.1371/journal.pone.0126047 |doi-access=free}}</ref>

The species has been found to contain the cholesterol-lowering drug [[lovastatin]].<ref name="pmid7614366">{{cite journal |last1=Gunde-Cimerman |first1=Nina |last2=Cimerman |first2=Aleksa |date=March 1995 |title=''Pleurotus'' Fruiting Bodies Contain the Inhibitor of 3-Hydroxy-3-Methylglutaryl-Coenzyme A Reductase—Lovastatin |journal=Experimental Mycology |volume=19 |issue=1 |pages=1–6 |doi=10.1006/emyc.1995.1001 |pmid=7614366}}</ref>
