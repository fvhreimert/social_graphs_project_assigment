'''''Caloscypha''''' is a fungal [[genus]] in the family [[Caloscyphaceae]] (order [[Pezizales]]). A [[monotypic]] genus, it contains the single species '''''Caloscypha fulgens''''', commonly known as the '''snowbank orange peel fungus''',<ref name="Arora1986">{{Cite book |last=Arora |first=David |author-link=David Arora |url=https://archive.org/details/arora-david-mushrooms-demystified-a-comprehensive-guide-to-the-fleshy-fungi-ten-speed-press-1986/page/837/mode/2up |title=Mushrooms Demystified: A Comprehensive Guide to the Fleshy Fungi |publisher=[[Ten Speed Press]] |year=1986 |isbn=978-0-89815-170-1 |edition=2nd |location=Berkeley, California |pages=837–38 |orig-date=1979}}</ref> '''spring orange peel fungus''', the '''golden cup''', or the '''dazzling cup'''. It is a [[cup fungus]], typically up to {{convert|4|cm|in|frac=8}} in diameter, with a bright to pale orange interior and orange; specimens that are old or bruised often have an olive-green discoloration, especially around the edges.

In North America, ''C. fulgens'' is usually found on the ground in [[forest litter]] near [[conifer]]s. Fruiting occurs in early spring following snow melt. The [[asexual reproduction|asexual]] ([[imperfect fungi|imperfect]]), or [[conidial]] stage of ''C.&nbsp;fulgens'' is the [[plant pathogen]]ic species ''Geniculodendron pyriforme'', known to infect dormant [[seed]]s of the [[Sitka spruce]].

==Taxonomy==
This species was first [[species description|described]] by [[Christian Hendrik Persoon]] in 1822 as ''Peziza fulgens'', and has been grouped in several different [[genera]] since its original description. [[Phylogenetic]] analysis of [[DNA sequence]] data shows that within the order [[Pezizales]], ''Caloscypha fulgens'' belongs in an evolutionary lineage with the families [[Helvellaceae]], [[Morchellaceae]], and [[Tuberaceae]].<ref name="Landvik1997" /> Since 1968, ''Caloscypha'' had been placed in the family [[Pyronemataceae]], a small grouping of fungi distinguished from other Pezizales by their relatively undeveloped [[peridium]].<ref name="Eckblaad1968" /> In 2002, the new family [[Caloscyphaceae]] was described to contain the [[monotypic]] genus ''Caloscypha''.<ref name="Harmaja2002" />

=== Etymology ===
The [[specific name (botany)|specific epithet]] means "bright colored", while the genus name ''Caloscypha'' means "beautiful cup".<ref name="Smith1980" /> The distinctive orange-yellow color of the fungus has earned it the [[common name]]s "spring orange peel fungus",<ref name="Tylukti1979" /> the "golden cup",<ref name="urlBMS" /> and the "dazzling cup".<ref name="McKnight1987" />

==Description==

The [[fruiting body]] of ''C.&nbsp;fulgens'' is roughly cup-shaped, although the cup may be somewhat flattened, lopsided or split; the size is up to {{convert|6|cm|frac=8|abbr=off}} in diameter.<ref>{{Cite book|last1=Davis|first1=R. Michael|title=Field Guide to Mushrooms of Western North America|last2=Sommer|first2=Robert|last3=Menge|first3=John A.|publisher=[[University of California Press]]|year=2012|isbn=978-0-520-95360-4|location=Berkeley|pages=405|oclc=797915861}}</ref> The inner surface of the cup is orange-yellow, while the external surface is pale yellow. Either the margin around the rim or the entire outer surface may be stained olive-green.<ref name="Tylukti1979" /> The green or bluish staining that occurs upon injury or with age is unique within the order [[Pezizales]].<ref name="Hansen2006" /> The [[stipe (mycology)|stem]], if present, is rather short. The [[spore print|spore deposit]] is white.<ref name="urlCalifornia Fungi: Caloscypha fulgens" />

A single specimen of an albino form, {{convert|2|cm|in|1|abbr=on|frac=8}} in diameter, was discovered in Northern [[Idaho]]; it was found to be lacking the [[pigment]] responsible for staining the outer surface olive-green.<ref name="Rogers1978" />

The [[spore]]s are translucent ([[hyaline]]), roughly spherical, thin-walled and smooth, with dimensions of 6–8&nbsp;[[μm]] in diameter.<ref name="Orr1979" /> The [[Ascus|asci]], the spore-bearing cells, are cylindrical and 80–100 by 7–8&nbsp;μm; the [[paraphyses]] are thin and filamentous and contain orange granules.<ref name="Tylukti1979" /> This fungus is considered nonpoisonous<ref name="Miller 2006">{{cite book|last1=Miller Jr.|first1=Orson K.|title=North American Mushrooms: A Field Guide to Edible and Inedible Fungi|last2=Miller|first2=Hope H.|publisher=[[FalconGuides]]|year=2006|isbn=978-0-7627-3109-1|location=Guilford, CN|pages=535|author-link=Orson K. Miller Jr.}}</ref> but inedible.<ref name="urlCalifornia Fungi: Caloscypha fulgens" /><ref name="Orr1979" /><ref>{{cite book |last=Phillips |first=Roger |title=Mushrooms and Other Fungi of North America |year=2010 |publisher=Firefly Books |location=Buffalo, NY |isbn=978-1-55407-651-2 |page=368}}</ref>

=== Similar species ===
''C. fulgens'' bears some resemblance to the orange-peel fungus (''[[Aleuria aurantia]]''); however, ''A.&nbsp;aurantia'' does not have the characteristic bluing reaction upon bruising or with age, and it fruits later in the season (usually in autumn).<ref name="urlCalifornia Fungi: Caloscypha fulgens" />

It also bears similarities to ''[[Otidea]]'' species (e.g. ''[[Otidea onotica|O.&nbsp;onotica]]'') which do not stain blue.<ref>{{Cite book |last=[[Audubon]] |title=Mushrooms of North America |publisher=[[Knopf]] |year=2023 |isbn=978-0-593-31998-7 |pages=26}}</ref>

==Distribution and habitat==

This species is usually found in the spring, often on [[forest floor|duff]] under conifers shortly after the snow melts. In North America, where it has been noted to occur only between March and July,<ref name=Paden1978/> it is widespread in the [[Rocky Mountains]] and the [[Pacific Northwest]].<ref name=Tylukti1979/> ''C.&nbsp;fulgens'' has been collected in Britain, and possibly arrived there from imported infected seeds.<ref name=Paden1978/> It has also been collected from Japan,<ref name=Nagao2002/> Sweden,<ref name=Nilsson1998/> The Netherlands,<ref name=Schmidt1997/> and [[Turkey]].<ref name=Dogan2006/>

== Imperfect state ==
The [[biological life cycle|life cycle]] of this fungus allows for both an [[imperfect fungi|imperfect]] (making asexual spores, or [[conidia]]) or perfect (making sexual spores) form; as has often happened in fungal [[Taxonomy (biology)|taxonomy]], the imperfect form was given a different name, because the relationship between the perfect and imperfect forms of the same species was not then known. The imperfect, or conidial stage of this fungus is the [[plant pathogen]] ''Geniculodendron pyrofirme'', first reported in 1964,<ref name="urlDiseases & Insects in BC Forest Seedling Nurseries - Seed fungus" /> and known to infect dormant seeds of the [[Sitka spruce]], ''Picea sitchensis''.<ref name="Paden1978" />

A 1978 study showed that about a third of Sitka spruce seed lots stored by the [[British Columbia]] Forest Service ([[Canada]]) contained diseased seeds, and these diseased seeds failed to germinate when sown in local nurseries.<ref name="Sutherland1978" /> The fungus can grow at low temperatures, contributing to its ability to kill seeds before they have a chance to germinate.<ref name="Epners1964" /><ref name="Salt1974" /><ref name="Thomson1983" /> Infected seeds tend to shrivel and dry up rather than rot.<ref name="Sullivan1983" />

Seed lots from squirrel seed caches have increased incidence of ''C.&nbsp;fulgens'' infection. Squirrels tend to cache [[pinecones]] repeatedly in the same location, and in cool, moist conditions favorable for fungus growth.<ref name="Sutherland1979" /> In 2002, ''G.&nbsp;pyriforme'' was found on imported conifer seeds in [[Germany]], the first such report in [[continental Europe]].<ref name="Schroder2002" />

== Conservation ==
''C. fulgens'' is listed on the [[Regional Red List|Red List]] of protected species in [[Slovakia]].<ref name="Kautmanova2008" />
