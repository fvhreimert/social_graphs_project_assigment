'''''Leccinum holopus''''', commonly known as the '''white birch bolete''', '''white bog bolete''', or '''ghost bolete''', is a species of [[bolete]] fungus in the family [[Boletaceae]] found in northern Asia, Europe, and northeastern North America. It associates with [[birch]] trees and is typically found in [[bog]]gy or swampy areas, often growing among [[sphagnum moss]].

[[Basidiocarp|Fruitbodies]] ([[mushroom]]s) of ''L.&nbsp;holopus'' have convex [[pileus (mycology)|caps]] measuring up to {{convert|10|cm|in|0|abbr=on}} in diameter. Often pure white—especially in young fruitbodies—the caps sometimes become flushed with [[buff (color)|buff]] or brownish tints. The whitish surface of the [[stipe (mycology)|stipe]] is covered with small, stiff, projecting scales (scabers) that become tan or darker in age. Some [[variety (botany)|varieties]] of ''Leccinum holopus'' have been described that vary in cap color or staining reaction, but [[DNA]] evidence suggests that most are the same [[taxon]]. Although the fruitbodies are [[edible mushroom|edible]], opinions vary as to their culinary desirability.

==Taxonomy==
Initially named as a species of ''[[Boletus]]'' by German mycologist [[Friedrich Wilhelm Gottlieb Rostkovius|Friedrich Rostkovius]] in 1844,<ref name="Rostkovius 1844"/> the fungus was later transferred to ''[[Leccinum]]'' by [[Roy Watling]] in 1960.<ref name="Watling 1960"/> [[Synonym (biology)|Synonyms]] resulting from transfer to different genera include: ''Krombholzia holopoda'' and ''K.&nbsp;holopus'' (both published by [[Albert Pilát]] in 1951);<ref name="Pilát 1951"/> ''Krombholziella holopus'' (Josef Šutara, 1989);<ref name="Šutara 1989"/> ''Trachypus holopus'' ([[Paul Konrad]] and [[André Maublanc]], 1952),<ref name="Konrad 1952"/> and ''Trachypus scaber'' f. ''holopus'' ([[Henri Romagnesi]], 1939).<ref name="urlMycoBank: Leccinum holopus"/> Other synonyms, according to [[Index Fungorum]],<ref name="urlFungorum synonymy: Leccinum holopus"/> include ''Leccinum olivaceosum'', described from France in 1994,<ref name="Lannoy 1994"/> and ''Leccinum aerugineum'' (1991).<ref name="Lannoy 1991"/> ''Leccinum holopus'' is [[classification (biology)|classified]] in [[section (botany)|section]] ''Scabra'' of genus ''Leccinum'', a grouping that includes [[Northern Hemisphere]] species associating exclusively with birch.<ref name="Bakker 2007"/>

The [[botanical name|specific epithet]] ''holopus'' is [[Ancient Greek|Greek]] for "with perfect stalk".<ref name="Schalkwijk-Barendsen 1991"/> [[Common name]]s given to the fungus include white birch bolete, white bog bolete,<ref name="Bessette 2000"/> and ghost bolete.<ref name="Phillips 2013"/>

Several subtaxa of ''Leccinum holopus'' have been described. In [[Form (botany)|form]] ''aerugineum'', described by Josef Šutara  in 2009, the flesh discolors green after injury.<ref name="Šutara 2009"/> The [[variety (botany)|variety]] ''americanum'', described by [[Alexander H. Smith]] and [[Harry Delbert Thiers]] in 1971 from collections made in [[Michigan]], injured flesh stains reddish.<ref name="Smith 1971"/> Lannoy & Estadès described ''Leccinum nucatum'' in 1993,<ref name="Lannoy 1993"/> a taxon that was later (2007) published as variety ''nucatum'' of ''L.&nbsp;holopus'';<ref name="Klofac 2007"/> no [[molecular phylogenetics|molecular]] evidence was found supporting the existence of this as a distinct taxon,<ref name="www.entoloma.nl"/> and it is therefore placed into synonymy with ''L.&nbsp;holopus''.<ref name="urlFungorum: L. holopus var. nucatum"/> ''Leccinum holopus'' var. ''majus'', described by [[Rolf Singer]] in 1966 (originally published by Singer as ''Krombholzia scabra'' f. ''majus''),<ref name="Singer 1966"/> is another historical variety without independent [[taxonomy (biology)|taxonomic]] significance.<ref name="urlFungorum: L. holopus var. majus"/>

==Description==

[[Basidiocarp|Fruitbodies]] of ''Leccinum holopus'' have convex to flattened [[pileus (mycology)|caps]] measuring {{convert|3|–|10|cm|in|abbr=on}} in diameter, with a narrow band of sterile tissue surrounding the margin. The caps are initially whitish, but can develop gray, [[buff (color)|buff]], tan, or pinkish tints during maturity; the color may also darken and become greenish with age.<ref name="Bessette 2000"/> The cap surface is initially covered with very fine hairs,<ref name="Jordan 2004"/> but later becomes more or less smooth, often with a sticky texture in age or in moist conditions. The [[trama (mycology)|flesh]] is white and lacks any distinct odor or taste; it can have either little or no bruising color reaction with injury,<ref name="Bessette 2000"/> or may become light pink in variety ''americanum''.<ref name="Miller 2006"/> On the cap underside is a porous surface comprising pores numbering 2 to 3 per millimeter, each of which is the end of a tube that extends to {{convert|2.5|cm|in|1|abbr=on}} deep. The color of the pore surface ranges from whitish to grayish to dingy brown, and has little color reaction to injury, although it may discolor yellowish or brownish.<ref name="Bessette 2000"/> There is a depression where the pores meet the [[stipe (mycology)|stipe]].<ref name="Kuo 2014"/> The stipe measures {{convert|8|–|14|cm|in|abbr=on}} long by {{convert|1|–|2|cm|in|1|abbr=on}} wide. Its whitish surface is covered with scabers that darken in age to tan or darker.<ref name="Bessette 2000"/> The stipe base often stains bluish.<ref name="Kuo 2014"/>

''Leccinum holopus'' produces a brown [[spore print]]. [[Basidiospore|Spores]] are somewhat fusoid (spindle-shaped) and measure 14–20 by 5–6.5&nbsp;[[micrometre|μm]].<ref name="Bessette 2000"/> The [[basidia]] (spore-bearing cells) are four-spored and measure 28.5–36.5 by 11.5–12.5&nbsp;μm. [[Cystidia]] on the pores are flask-shaped (lageniform) to fusiform, and 39.0–45.5 by 7.5–9.0&nbsp;μm, while those of the stipe (caulocystidia) are fusiform, club-shaped, or cylindrical, measuring 39.0–54.5 x 9.1–13.5&nbsp;μm. There are no [[clamp connection]]s present in the [[hypha]]e of ''L.&nbsp;holopus''.<ref name="www.entoloma.nl"/> The [[cap cuticle]] is arranged in the form of a cutis—with hyphae that run parallel to the cap surface.<ref name="Kuo 2014"/>

Several [[Chemical tests in mushroom identification|chemical tests]] can be used to help verify an identification of ''L.&nbsp;holopus''. A drop of [[ammonium hydroxide]] solution turns the cap cuticle a pinkish color, but has no reaction with the flesh. A drop of dilute [[potassium hydroxide]] (KOH) has no reaction on the cap surface, and either no reaction or a brownish reaction with the flesh. Application of [[iron(II) sulfate]] solution does not have a reaction on the cap surface, and either no reaction to slightly olive coloration on the flesh.<ref name="Kuo 2014"/>

===Similar species===
''[[Leccinellum albellum]]'' is similar in appearance to ''L.&nbsp;holopus'', but grows in association with [[oak]] and has a more southerly distribution.<ref name="Binion 2008"/> ''[[Leccinum scabrum|L.&nbsp;scabrum]]'' is a widely distributed lookalike that can be distinguished from ''L.&nbsp;holopus'' by its larger size and generally darker colors.<ref name="McKnight 1987"/>

==Edibility==
Although commonly considered [[edible mushroom|edible]], opinions vary on the culinary appeal of ''Leccinum holopus'' fruitbodies. Michael Kuo, writing in ''100 Edible Mushrooms'', considers it a good edible;<ref name="Kuo 2007"/> Peter Roberts and Shelley Evans in ''The Book of Fungi'' say "it is edible, but is said to be tasteless and pappy, so is not recommended."<ref name="Roberts 2011"/>  Fruitbodies are optimally harvested when they are young, before the flesh becomes too spongy, and before insect larvae establish themselves. Minimal cleaning is required in the field. The mushroom has a mild, somewhat sweet flavor that is enhanced after brief [[sautee]]ing. Drying the mushrooms enhances the flavor, but diminishes the sweetness of fresh mushrooms.<ref name="Kuo 2007"/>

==Habitat and distribution==

''Leccinum holopus'' is a [[mycorrhiza]]l species.<ref name="urlMushroomExpert"/> It fruits on the ground (often among ''[[Sphagnum]]'' moss), singly to scattered in wet areas like [[cedrus|cedar]] swamps, [[bogs]], or soggy forests. Like most ''Leccinum'' species,<ref name="Bakker 2004"/> the fungus is highly host-specific and associates with [[birch]] (''Betula''). In North America, the range of ''Leccinum holopus'' extends from eastern Canada to [[New York (state)|New York]], extending west to the northern [[Rocky Mountain]]s, roughly coinciding with the distribution of the [[paper birch]] (''Betula papyrifera''). In this range, it is common and fruits from August to October.<ref name="Bessette 2000"/> ''L.&nbsp;holopus'' var. ''americanum'' is known only from North America.<ref name="Roberts 2011"/> The fungus is rare in southern Europe, but more common in the ''Sphagnum'' swamps in the north.<ref name="Kibby 2006"/> In Asia, it has been recorded from Taiwan and [[Qinghai]] (China).<ref name="Fu 2006"/>

Fruitbodies of ''L.&nbsp;holopus'' are a source of food for [[diptera|fly]] species such as ''[[Pegomya winthemi]]'' (family [[Anthomyiidae]]) and ''[[Megaelia pygmaeoides]]'' (family [[Phoridae]]).<ref name="Wheeler 1984"/>
