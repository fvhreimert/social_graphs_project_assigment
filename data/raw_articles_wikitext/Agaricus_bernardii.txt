'''''Agaricus bernardii''''', commonly called the '''salt-loving agaricus''',<ref name="Arora 1986"/> or '''salty mushroom''', is an [[agaric]] fungus in the family [[Agaricaceae]]. The mushroom's thick [[stipe (mycology)|stem]] is usually shorter than the diameter of the [[Pileus (mycology)|cap]], which ranges from {{convert|5–15|cm|abbr=off|frac=2}} and is convex to flattened. The cap surface is whitish to [[buff (color)|buff]], and can develop scales or warts in age. The [[lamella (mycology)|gills]] are initially pink before turning brown when the [[spore]]s mature. The [[trama (mycology)|flesh]] turns reddish when it is cut or bruised. It resembles species such as ''[[Agaricus bitorquis|A.&nbsp;bitorquis]]''.

Found in Eurasia, North America, New Zealand, and Australia, ''A.&nbsp;bernardii'' is a [[salt-tolerant]] species that grows in salt marshes, dunes, and coastal grassland. An [[edible mushroom]], it is stronger in flavor but similar to the store-bought button mushrooms, ''[[Agaricus bisporus|A.&nbsp;bisporus]]''.

==Taxonomy==

=== Taxonomic history ===
The species was first [[species description|described]] by French mycologist [[Lucien Quélet]] as ''Psalliota bernardi'' in 1879, based on collections made in [[La Rochelle]], a seaport on the [[Bay of Biscay]] (France).<ref name="Quélet 1878" /> [[Pier Andrea Saccardo]] transferred it to ''[[Agaricus]]'' in 1887.<ref name="urlMycoBank: Agaricus bernardii" />

=== Modern classification ===
The infrageneric (below genus-level) [[Taxonomy (biology)|classification]] of ''A.&nbsp;bernardii'' is uncertain. In his 1978 proposed classification, [[Paul Heinemann]] placed it in the subsection ''Bitorques'' of the [[section (botany)|section]] ''Agaricus''.<ref name="Heinemann 1978" />  Although the species has some similarities with species in the section ''Duploannulatae'' based on the structure of its [[partial veil|veil]] and its tendencies towards rufescence (developing a red coloration), [[molecular phylogenetic|molecular]] analysis shows that it does not belong in this section.<ref name="Challen 2003" /><ref name="Kerrigan 2008" /> An earlier (1999) analysis suggested that it is closely related to the "Agaricus clade", which contains ''[[Agaricus subperonatus|A.&nbsp;subperonatus]]'', ''[[Agaricus devoniensis|A.&nbsp;devoniensis]]'', ''[[Agaricus bisporus|A.&nbsp;bisporus]]'', ''[[Agaricus spissicaulis|A.&nbsp;spissicaulis]]'', ''[[Agaricus bitorquis|A.&nbsp;bitorquis]]'', and ''[[Agaricus impudicus|A.&nbsp;impudicus]]''.<ref name="Mitchell 1999b" /> In 1986, [[Henri Romagnesi]] placed it in section ''Chitonioides'';<ref name="Romagnesi 1986" /> Solomon Wasser demoted this to a subsection of ''Duploannulatae'' in 1995,<ref name="Wasser 1995" /> and later provided molecular support for his decision. In addition to ''A.&nbsp;bernardii'', species in Wasser's concept of subsection ''Chitonioides'' include ''[[Agaricus rollanii|A.&nbsp;rollanii]]'', ''[[Agaricus bernardiiformis|A.&nbsp;bernardiiformis]]'', ''[[Agaricus gennadii|A.&nbsp;gennadii]]'', ''[[Agaricus pequinii|A.&nbsp;pequinii]]'', and ''[[Agaricus nevoi|A.&nbsp;nevoi]]''.<ref name="Didukh 2005" />

=== Etymology ===
The species was named after the original collector, G.&nbsp;Bernard.<ref name="Quélet 1878" /> ''A.&nbsp;bernardii'' is [[common name|commonly]] known as the salt-loving mushroom.<ref name="Roberts 2011" />

[[Synonym (taxonomy)|Synonyms]] include ''Psalliota bernardii'', ''Pratella bernardii'', ''Fungus bernardii'', and ''Agaricus campestris'' subsp. ''bernardii''.<ref name="urlMycoBank: Agaricus bernardii" />

==Description==


The [[Basidiocarp|fruit bodies]] of ''A. bernardii'' have [[pileus (mycology)|caps]] that range in shape from convex to flattened, and reach a diameter of {{convert|5–15|cm|abbr=off|frac=2}}. The cap surface is dry and smooth, with a white or buff color that can develop brownish spots in maturity. In age, the surface often forms scales or warts. The [[trama (mycology)|flesh]] is thick, firm, and stains reddish-orange or reddish-brown when cut, although this reaction can be slow to develop.<ref name="Mitchell 1999"/> Its odor ranges from mild to briny to pungent.<ref name="Arora 1986"/> The [[lamella (mycology)|gills]] are free from attachment to the stem, and packed close together. Initially grayish-pink to pinkish, they turn reddish-brown and then chocolate brown as the spores mature. The [[stipe (mycology)|stem]] is solid (i.e., not hollow), firm, and measures {{convert|4–10|cm|abbr=on|frac=2}} long by {{convert|2–4|cm|abbr=on|frac=2}} thick. A thick, white, rubbery [[partial veil]] covers the gills of the immature mushroom, and eventually remains as a [[annulus (mycology)|ring]] on the middle of the stem.<ref name="Mitchell 1999"/>

''A. bernardii'' mushrooms produce a dark brown [[spore print]]. The [[spore]]s are smooth, broadly elliptical, and have dimensions of 6–7.5 by 5–6&nbsp;[[μm]].<ref name="Arora 1986"/> The [[basidia]] (spore-bearing cells) are four-spored and club-shaped, measuring 14–25 by 4–7&nbsp;μm; the [[sterigmata]] are 4–5&nbsp;μm. Cheilocystidia ([[cystidia]] on the gill edge) are broadly club-shaped to cylindrical, [[hyaline]] (translucent), and measure 17–30 by 4–8&nbsp;μm.<ref name="Mitchell 1999"/>

===Similar species===
'', a lookalike species]]

''A. bitorquis'', also edible, has a similar appearance, but can be distinguished by its double ring, and the lack of a fishy or briny odor. Additionally, ''A.&nbsp;bitorquis'' does not stain reddish when cut, and usually does not have a scaly or warty cap.<ref name="Arora 1986"/> The Hungarian species ''[[Agaricus bernardiiformis|A.&nbsp;bernardiiformis]]'', named for its similarity to ''A.&nbsp;bernardii'', is distinguished from the latter by its smaller spores (6.2–8.2 by 5.4–6.2&nbsp;μm) and its club-shaped cheilocystidia that measure 17–35 by 7–9.5&nbsp;μm.<ref name="Didukh 2005"/> [[MycoBank]], however, considers the two species to be [[conspecific]].<ref name="urlMycoBank: Agaricus bernardiiformis"/>

==Distribution and habitat==
The species is found in Asia, Europe, North America<ref name="Roberts 2011" /> (including Mexico)<ref name="Perez-Silva 1986" /> and New Zealand.<ref name="Mitchell 1999" />

''A. bernardii'' is a [[saprobic]] species.<ref name="urlMushroomExpert" /> Its mushrooms fruit singly, scattered, or in groups on the ground. They grow in sandy soils, lawns, and in habitats with a high salt concentration, like along ocean coasts and salt marshes.<ref name="Arora 1986" /> Once primarily a maritime species, the fungus has spread inland to roadside verges where salt has been applied to [[de-ice]] the roads.<ref name="Roberts 2011" /><ref name="Scott 1985" /> Fruit bodies sometimes form underground.<ref name="Arora 1986" /> Mushrooms can also grow in [[fairy ring]]s, especially when in grasslands or pastures.<ref name="Mitchell 1999" />

== Ecology ==
A Czech study determined that the mushrooms will strongly [[bioaccumulation|bioaccumulate]] silver from contaminated soil. Although the average concentration of silver in the soil is typically less than 1 milligram per kilogram of soil, it can be significantly elevated near industrial sites such as mines and smelters. The concentration of silver in the caps—which reached levels of up to 544&nbsp;mg per kg of mushroom tissue ([[dry matter|dry weight]])—was about twice that of the stems.<ref name="Borovička 2010" />

==Uses==
Although the mushrooms sometimes have an odor that is briny or pungent,<ref name="Roberts 2011" /> they are [[edible mushroom|edible]] and good.<ref name="Davis 2012" /> [[David Arora]] compares it to the closely related ''A.&nbsp;bitorquis'', "but a little chewier".<ref name="Arora 1986" />
