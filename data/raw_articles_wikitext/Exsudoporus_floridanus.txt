'''''Exsudoporus floridanus''''' is a species of edible [[bolete]] mushroom in the family [[Boletaceae]]. In 1945, American mycologist [[Rolf Singer]] described a species he found in [[Florida]] during his 1942–3 tenure of a [[List of Guggenheim Fellowships awarded in 1942|Guggenheim Memorial Fellowship]].<ref name="Singer 1945"/> He originally described it as a [[subspecies]] of the eastern North American species ''[[Boletus frostii]]'', but later considered it worthy of distinct species status in a 1947 publication.<ref name="Singer 1947"/> Based on morphological and phylogenetic data, Vizzini and colleagues transferred this species to a newly described genus ''[[Exsudoporus]]'' in 2014.<ref name="Vizzini 2014"/>
Due to lack of sufficient sequences, Wu et al. (2016) were reluctant to accept ''Exsudoporus'' and considered it a synonym of ''[[Butyriboletus]]'', so they proposed a new combination ''Butyriboletus floridanus''.<ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Wu|first1=Gang|last2=Li|first2=Yan-Chun|last3=Zhu|first3=Xue-Tai|last4=Zhao|first4=Kuan|last5=Han|first5=Li-Hong|last6=Cui|first6=Yang-Yang|last7=Li|first7=Fang|last8=Xu|first8=Jian-Ping|last9=Yang|first9=Zhu L.|date=November 2016|title=One hundred noteworthy boletes from China|url=http://link.springer.com/10.1007/s13225-016-0375-8|journal=Fungal Diversity|language=en|volume=81|issue=1|pages=25–188|doi=10.1007/s13225-016-0375-8|s2cid=22506275|issn=1560-2745|url-access=subscription}}</ref> However, following phylogenetic and morphological analyses clearly resolved ''Exsudoporus'' as a monophyletic, homogenous and independent genus that is sister to ''Butyriboletus''. <ref name="Loizides 2019"/><ref name=Alona2022/>

''Exsudoporus floridanus'' differs from ''Exsudoporus frostii'' in having a lighter cap color<ref>Specifically, "Corinthian red" in the subspecies compared with "jasper red", "nopal red" or "carmine" in the typical form, using [[Robert Ridgway|Ridgway]] color standards.</ref> and in the texture of the cap surface: the subspecies is [[tomentose]] (covered with dense, short, soft, matted hairs) or [[wikt:velutinous|velutinous]] (like velvet), compared to the relatively smooth surface of ''E.&nbsp;frostii''. Singer notes that although the physical characteristics between the two taxa may be blurred and are hard to define, the area of origin is a reliable indicator of subspecies status. ''E.&nbsp;floridanus'' is found on shaded lawns and [[scrubland]] in open oak stands in non-[[tropical]] regions of Florida, typically on grassy or sandy soil. It grows under or near several oak species, including [[Chapman oak]] (''Quercus chapmanii''), [[Quercus laurifolia|swamp laurel oak]] (''Q.&nbsp;laurifolia''), and [[southern live oak]] (''Q.&nbsp;virginiana''), and it fruits between May and October.<ref name="Singer 1947"/>

== Edibility ==
''Exsudoporus floridanus'' is said to be edible but bitter, tasting slightly acidic, akin to [[Lemon|lemons]]. It is known to cause [[gastric upset]] in some who consume it.<ref>{{Cite web |date=2015-07-24 |title=Exsudoporus floridanus |url=https://boletes.wpamushroomclub.org/product/boletus-floridanus/ |access-date=2022-10-25 |website=The Bolete Filter |language=en-US}}</ref>
