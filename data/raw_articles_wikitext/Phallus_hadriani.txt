'''''Phallus hadriani''''', commonly known as the '''dune stinkhorn''' or the '''sand stinkhorn''',<ref name=NGSWG>{{NGSWG}}</ref> is a species of [[fungus]] in the [[Phallaceae]] (stinkhorn) family. The [[stipe (mycology)|stalk]] of the [[basidiocarp|fruit body]] reaches up to {{convert|20|cm|abbr=on|frac=2}} tall by {{convert|4|cm|in|abbr=on|frac=4}} thick, and is spongy, fragile, and hollow. At the top of the stem is a ridged and pitted, thimble-like cap over which is spread olive-colored [[spore]] slime ([[gleba]]). Shortly after emerging, the gleba liquefies and releases a fetid odor that attracts insects, which help disperse the spores. ''P.&nbsp;hadriani'' may be distinguished from the similar ''[[Phallus impudicus|P.&nbsp;impudicus]]'' (the common stinkhorn) by the presence of a pink or violet-colored [[volva (mycology)|volva]] at the base of the stem, and by differences in odor.

It is a widely distributed species, and is native to Eurasia and North America. In Australia, it is probably an [[introduced species]]. It typically grows in public lawns, yards and gardens, usually in sandy soils. It is said to be [[edible mushroom|edible]] in its immature egg-like stage.

==Taxonomy==
The species was first [[species description|described scientifically]] by the French botanist [[Étienne Pierre Ventenat]] in 1798,<ref name=Ventenat1798/> and [[sanctioned name|sanctioned]] by [[Christiaan Hendrik Persoon]] under that name in his 1801 ''Synopsis Methodica Fungorum''.<ref name=Persoon1801/> [[Christian Gottfried Daniel Nees von Esenbeck]] called the species ''Hymenophallus hadriani'' in 1817;<ref name=Nees1817/> this name is a [[synonym (biology)|synonym]].<ref name="urlMycoBank: Phallus hadriani"/> According to the taxonomical database [[Index Fungorum]], additional synonyms include: ''Phallus iosmus'', named by [[Miles Joseph Berkeley|Berkeley]] in 1836; ''Phallus imperialis'', [[Stephan V.M. Schulzer von Müggenburg|Schulzer]], 1873; ''Ithyphallus impudicus'' var. ''imperialis'' and ''Ithyphallus impudicus'' var. ''iosmos'', [[Giovanni Batista De Toni|De Toni]], date unknown.<ref name="urlFungorum synonymy: Phallus hadriani"/>

The [[specific name (botany)|specific epithet]] ''hadriani'' is named after the Dutch botanist [[Hadrianus Junius]] (1512–1575),<ref name="urlPhalli, Home Page"/> who wrote a pamphlet on stinkhorn mushrooms in 1564 (''Phalli, ex fungorum genere, in Hollandiae sabuletis passim crescentis descriptio'').<ref name=Schaechter1998/>

==Description==



The immature fruiting bodies of ''P.&nbsp;hadriani'' in the egg stage have dimensions of {{convert|4|to|6|cm|abbr=on|frac=4}} by {{convert|3|to|4|cm|abbr=on|frac=4}}, and are colored rosy-pink to violet.<ref name=Ellis1990/> They are typically submerged in the ground<ref name=":audu">{{Cite book |last=[[Audubon]] |title=Mushrooms of North America |publisher=[[Knopf]] |year=2023 |isbn=978-0-593-31998-7 |pages=121}}</ref> with [[rhizomorphs]] (aggregations of [[mycelium]] resembling plant roots) at the base.<ref name=Bessette2007/> The eggs are enclosed in a tough covering and a gelatinous layer that breaks down as the stinkhorn emerges. Mature fruiting bodies may be {{convert|10|to|20|cm|abbr=on|frac=2}} tall by {{convert|3|to|4|cm|abbr=on|frac=4}} thick. The white- or cream-colored [[stipe (mycology)|stipe]] is up to 3&nbsp;cm wide,<ref name=":audu" /> hollow, spongy and honeycombed. The head is [[wikt:reticulate|reticulate]], ridged and pitted, and covered with olive green [[gleba]]l mass. The [[volva (mycology)|volva]] is cuplike, and typically retains its pink color although it may turn brownish with age. Fruit bodies are short-lived, typically lasting only one or two days.<ref name="urlCalifornia Fungi: Phallus hadriani" />

Although the odor of ''P.&nbsp;hadriani'' has been described by some authors as faint and pleasant<ref name=Demirel2004/> or like [[Viola odorata|violets]],<ref name=Shaw2002/> others describe the smell as [[wikt:fetid|fetid]] or putrid.<ref name=Smith2005/> The gleba is known to attract insects, including flies, bees, and beetles, some of which consume the spore-containing slime. It is thought that long-distance spore dispersal is facilitated by these insects, who may deposit in their feces intact spores that survive the passage through the digestive tract.<ref name=Shaw2002/>

The [[spore]]s are cylindrical, smooth, and [[hyaline]] (translucent), with dimensions of 3–4 by 1–2&nbsp;[[μm]].<ref name=Healy2008/> The [[basidia]] (spore-bearing cells) are cylindrical, with dimensions of 20–25 by 3–4&nbsp;μm. They have eight [[sterigmata]] (slender extensions that attach to the spores), as well as a clamp at their base.<ref name=Demirel2004/>

===Similar species===
''[[Phallus impudicus]]'' has the same overall appearance as ''P.&nbsp;hadriani'', but is distinguished by its white volva.<ref name="urlPhallus impudicus and P. hadriani: The Common Stinkhorn (MushroomExpert.Com)"/> Another similar stinkhorn, ''[[Phallus ravenelii|P.&nbsp;ravenelii]]'' has a smooth, not reticulate head.<ref name=Bessette2007/>

==Habitat and distribution==
''Phallus hadriani'' is known to be in [[Australia]] (where it is thought to be an [[introduced species]] imported on [[Woodchipping|woodchip]] mulch used in gardening and landscaping),<ref name=Smith2005/> [[North America]],<ref name="urlCalifornia Fungi: Phallus hadriani" /> [[Europe]] (including Denmark,<ref name=Lange1957/> Ireland,<ref name=Hassell1952/> Latvia,<ref name=Vimba1997/> The Netherlands,<ref name=Brouwer2009/> Norway,<ref name=Hoiland1975/> Poland,<ref name=Zabawski1976/> Slovakia,<ref name=Lizon1989/> Sweden,<ref name=Gothnier2004/> Ukraine,<ref 
Scotland,<ref
name=Koretskii1979/> and Wales<ref name=Rhind1999/>) Turkey ([[Iğdır Province]]),<ref name=Demirel2004/> [[Japan]],<ref name=Kasuya2007/> and China ([[Jilin Province]]).<ref name=Wang2004/>

''Phallus hadriani'' is a [[saprobic]] species, and thus obtains nutrients by decomposing organic matter. In North America, it is commonly associated with tree stumps, or roots of stumps that are decomposing in the ground.<ref name=Healy2008/> In [[Great Britain]], its distribution is more or less restricted to coastal [[dune]]s,<ref name=Pegler1995/> while in Poland, it has been noted to avoid humid and [[humic acid|humic]] forest soils, and live in symbiosis with [[xerophilous]] grasses and the black locust tree, ''[[Robinia pseudoacacia]]''.<ref name=Wojewoda1965/> The mushroom is one of three species [[protected species|protected]] by the [[Regional Red List|Red Data Book]] of Latvia.<ref name=Vimba1997/>

== Edibility ==

Like many other stinkhorns, this species is thought to be [[edible mushroom|edible]] when in its egg form.<ref name="urlCalifornia Fungi: Phallus hadriani" /> Central Europeans and the Chinese consider the eggs a delicacy.<ref name="Arora1991" /> Regarding the edibility of mature specimens, one author commented on the genus in general, "No one with his sense of smell developed would think of eating the members of this group."<ref name="Marshall1928" />
